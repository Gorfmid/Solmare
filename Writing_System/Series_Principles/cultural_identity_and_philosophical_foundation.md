## 1. Cultural Recursion

Every civilization should possess **recurring cultural touchstones**.

These should appear naturally throughout the series.

Examples include:

- proverbs
- sayings
- military academy phrases
- traditions
- holidays
- foods
- music
- stories
- children's lessons
- family rituals
- military customs
- famous historical quotations
- commemorative ceremonies

**Do not explain them when first introduced.**

Allow readers to learn their meaning through **repetition and context**.

**Goal:** Readers recognize a civilization by its culture **before anyone identifies it by name**.

Faction lists: `Universe/cultural_touchstones.md`

## 2. Dominion Proverbs

The Dominion has a rich **oral and military tradition**.

Certain sayings should recur naturally throughout the series.

Different people may repeat the **same proverb** in different contexts — grandmother, military instructor, planner, politician, veteran, shipyard worker, border officer.

### Established canon

> *"Preparation honors those who come after us."*

> *"Conquest is what other people call it when sacrifice succeeds."*

The meaning of Dominion proverbs should **never be explicitly explained**.

Readers should gradually understand that Dominion citizens sincerely believe **sacrifice preserves future generations**.

Additional Dominion sayings should be **developed gradually** over the course of the series — not introduced in a batch.

Use sparingly. Earned in dialogue. One proverb per scene at most.

## 3. Cultural Memory

Fleet Authority and the Dominion experience history **differently**.

### Fleet Authority is young

Fleet characters naturally reference:

- exploration
- recent discoveries
- new colonies
- current missions
- living pioneers

Fleet citizens often think **prospectively**.

### The Dominion is old

Dominion characters naturally reference:

- historical campaigns
- ancestors
- traditions
- centuries-old institutions
- previous generations
- old infrastructure
- famous commanders
- events everyone already knows

Dominion citizens often think **historically**.

Dominion characters **frequently quote** teachers, parents, grandparents, academy instructors, old commanders, and historical sayings. Fleet characters **almost never** do. Dominion values **continuity**; Fleet values **innovation**. Show the difference; never explain it. See `Universe/The_Dominion/dominion_speech.md`.

### Writing rule

Neither approach is superior. They reflect two very different civilizations.

Readers should **feel** this difference without it ever being explained.

Do not have characters comment on the contrast. Show it through what they notice, quote, worry about, and take for granted.

## 4. The True Antagonist

**The Dominion is not the antagonist.**

**Fleet Authority is not the antagonist.**

The true antagonist of Book One — and ultimately the series — is **history itself**.

Two civilizations whose histories **diverged** — not two species that met by accident at a treaty line.

Fleet Authority began as a **breakaway founding** from Dominion civilization; its public memory scrubbed or reframed that inheritance. The Dominion retained it. Over centuries, incompatible security doctrines and **outward Fleet expansion** meeting **maintained Dominion border geometry** placed them on a collision course neither fully understands from the other's archive.

See `Universe/Fleet/fleet_dominion_frontier_history.md`.

**That collision — not malice — is the source of the conflict.**

---

## 5. Series Philosophy

The Solmare Cycle is **not** a story about good versus evil.

It is a story about **good people serving civilizations whose histories have placed them on an unavoidable collision course**.

Every major decision should be understandable from the perspective of the character making it.

Readers should frequently think:

> *I understand exactly why they made that decision.*

…even when that decision moves the civilizations closer to war.

**Success metric:** If readers can empathize with **both** civilizations simultaneously, the series is succeeding.

Reader allegiance may still favor Fleet Authority operationally. Empathy is not endorsement.

## 6. Civilization Scene Test

Before writing any Fleet or Dominion scene, ask:

> **What piece of this civilization will the reader understand better after this scene?**

The answer should **never** be:

- Technology

It should be something like:

- family
- tradition
- leadership
- logistics
- education
- history
- transportation
- military culture
- work
- food
- holidays
- sacrifice
- humor
- bureaucracy
- friendship
- duty

**Every chapter** should leave the reader understanding **one new aspect of one civilization**.

*(Complements the character-focused scene test in `solmare_craft_doctrine.md`.)*

---

## Do Not

- Explain touchstones or proverbs on first introduction
- Frame either civilization as cartoon evil or purely righteous
- State the history-as-antagonist thesis in character dialogue or narration
- Use technology exposition as a substitute for civilization texture
- Resolve strategic incompatibility as a misunderstanding that a single conversation could fix

---

## THE SOLMARE PRINCIPLE

History creates civilizations.

Civilizations create doctrine.

Doctrine creates decisions.

Decisions create conflict.

The people living through those decisions are the story.

Never write a battle before understanding the history that made it inevitable.
