# Revision Pass 6 — AI Signature Removal

**Router:** `Writing_System/revision_pass.md`  
**Lifecycle:** Stage 2 — runs **after** Character Pass (Pass 5), before Final Candidate / Writing Pass 1  
**Trigger phrases:**  
- *AI Signature Removal Pass on Chapter X*  
- *AI Signature Pass on Chapter X*  
- *Strip AI polish from Chapter X*

**Load only this file during Pass 6.** Structure and character should already be stable.

---

## Purpose

Strip subtle “AI polish” that over-explains, over-articulates, and over-certifies.

The chapter’s story and voices should already be right. This pass makes the prose feel more human and confident by **removing** what the narrator and dialogue should not say.

**Expected effect:** often −5–10% words; scenes feel stronger because images and silence do the work.

**Do not:**
- Add new scenes
- Expand emotional moments
- Make the prose more dramatic
- Reopen plot / structure (that was Passes 1–2)
- Rebuild character sheets (that was Pass 5)

**Do:**
- Cut meaning sentences after strong images
- Shorten over-articulate dialogue
- Replace narrative certainty with human hedge where earned

### 6.1 Ban “meaning” sentences after a strong image

If the image already did the work, **cut the sentence that explains what it means**.

| Weak | Prefer |
|------|--------|
| The schoolhouse sat empty. Children's drawings still hung on the walls. Nobody could mistake what had happened. | The schoolhouse sat empty. Children's drawings still hung on the walls. |
| A high chair. Food still in the tray. The absence was unbearable. | A high chair. Food still in the tray. |
| Dark hulls took the settlement apart. This was horror beyond words. | Dark hulls took the settlement apart. |

**Pattern to kill:** strong concrete image → narrator or dialogue that tells the reader how to feel / what it means / that nobody could miss it.

**Test:** Delete the last sentence. If the scene is clearer or stronger, leave it deleted.

**Related:** Pass 3 §3.1 (clever captions). Pass 6 is the dedicated strip after character voice is locked.

**Search (heuristic):** `nobody could|could not mistake|what had happened|this was|that meant|the horror|the weight of|beyond (words|comprehension)|felt (real|wrong|like)`

---

### 6.2 Ban over-articulate military / professional speech

Real military and technical professionals are **concise**. They do not deliver thesis dialogue under pressure.

| Weak | Prefer |
|------|--------|
| I think what we're witnessing is a fundamental change in how we understand first contact. | That's… different. / Well. That's new. |
| What we are observing represents an industrial process rather than a conventional engagement. | They're harvesting it. / This isn't a raid. |
| I find myself unable to reconcile this with my prior training. | Cut — or one short line: My training was for raids. |

**Who benefits most from saying less:** Park, Reyes, Calder, Walsh, Brenner — and any bridge / ops voice that sounds like a seminar.

**Keep** longer speech when the character sheet earns it (Ellis arguing history; Rowan in Council; Vex with a short doctrine line that is *his*). Even then, prefer one concrete claim over a paragraph of meaning.

**Test:** Would this person say this on a live bridge, or only in an essay? If essay — cut or compress to the work sentence.

**Search (heuristic):** `fundamental|what we're witnessing|represents|in how we|I find myself|unable to reconcile|it is important to|we must understand`

---

### 6.3 Ban excess narrative certainty

AI likes certainty. Humans hedge, hope they are wrong, and revise.

| Weak | Prefer |
|------|--------|
| Calder knew exactly what this meant. | Calder had a theory. He hoped it was wrong. |
| She understood immediately. | She thought she understood. |
| There was no other explanation. | He could not see another explanation yet. |
| He was certain. | He was almost certain. / He wanted to be wrong. |

**Keep** hard certainty when the character has **earned** it (Brenner after a long look: *They're harvesting it.*; Park after checking: *This isn't a battle.*). Do not fake uncertainty that contradicts competence.

**Test:** Is the certainty the POV’s lived conclusion after evidence — or the narrator comforting the reader? If the latter, hedge or cut.

**Search (heuristic):** `knew exactly|understood immediately|there was no (other\|doubt)|was certain|could not be mistaken|clearly meant|obviously`

---

### 6.4 Lived body, not furniture — simplify and expand

Distilled from Book Two Ch.6 Rowan annex open (2026-07-24). Cursor rule: `.cursor/rules/lived-body-not-furniture.mdc`.

When a fatigue / cost / domestic beat is cute or thin:

1. **Prefer the person.** Do not personify chairs, cushions, rooms, or slate stacks as the ones who “remember” or “forgive.”
2. **Simplify and expand.** Cut the metaphor wink. Expand one clear physical fact + one clear situational fact.
3. **Do not recycle Book One domestic check-ins.** Especially *Are you eating* / food-nag spouse messages as the default exhausted-officer beat. If family contact is needed, make it specific to this chapter — or show the unread queue without quoting a spent line.

| Weak | Prefer |
|------|--------|
| The chair had not forgiven him. The cushion remembered wrong angles. | His body had not forgiven him for it. His neck was stiff. |
| His wife's last message — *Are you eating* — and he had read it twice | Her message sat unread past the first line. He had meant to answer between meetings. The meetings had not stopped. |
| The slate stack remembered every argument | The slate stack was taller than when he had last looked — unfinished briefs, unanswered notes |

**Test:** Is the object feeling, or is the person? Keep the person. Can you expand one concrete detail instead of a clever caption? Do that.

**Search (heuristic):** `had not forgiven|remembered wrong|Are you eating|cushion remembered|chair had not`

---

## How to run

1. Confirm Passes 1–5 are done (or author authorizes Pass 6 alone on a Final Candidate).  
2. Read once for **images that already landed** — cut the meaning sentence after.  
3. Read dialogue for **seminar voice** — shorten to work speech.  
4. Read POV certainty — hedge only where it makes the human sharper, not vaguer.  
5. Check fatigue / domestic beats for **furniture poetry** and **recycled Book One check-ins** (§6.4).  
6. Do not add drama to fill the holes. Silence and objects stay.  
7. Exit when the chapter feels present, not explained.
