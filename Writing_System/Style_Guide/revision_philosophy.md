# REVISION PHILOSOPHY

These rules apply to every revision pass on **The Kestrel Veil Incident**—human or AI-assisted.

**Authority:** Load alongside `global_writing_principles.md`. When refinement mode defaults conflict with this document, **revision philosophy wins** for edit decisions (cut vs. keep, tighten vs. expand).

---

## Core Principle

Every revision should improve the manuscript through **subtraction as often as addition**.

Do not assume that making a scene longer makes it better.

The objective of every revision is not to increase word count—it is to improve the quality, readability, emotional resonance, and momentum of the story.

**Do not revise simply because you can.** Revise only when the new version is **demonstrably stronger** than the existing version. Preserve excellent prose, memorable dialogue, and emotionally effective scenes. The objective is **continuous improvement, not continuous change**.

---

## Revision Questions

When revising, continually ask:

* Does this paragraph strengthen the characters?
* Does this scene deepen the world?
* Does this dialogue reveal personality or relationships?
* Does this information advance the story?
* Does this increase emotional impact?
* Does this make the reader want to keep reading?
* Does this sound profound for its own sake rather than from the POV character? (see *Avoid Artificially Profound Prose* in `global_writing_principles.md`)

If the answer is **no**, consider **removing, simplifying, or combining** the material rather than expanding it.

---

## Exposition Discipline

Avoid adding exposition simply because information exists.

Readers rarely need to know everything.

Instead, reveal only the information necessary for the reader to understand the current scene while maintaining curiosity about the larger world.

---

## Favor

* clarity over complexity
* emotional impact over explanation
* memorable moments over exhaustive detail
* strong pacing over completeness
* implication over exposition

---

## Standard

Every paragraph should justify its existence.

Every scene should earn its place in the novel.

Whenever possible, communicate more with fewer words.

A well-crafted sentence is often more powerful than an additional page of explanation.

**When in doubt, leave it.** If existing prose, dialogue, or a scene already works, do not alter it for variety, completeness, or the appearance of effort.
