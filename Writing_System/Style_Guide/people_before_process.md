# PEOPLE BEFORE PROCESS

**Scope:** The Solmare Cycle — every book, every chapter, every revision  
**Authority:** Series-level philosophy. Takes precedence over technical exposition defaults when they conflict.  
**Related:** `revision_pass.md` · `Revision_Pass/01_people.md` · `Revision_Pass/05_character.md` (Character Pass — after structure) · `global_writing_principles.md`

---

## The Principle

The Solmare Cycle is fundamentally about governments, fleets, diplomacy, doctrine, logistics, and civilization.

Those things must always be experienced through people.

Readers remember **Calder** — not sensor doctrine.  
Readers remember **Park** — not lexicon debates.  
Readers remember **Vex** — not Dominion terminology.

Processes, institutions, and systems exist to create memorable moments for characters. Not the other way around.

---

## The Reader Memory Test

**After writing or revising every scene, ask:**

> Six months after finishing this book, what will the reader remember?

If the answer is a process, a doctrine, an institutional debate, a technical explanation, or a political argument — the scene is missing its emotional anchor.

The answer should instead be something like:

- Calder standing in an empty school.
- Park quietly realizing the relay wasn't destroyed — it was removed.
- Vex recognizing a familiar pattern before anyone else does.
- Thessaly keeping frightened professionals functioning.
- Brenner advocating for someone else immediately after his own promotion.

**Readers remember people. The processes exist to reveal who those people are.**

---

## Demonstrate Competence — Never Announce It

Do not tell the reader a character is intelligent, disciplined, experienced, compassionate, dangerous, or capable.

Demonstrate those qualities through:

- observation
- decisions
- patience
- mistakes
- preparation
- restraint
- behavior under pressure

Competence should be experienced. Not described.

See also: `Revision_Pass/competence_before_attraction.md`

---

## Show the Work

Whenever possible, let readers live inside a character's thought process.

Do not skip directly to conclusions.

Instead, allow readers to experience:

- what the character notices
- what they deliberately ignore
- what they hesitate over
- what they verify
- what they doubt
- why they finally act

The journey to a conclusion is usually more memorable than the conclusion itself.

---

## Sensory Anchoring

Whenever emotional moments occur, anchor them in physical reality.

Use:

- objects
- environments
- sounds
- textures
- movement
- silence
- routine
- small observations

Avoid replacing sensory experience with exposition.

> An empty school is more powerful than a paragraph explaining the loss of children.  
> A cold cup of coffee can reveal exhaustion better than saying someone is tired.  
> A remaining fence post can communicate loss more effectively than a speech.

---

## Emotion Through Behavior

Avoid speeches explaining emotion.

Instead, show emotion through:

- hesitation
- routine
- body language
- interrupted habits
- unfinished sentences
- restraint
- small acts of kindness
- professional discipline under stress

Professionals rarely announce how they feel.

Readers should infer emotion from behavior.

---

## The Revision Test

During every revision pass, identify sections where explanation dominates.

Ask:

> Can this information instead be revealed through a character experiencing it?

If yes:

- Replace explanation with experience.
- Replace process with observation.
- Replace exposition with behavior.

The goal is not to reduce worldbuilding. The goal is to make every piece of worldbuilding **belong to someone in the scene**.

---

## Guiding Principle

> Readers remember moments. Moments happen to people. Institutions, technology, doctrine, and politics exist to create those moments — not replace them.

Every chapter should leave the reader remembering a person, not a process.

---

## Origin

Distilled from *The Kestrel Veil Incident* (Book One) — specifically from the passages that worked and the passages that required structural revision. The lesson: when readers stopped caring, it was because a process had replaced the person experiencing it.

This principle is not a checklist item. It is the reason the checklist exists.
