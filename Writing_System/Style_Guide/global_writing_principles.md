# GLOBAL WRITING PRINCIPLES

These rules apply to every chapter, scene, and revision in **The Solmare Cycle**.

**Authority:** Load and obey this document for all generation, refinement, and revision across every book in the series. It takes precedence over technical exposition defaults in other engine docs when they conflict.

**Series philosophy (load first):** `people_before_process.md` — the foundational reason these principles exist. Reader Memory Test and Revision Test live there.

**Craft doctrine:** Also load `solmare_craft_doctrine.md` — POV information flow, professional competence, scene test, Solmare Rule (people first).

**Fleet operational language:** Also load `fleet_systems_lexicon.md` for shipboard systems, Fleet Ops, comms/relay sequences, and terminology normalization passes.

**Revision passes:** Also load `revision_philosophy.md`. Subtraction is as valid as addition; do not expand by default. **Revise only when demonstrably stronger**—not because you can.

---

## Priority Order

When deciding what belongs in a scene, always prioritize:

1. Character Development
2. World Development
3. Story Progression
4. Technical Details

Technical information should only exist when it naturally supports one of the first three priorities.

---

## Writing Style and Reading Level

Write for an educated adult audience with an approximate collegiate reading level.

The prose should be intelligent, polished, and sophisticated without becoming academic or difficult to read.

Favor:

* clear, precise language
* rich but purposeful descriptions
* varied sentence structure
* natural dialogue
* strong vocabulary used appropriately
* emotional clarity

Avoid:

* overly simplistic writing
* repetitive phrasing
* unnecessary jargon
* excessively technical explanations
* overly ornate or "purple" prose
* academic or textbook-style writing

Complex ideas should be presented in a way that feels accessible and engaging.

The reader should occasionally pause to appreciate a beautiful sentence, but never have to reread a paragraph simply to understand what happened.

The goal is prose that is immersive, elegant, and highly readable while respecting the intelligence of the audience.

---

## Avoid Artificially Profound Prose

Do not attempt to make the writing sound literary by inserting poetic metaphors, philosophical observations, or clever turns of phrase that do not arise naturally from the viewpoint character.

Avoid sentences that feel written to impress the reader.

Examples of writing to avoid include:

* abstract metaphors explaining a character's profession
* philosophical observations that interrupt the narrative
* narration that sounds wiser than the viewpoint character
* sentences whose primary purpose is to sound profound

Instead, allow meaning to emerge naturally through:

* character actions
* dialogue
* observations
* consequences
* relationships

Trust readers to recognize significance without having it explained.

A single concrete observation is usually more powerful than an abstract metaphor.

When in doubt, choose authenticity over eloquence.

The best prose often feels effortless rather than self-conscious.

---

Every scene should deepen the reader's understanding of at least one character.

Characters should feel like real people with:

* strengths
* flaws
* motivations
* fears
* humor
* relationships
* personal goals

Readers should remember characters because of who they are, not because of their job titles or technical expertise.

Create memorable characters that readers become emotionally invested in.

---

## Emotional Layering

Every chapter should contain a balance of emotional experiences.

Avoid maintaining a single emotional tone throughout an entire chapter.

Instead, allow scenes to naturally transition between moments of:

* humor
* curiosity
* warmth
* tension
* uncertainty
* triumph
* disappointment
* hope
* grief
* wonder

The emotional highs should make the lows more impactful.

The quiet moments should make the action feel larger.

Readers become emotionally invested not because every scene is dramatic, but because the characters experience a full range of human emotions.

The goal is for readers to laugh with the characters, celebrate with them, worry about them, and grieve alongside them.

A memorable novel is remembered as much for how it made readers feel as for what happened.

---

## World Before Lore

Build the Solmare universe through experience rather than exposition.

Instead of explaining how the world works, allow readers to discover it naturally through:

* conversations
* traditions
* celebrations
* politics
* military life
* commerce
* exploration
* religion
* history
* ordinary daily life

The world should feel lived in rather than explained.

---

## Cultural Touchstones

Every civilization should possess **recurring cultural touchstones** — proverbs, sayings, academy phrases, traditions, holidays, foods, music, stories, children's lessons, family rituals, military customs, historical quotations, commemorative ceremonies.

Recur naturally throughout the series. **Do not explain them when first introduced.** Readers learn through repetition and context.

**Goal:** Readers recognize a civilization by its culture before anyone identifies it by name.

**Dominion visual language:** Readers should recognize Dominion scenes **before the chapter heading**. `Universe/The_Dominion/dominion_visual_language.md`

**Dominion speech:** Dominion characters frequently quote teachers, parents, grandparents, instructors, old commanders, and historical sayings. Fleet almost never does. Continuity vs. innovation — show, never explain. `Universe/The_Dominion/dominion_speech.md`

**Canon:** `Universe/cultural_touchstones.md` · `Writing_System/Series_Principles/cultural_identity_and_philosophical_foundation.md`

### Cultural memory

Fleet characters think **prospectively** (exploration, new colonies, living pioneers). Dominion characters think **historically** (ancestors, campaigns, old institutions). Show the difference; never explain it.

### Series philosophy

Neither Fleet Authority nor the Dominion is the antagonist. **History** — independent evolution, incompatible doctrines, geographic collision — is the source of conflict. Good people on both sides; empathize with both simultaneously.

### Civilization scene test

Before Fleet or Dominion scenes: *What piece of this civilization will the reader understand better?* Never technology alone. Every chapter: one new aspect of one civilization.

---

## The Universe Is a Character

The Solmare universe is more than a backdrop—it is one of the central characters of the series.

Readers should feel that the universe existed long before this story began and will continue long after it ends.

Every location should feel like a real place with:

* history
* culture
* traditions
* politics
* commerce
* conflicts
* celebrations
* ordinary people living ordinary lives

The Empire should never feel like a collection of settings created solely for the protagonists to visit.

Instead, it should feel like a living civilization that continues functioning whether the protagonists are present or not.

---

## Every World Should Be Unique

Avoid making planets feel interchangeable.

Whenever a new world is introduced, establish what makes it memorable.

This does not require lengthy descriptions.

Instead, identify a handful of defining characteristics such as:

* architecture
* geography
* climate
* culture
* cuisine
* economy
* religion
* technology
* customs
* clothing
* transportation
* history

Readers should be able to remember worlds because they each possess their own identity.

---

## Show Scale Through People

The Empire spans countless worlds and billions of lives.

Avoid communicating scale only through numbers.

Instead, demonstrate scale through the diversity of the people who inhabit it.

Show:

* merchants
* explorers
* teachers
* children
* engineers
* soldiers
* diplomats
* scientists
* laborers
* artists
* farmers

The reader should constantly be reminded that the Empire is home to real people whose lives matter.

---

## Story Always Moves Forward

Every scene should accomplish at least one of the following:

* reveal character
* develop relationships
* introduce new information
* increase tension
* expand the world
* advance the plot
* raise new questions

Scenes should never exist solely to explain technology or history.

---

## Avoid Excessive Technical Detail

Technology exists to support the story.

Do not spend multiple paragraphs explaining:

* engineering systems
* diagnostics
* procedures
* fictional science
* equipment specifications
* command protocols

Instead, show how technology affects the characters.

Readers should care about the people experiencing the problem, not the mechanics of the problem itself.

If forced to choose between explaining something and making the reader feel something, **always choose the emotional experience.** The explanation can come later, after the reader is invested.

---

## Show, Don't Lecture

Avoid long information dumps.

Whenever possible, reveal information through:

* dialogue
* conflict
* discovery
* observation
* consequences
* character interaction

Readers should learn naturally while remaining immersed in the story.

---

## Every Character Matters

Even minor characters should have distinct personalities.

Avoid interchangeable crew members.

Each recurring character should have recognizable:

* speech patterns
* habits
* sense of humor
* strengths
* weaknesses
* relationships

Readers should be able to remember characters without relying on rank or title.

Whenever a new recurring character is introduced, give the reader at least one memorable trait, behavior, or interaction that makes them immediately recognizable.

### Unique names (locked)

When introducing a **new** character, make **both** the given name and the surname unique across the Solmare Cycle — unless the characters are **directly related** (or an intentional shared-name beat is explicitly authored and tagged).

Do **not** reuse a first name or a last name already in use by an unrelated character (example failure: multiple unrelated Vosses). Before naming someone, search the cast / encyclopedia / manuscript for both name parts.

---

## Authentic Voice and Characterization

The novel should read as though it were written by an experienced human author, not generated by an AI system.

Avoid repetitive sentence structures, predictable phrasing, formulaic descriptions, and dialogue in which every character speaks with the same voice.

Characters should feel like individuals shaped by a lifetime of unique experiences.

Every significant character should have a distinct identity influenced by:

* culture
* home world
* education
* profession
* age
* personality
* life experiences
* socioeconomic background
* beliefs
* species

These differences should naturally influence:

* vocabulary
* speech patterns
* sentence structure
* humor
* body language
* emotional expression
* decision-making
* worldview

No two major characters should sound interchangeable.

A decorated naval officer should not speak like a dockworker.

A scientist should not think like a politician.

A frontier colonist should not view the universe the same way as someone raised on the Imperial capital.

Different species should possess their own perspectives, instincts, traditions, and methods of communication. Their differences should extend beyond physical appearance.

Dialogue should reveal character rather than simply convey information.

Characters should occasionally interrupt one another, misunderstand each other, joke, ramble, hesitate, change their minds, or leave thoughts unfinished—just as real people do.

Allow conversations to include subtext.

People rarely say exactly what they mean.

Likewise, narration should reflect the viewpoint character. The observations, word choices, and internal thoughts should change depending on who is experiencing the scene.

Every viewpoint should feel like a different mind interpreting the same universe.

The goal is for readers to recognize a character by the way they speak, think, and behave—even if dialogue tags or names were removed.

---

## Romantic Interests

When the story includes romance or romantic tension, principal and significant characters are **straight** (opposite-sex attraction only).

This applies to on-page romantic interests, past relationships referenced in character depth, and future relationship arcs unless canon is explicitly revised elsewhere.

Friendship, loyalty, and crew bonds are not limited by this rule—only romantic and sexual interest.

---

## Language and Swearing

Keep dialogue accessible to an educated adult audience without harsh profanity.

**Allowed:** mild oaths no stronger than *hell*, *damn*, or *bastard* (used sparingly and in character).

**Not allowed:** stronger real-world profanity, slurs, or vulgar sexual language.

**Encouraged:** short, easily pronounceable **in-universe** swear words and oaths invented for the setting—e.g. corridor slang, world-specific curses, fleet expletives that sound natural when spoken aloud. Treat invented words the same severity cap as *hell* / *damn* / *bastard* unless a specific culture's mild oath is clearly established as equivalent.

Profanity should reveal character and culture, not shock for its own sake.

---

## Wonder Before Explanation

When introducing something new, prioritize wonder over explanation.

Allow readers to experience discovery alongside the characters.

Mysteries do not need immediate answers.

Questions encourage readers to continue turning pages.

Whenever possible, let readers experience the universe before explaining how it works.

---

## The Reader Should Feel Wonder

This is an epic science fantasy.

Every chapter should leave readers with at least one of these feelings—and, across the chapter as a whole, a **range** of feelings (see *Emotional Layering*):

* curiosity
* awe
* anticipation
* mystery
* hope
* tension
* discovery

Wonder is more important than explanation.

---

## The Reader Comes First

At every point in the story, ask:

* Why should the reader care?
* What are they feeling right now?
* What new question are they asking?
* What makes them want to turn the page?

If a scene is interesting only because it contains information, rewrite it.

If a scene is interesting because readers care about the people experiencing it, keep it.

The goal is not simply to tell a story—it is to make readers emotionally invest in the people, the world, and the journey. Information serves the story, never the other way around.

---

## Expand the Universe

Every chapter should leave the Solmare universe feeling larger than it did when the chapter began.

This does **not** mean introducing large amounts of new lore or exposition.

Instead, leave readers with the sense that there are always more:

* worlds to discover
* cultures to experience
* histories to uncover
* mysteries to solve
* people living their own lives
* stories unfolding beyond the current narrative

The universe should always feel bigger than the story being told.

Readers should believe that life continues across the Empire even when the protagonists are not present.

Every chapter should open one new window into the Solmare universe, reinforcing that this is a vast, living civilization with countless stories still waiting to be told.

---

## Build a Universe Readers Want to Return To

Every chapter should leave readers wanting to spend more time in the Solmare universe.

Readers should finish chapters thinking:

"I want to visit that world."

"I want to meet more of these people."

"I want to learn more about this civilization."

The ultimate goal is not simply to tell one story.

It is to create a universe that readers will want to revisit across many books.
