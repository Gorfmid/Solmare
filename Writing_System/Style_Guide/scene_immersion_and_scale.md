# Scene Immersion, Scale, and Information Delivery

**Status:** Locked author doctrine (2026-07-24)  
**Scope:** Drafting and major scene construction for Solmare chapters  
**Not:** Stage 2 Revision Pass numbered doctrine — do not treat as Pass 1–6 checklist items unless the author explicitly asks to apply these rules to a chapter.

**Includes:** Scene immersion · Clancy scale · Crisis escalation · Dialogue · Information delivery · **Solmare scale**

**Cursor rule:** `.cursor/rules/scene-immersion-and-scale.mdc`

---

## Scene immersion rules

The story must prioritize **lived experience over explanation**.

Major events cannot happen primarily through briefings, reports, summaries, or policy discussions.

When a civilization-scale event occurs, **show the impact before the response**.

For major crises, always include:

1. **The first human reaction.**
   - Who notices?
   - What changes in the room?
   - What assumption is immediately broken?

2. **The machinery responding.**
   - People moving.
   - Systems activating.
   - Ships, crews, departments, and institutions reacting.

3. **The emotional scale.**
   - Confusion.
   - Fear.
   - Fatigue.
   - Anger.
   - Determination.

4. **The personal cost.**
   - One person affected.
   - One decision that matters.
   - One ordinary life disrupted.

Avoid resolving major events primarily through:

- meetings
- briefings
- policy discussions
- explanation

A meeting should be the **consequence** of an event, not the event itself.

---

## Clancy scale principle

For military, political, or technological events, do not summarize the machinery.

Show enough operational detail that the reader understands:

- how many people are involved
- how many systems must work
- how much preparation is required
- what failure means

Technical detail should create **tension**, not demonstrate knowledge.

A launch sequence, deployment, investigation, or emergency response should make the reader feel the **weight of the machine**.

---

## Crisis escalation rule

When a major unexpected event occurs:

**Before** characters debate what it means, show:

- alarms
- alerts
- movement
- personnel reactions
- orders being issued
- uncertainty

The reader should experience:

**"What is happening?"**

before receiving:

**"Here is what it means."**

---

## Dialogue rule

Characters should not all speak in institutional language.

Each character retains their own voice.

| Role | Speaks |
|------|--------|
| Officials | policy |
| Operators | reality |
| Scientists | uncertainty |
| Civilians | consequences |
| Soldiers | action |

Reduce repeated structures such as:

*X is not Y. X is Z.*

Use sparingly for emphasis only.

---

## Information delivery rule

Prefer:

**scene → reaction → interpretation**

over:

**interpretation → explanation → scene**

The reader should discover the significance alongside the characters.

---

## Solmare scale rule

The Fleet is not a collection of characters discussing events.

The Fleet is a **living institution**.

When something unprecedented occurs, show:

- command centers waking up
- ships changing status
- crews reacting
- maintenance crews preparing
- families receiving notices
- civilians adapting

The reader should feel that the event is **larger than any one character**.
