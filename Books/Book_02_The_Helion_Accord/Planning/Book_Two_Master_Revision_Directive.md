## 2. Primary Revision Goals

### 2.1 Preserve the existing story

Retain usable scenes, chapter structure, continuity, major beats, strong dialogue, worldbuilding, and Fleet/Dominion terminology. Revise/replace/move/expand only where updated canon requires it. Do not radically restructure the whole manuscript unless a chapter no longer works.

### 2.2 Increase character focus

Emphasize development, relationships, personal history, emotional consequences, trust through repeated interaction, private fears/failures, and life outside formal ops.

War/politics/doctrine/tech remain important but must be **experienced through characters**.

Avoid chapters that function only as briefings, policy talk, technical explanation, strategic summaries, or institutional exposition.

**Every major chapter needs at least one:** relationship change, personal reveal, past affecting present, hidden/admitted fear, friendship deepening, trust earned/damaged, or visible personal consequence.

### 2.3 Simplify the prose

Clearer, warmer, easier than Book One: less technical/stiff/abstract/institutional; more immediate and natural.

Avoid dramatic/obscure metaphors, abstract philosophy, jargon stacks, policy dialogue, multi-idea sentences, characters explaining what they already know, repeated declarations.

Prefer concrete observation, clear emotion, short tense sentences, natural dialogue, simple imagery, tech via consequence/context.

## 3. Theme and End State

> **Trust is harder than war.**

Book Two = **Cooperation.** Fleet and Dominion know they are neighbors; they do not trust each other. Peligar pressure forces limited cooperation before either side is ready.

**Helion Accord** establishes:

- Limited treaty  
- Rescue rights  
- Shared warning network  
- Joint observation  
- Limited military coordination  

Explicitly **not an alliance.** Alliance = Book Three.

## 4. Calder Venn

### Identity (locked)

- Canonical name: **Calder Venn**
- No Nathan; no second Calder; no ancestry/dynasty/bloodline/founding-family plot
- Importance from actions, judgment, failures, command experience, Thessaly relationship, contact/cooperation role

### Arc

Book One strengths (observe, wait, evidence, refuse assumptions) become Book Two danger when applied too rigidly.

**Central flaw:** dependence on certainty — not cowardice or stupidity.

He delays for confirmation, another pass, clearer picture, more evidence, stronger authorization.

### Major failure

- Credible, character-rooted, morally painful, confidence-damaging, not resolved quickly  
- Delay materially reduces chance to evacuate / warn / intervene / save lives  
- Not sole cause of unstoppable disaster; more people could have survived if he acted sooner  
- Defensible decision that was still wrong  

### Crisis of confidence

He does not fully understand the cost until **deep recon**. Then he sees processing speed, short windows, what delay allowed.

Aftermath across **multiple chapters** via behavior: hesitation, second-guessing, withdrawal, poor sleep, overcorrection, avoiding Thessaly, trying to surrender authority, losing faith in First Doctrine and himself.

Do not absolve quickly. Do not fix with one inspiring speech.

## 6. Calder ↔ Thessaly Reciprocal Arc

### Early / middle

Calder trusts her, gives real authority, helps her see one failure does not disqualify command — through repeated choices, not lectures. He is younger/newer but recognizes she already does command work.

### Later

After his failure + recon: she pulls him back without minimizing the dead or claiming he was secretly right. Same truth he gave her returns to him: failure does not remove the next decision.

### End

Thessaly accepts a command position; Calder supports; quiet, mature, earned — not a triumph ceremony. They reach it together. Her recognition that she demanded perfection of herself while telling him to continue helps her accept.

Optional spirit line (do not force): *“You completed First Doctrine. Now we have to write what comes next.”*

## 8. Soraya Ellis and Founding History

### Prelude

Ancient departure from Dominion space during harvest season. Later becomes Founders’ Week (Fleet) and Harvest remembrance (Dominion). Reader may sense connection; **characters must not know**. Neither side knows where the other came from; each assumes independent origin. Do not overexplain.

### Roles (distinct)

| Calder | Ellis |
|--------|-------|
| Present observational truth | Historical archival truth |
| What Peligar do now; cost of delay | Origin, holidays, meaning of Harvest |
| Deep recon | Archive stages; uses Calder’s evidence for final reinterpretation |

**Ellis owns the historical investigation.** Do not assign discovery to Calder, Thessaly, Holt, Park, Rowan, Vex, a one-off Dominion historian, or an automated system.

### Revelation sequence (locked order)

1. Ellis notices similarities between Fleet and Dominion records.  
2. Ellis proves Fleet’s founders originated within the civilization that became the Dominion.  
3. Ellis proves Founders’ Week and Harvest Season commemorate the same departure.  
4. Calder’s deep recon observes modern Peligar processing.  
5. Ellis reexamines ancient “harvest” references.  
6. Ellis concludes departure occurred because a **Peligar harvest** was approaching — founders were **refugees**.

Initial interpretation of the break (after stage 2) may be political/colonial/ideological/etc. **Do not reveal Peligar reason at the same moment as the breakaway proof.**

### Emotional meaning

Fleet: founders were not only explorers — refugees; Founders’ Week keeps only part of the truth.  
Dominion: Harvest was not merely agricultural; those who left were not only deserters; survival traditions began under Peligar pressure.  

Shared coda:

> Fleet remembered leaving.  
> Dominion remembered preparing.  
> Neither remembered what they were escaping.

Strengthen Ellis across the book (frustration, resistance, fear of politicization, emotional reaction) — not a walk-on lore dispenser.

## 9. Peligar Canon

### Communication

No direct communication with Fleet/Dominion: no negotiations, demands, warnings, translated speeches, diplomacy, monologues, ambassadors, POV chapters.

Intercepts sound like layered screaming (multi-channel). Park/specialists may prove structured, repeated, coordinated, linked to movement/processing — **never translate**.

Truth comes from observation (Calder’s recon), not speech.

### No POV

No internal politics, emotions, hierarchy, philosophy, self-name, private motives. Brief incomplete incidental glimpse only; not a character intro.

### Behavior

Not conventional conquerors — **processors**. Everything can become resource. Fleet/Dominion are nuisances (anthill / construction metaphor as author understanding; on page use concrete observation, not lectures). Horror = indifference, not sadism.

### Precursor assessment (Ch.2+ only — do not rewrite Ch.1)

**Author lock.** Full directive: [`peligar_precursor_integration.md`](peligar_precursor_integration.md) · seed: `Universe/The_Dominion/peligar_planning_seed.md`.

- Book One contact / anomalies around *Kestrel Veil* were **not** an attack fleet. Anomalies were caused **in part** by **Peligar precursor activity** (slow assessment of whether a region is worth consuming).
- Expansion model: identify → long-term assess → evaluate conditions → decide processing viability. Not recon/war/diplomacy in their frame — **assessment**.
- *Kestrel Veil* was the first identifiable **witness**, not the anomaly. Activity was already underway; the scout exposed the pattern.
- Dominion implication (later, earned): they are not “returning”; they **never left**; they have been assessing.
- **Reveal pacing:** connected anomalies (early) → pattern (middle) → precursor assessment (later) → watching preparation, not attacks (eventually). No Ch.1 dump.

### Fleet / tech scale

Visible fleet smaller in count than combined human forces; vastly more advanced. Different scale, not upgraded human ships. Real warships exist — not “only construction equipment.” Some capitals = mobile civilizations (habitats, foundries, yards, processing, populations). Larger than Leviathans. Concrete comparisons only (Leviathan in a bay; “station” changes course; blocks stars). No gods/monsters/cosmic-horror metaphors.

**Dominion knowledge lock:** ~1000 years of **defensive** contact; **one observed ship type / return family** in the archive. They hold their edge; they have not pushed into Peligar space. Interior scale/types are unknown until deep recon. See `Universe/The_Dominion/peligar_planning_seed.md`.

### Name adoption

Early: Dominion designation / temporary Fleet class. Later: Fleet uses “Peligar” without qualification through practical ops use — no ceremony.

## 10. Deep Reconnaissance Mission

**Placement:** Late Act IV or early Act V — after Calder’s major delay; after enough trust to share cloak; before his crisis fully resolves. Absorb into existing chapters (do not auto-add chapters).

**Hull (author lock):** ***Kestrel Veil*** — partially repaired (main engines and communications online; may still list / carry unfinished structural work). Comms capable; **TX sealed** deep in Peligar space so they do not paint position.

**Cloak source (author lock):** Field unit taken from ***Long Fathom*** (Dominion donor hull / yard asset), fitted to *Veil* for one mission. **Fleet must return** the unit; not Fleet kit.

**Crew (author lock):**
- *Veil* command crew given an **opt-in** (on page: command circle only; all accept knowing the risks).
- **Thessaly goes** with *Veil* (does not hold the joint table from Helion).
- **Vex rides with Calder’s crew** — present, professional, **not happy about it** (SSD cost + living on a Fleet scout).
- Farewell beats before launch: **Park ↔ Holt**; **Calder ↔ Lisette** (aunt / Cardamom Oven); other farewells optional and light.

**Profile:** Dominion cloak on Fleet hull; silent running; passive only; emission control; minimal/no TX; Dominion expertise aboard via Vex. Submarine tension, not battle spectacle. Cloak **quirks:** mix of mechanical limits (heat / field budget / odd dropouts) and light comic glitches — never make the tech a joke that removes danger.

**Calder expects:** bases, colonies, supply lines, conventional empire / attack logistics.  
**Calder finds:** processing; mobile civilization scale; no peer recognition; short windows explained; evidence that **assessment / precursor work** was already running ahead of open processing (they were not “arriving to invade”).  
**Connects to failure:** understands people were alive while he kept observing; delay didn’t cause the Peligar — it reduced survival against a process that had already been measuring the region.

## 11. Dominion Cloaking

Based on recovered/reverse-engineered Peligar fragments. Inferior copies; incomplete science; major advantage over Fleet; authentic Peligar still far ahead. Sharing = enormous political cost. Limits: heat, speed, power, passive only, no TX/active scan, duration, focused-scan detection risk, **hard rule — cannot fire and maintain cloak** (field finishes on weapons discharge; same rule for Dominion copies and authentic Peligar), hard nav, crew strain. **Under fire:** holding cloak is not invulnerability — it can spoil enemy track / cause misses / soft hits; enough fire still lands. Dropping cloak is the price of returning fire. **Book Two share model:** one-mission loan of *Long Fathom*’s unit to *Kestrel Veil*; **must be returned**; not Fleet routine kit by book end. Not an easy later plot device. On *Veil*, the downsized/adapted fit is imperfect — quirks and failures at odd times are allowed (mechanical + light comic), provided detection risk stays real.

**Process peek (author lock):** During the loan / install / recovery cycle, Fleet gets a **limited look at how Dominion builds cloak fields** (yards, materials, incomplete reverse-engineering steps — enough for engineers to understand the shape of the problem). That peek does **not** yield a Fleet-built cloak in Book Two. Fabrication timeline is long; any Fleet cloak is a later-book question. SSD will treat even the peek as contamination; keep it sealed and incomplete on page.

## 14. Tone, Dialogue, Prose Rules

**Tone:** serious, procedural, human, thoughtful, hopeful without sentimentality. No profanity, sexual content, chosen-one, simple villains, cosmic horror, multiverse, exaggerated heroics, action one-liners. Allow humor, friendship, quiet affection, awkwardness, fatigue, grief, ordinary life.

**Dialogue:** natural; interrupt/hesitate/joke/leave unsaid. Tags: said / asked / answered. Avoid doctrine summaries and mission-brief dialogue.

**Analogies:** concrete and brief only (scale, character, clarity). Never “dead god swallowing eternity.”

**Technical language:** necessary detail only; teach by consequence; one clear image beats jargon.

10. End on turn / question / consequence / emotional beat.  

Do not force every plotline into every chapter. Revise as a **unified novel** — track emotional momentum across files.
