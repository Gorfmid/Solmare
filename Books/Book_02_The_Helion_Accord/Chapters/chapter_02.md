# Chapter 2

## Shared Words

---

***KESTREL VEIL — READY ROOM***
*CONTACT NODE SEVEN BERTH*

The packet from Helion had arrived overnight and still sat on the ready-room table like it might retract if nobody touched it.

Calder had read it. Twice. The second time had not improved the contents.

He hated it.

Not the work of talking. Talking he could do. He hated what the packet asked him to become: a scout captain turned into an envoy, sitting in a bolted room arguing over procedures and word lists while Helion watched from a safe distance. He had built his command on watching longer than politics preferred, and on not claiming more than the instruments showed. This packet wanted him to hold a table between two civilizations that still treated each other as a security problem. It wanted decisions from him without giving him a clear situation to decide.

He set the packet down far harder than he meant to.

"Why us," he said quietly. "We are not trained for this. We did not ask for it."

"Rowan wants a Fleet decision-maker at Contact Node Seven," he said. "Protocol work. Building a word list both sides can use. Provisional contact rules with Dominion Civil Government in the room." He looked at Thessaly, then at Park, then at the bulkhead as if the ship might offer a better solution. "We are a scout crew. We are not mediators. Helion has an entire Diplomatic Office that exists so that we do not have to invent this."

Thessaly did not reach for the packet. She had already read it. "Diplomatic Office stays on Helion. They already solved the first problem: how to talk at all. Eighteen months of contact was long enough for Fleet Communications and Dominion Science to train a few speakers each. Those people can carry an ordinary conversation without a machine. Interpreters will be in the venue. What Rowan wants from us is different. Someone who can end a session. Someone who can decide what words both sides may use. Park has the recording in custody and owns the operational word list once both sides start building one. Council will not send career envoys into the Neutral Zone until someone builds procedures that survive first contact with stubborn people."

"So we sit there while someone else translates," Calder said.

"You sit there so the translation does not become policy by accident," Thessaly said. "Helion can argue from a distance. Someone still has to make sure a bad word does not start a fight."

"That is a polite way of saying we are expendable," Calder said.

"That is a precise way of saying we already had a seat at the table," Thessaly said.

Brenner leaned in the hatch with a tool kit under one arm. He had come for a panel. He found a briefing instead.

"If they wanted diplomats," he said, "they picked the wrong ship. I spend half my watch keeping the coffee urn running."

Park did not look up from the slate. He had a *Kestrel* mug on the table beside him. Without commenting, he turned it so the label faced the bulkhead, away from Calder.

Calder noticed. The deliberate turn. The wrong side of the mug. For half a second he thought of Lisette — first command, the ridiculous print she had chosen — and stared.

Park did not elaborate.

"Session One in forty minutes," Thessaly said. "Park has the header stack. I have seating. You hold Fleet authority at the table, whether you like the job or not."

Calder picked up the packet, folded it once, and put it in his pocket. "I do not like the job."

"Neither do I," Thessaly said. She held his eye a beat longer than procedure required. "That does not change the assignment. It is the job. We do it anyway."

Calder nodded once. He trusted her judgment. That was enough.

"Then we do it," he said, and gave her a wry smile. The insider kind. They both knew how little either of them wanted this.

Thessaly returned it. Brief. Shared.

Brenner pushed off the hatch frame. "For the record," he said, "if anyone asks what I am doing while you play diplomats, I will be keeping the ship running."

He chuckled once and headed aft toward the panel he had come for in the first place.

"You do that," Calder said.

They left the ready room. Calder, then Thessaly, then Park. The mug went with Park. Calder did not ask why.

***CONTACT NODE SEVEN — NEUTRAL ZONE OUTER CORRIDOR***
*PROTOCOL SESSION ONE*

The room had two tables bolted down the length of the deck, Fleet on one side and Dominion on the other, with a gap between for a runner. Relay pedestals for the voices joining from elsewhere. No banners. No shared seal.

Park sat on the Fleet side with his slate open to the draft header stack and a coffee mug from the *Kestrel*. The mug was labeled BEST CAPTAIN EVER — a gift from Calder's aunt Lisette for first command, tourist-tacky and sincere in the same breath. Park knew three things about it. It was not his. Nobody in Communications had claimed it. Captain Calder had spent six months quietly looking for it without formally reporting it missing. Park had considered returning it. He had decided against it. The meeting was already complicated enough.

The node pantry stocked only tea. Across from him, the Dominion side was still empty — tea already poured into cups, waiting for people who had not arrived yet. The Helion pedestal was labeled for Admiral Rowan. The second waited for a Civil Government voice joining remotely.

The walls were institutional gray. A second wall display had gone up the week before; the screws were still bright. The room smelled like recycled air and machine oil.

Outside the sealed viewports, the Neutral Zone outer corridor held ordinary traffic: freighters on approved routes, a Dominion border watch holding station, a Fleet packet runner waiting for clearance. Months ago, during the early talks after contact, Fleet and Dominion had drawn the zone as a buffer — warships not allowed, packet and freight traffic still permitted under agreed corridors. What moved past the glass was what that rule still allowed. None of that traffic knew what the room inside was for.

Thessaly checked the seating chart one more time. Calder stood near the wall display, reading the agenda without looking comfortable about any line of it.

Park had arrived early on purpose. He walked the room before politics filled it: exits, recorder ports, who sat with their back to a door. Rowan would join remotely from Helion — talk over him and the gap in the channel would make it look like rudeness whether you meant it or not. Thessaly had already rearranged one chair so Senka would not face the Fleet door alone.

"We still have not agreed on the recording," Thessaly said. "Fleet wants the whole session on audio. Dominion wants a written summary only. No raw sound unless both sides say yes."

"I know," Park said. "I put both versions in the packet."

"And suspension?"

"Either side can suspend. Either side can leave. Neither side can force the other to keep talking." He tapped the slate. "That part they agreed on. The contested parts are still in both drafts."

Calder said, "Keep the contested parts visible. Do not smooth them for the record."

Park nodded. He had already written the cover note that way. Eighteen months of contact had taught Fleet how to exchange greetings, traffic notices, and ordinary courtesy through interpreters and learned speakers. It had not taught anyone how to build a shared operational word list. The *Kestrel*'s audio intercept was still in the archive, provisional and unknown. Park still did not have a better category for it.

He had slept badly since that watch. The waveform stayed with him.

The Dominion party entered through the far door: two linguists in Science Directorate gray, and a Civil Government liaison in the darker coat that meant political authority without military rank. A Fleet Communications interpreter took the standing post by the wall display. A Dominion speaker took the matching post opposite. Ordinary talk would go through them when someone needed it. The harder work was operational words that could move ships. That was why Park was already seated.

For a moment nobody spoke.

Eighteen months of packets and compressed stills had not prepared anyone in this room for unmediated air.

Calder had seen Dominion faces in advance packets — compressed, labeled, safe at a distance. Standing across the gap, the distance failed. Hands. Breath. Eyes. Skin under the same station light. Not humanoid. Human. Every detail a screen could argue away and a room could not.

Park saw it too. Same hands. Same eyes.

On the Dominion side, Senka's face lost its practiced stillness for a moment. Orlen's notebook stayed closed a beat too long. Vorin looked at Calder's hands and did not look away fast enough.

Nobody named what they were seeing. Suspicion held. So did the silence.

Thessaly broke it by checking that both interpreters were in post and neither door was blocked.

The liaison was a woman in her fifties with the posture of someone who had spent a career making rooms behave. She introduced herself as Delegate Lira Senka, in careful Fleet speech. Learned. Practiced. She did not offer a hand. Neither did Calder.

"Captain Venn. Commander Thessaly. Officer Park," Senka said, using the names from the advance packet with careful accuracy. "Civil Government authorizes this venue under provisional contact rules. We are here to set procedures both sides can use. Nothing beyond that scope today."

"Understood," Calder said. The word came out flatter than he meant. He had expected a foreign face. He had found a familiar one. That was worse.

The linguists took their seats. The senior one set a small notebook and a tea cup in precise alignment. She was thin, mid-forties, with the kind of attention that made silence feel like work. "Nadya Orlen. Science Directorate, Comparative Speech. My colleague is Ilya Vorin."

Vorin was younger, early thirties, and watched the relay pedestals the way Park watched buffer queues. He said, "Authorized scope is speech between our two sides only. Not the external intercept material."

"Agreed," Park said.

Rowan's channel opened on the Fleet pedestal. Short enough to feel like presence. Long enough to remind everyone that Helion was not in the room.

"Admiral Rowan," Calder said.

"I have the agenda," Rowan said. "We are authorizing contact protocols for operational exchange. Not alliance language. Not public framing. If either side needs to stop, you stop. Park runs the language work. Thessaly owns procedure. Captain Venn holds Fleet authority for decisions at this table. Delegate Senka, do you confirm Dominion authority for this session?"

"I confirm provisional authority," Senka said. "Strategic Security retains review on anything touching classified external contact. Science Directorate retains review on linguistic method. Civil Government retains the right to end the venue."

"Then we begin," Rowan said. "Header stack first. Who may speak. Who records. What may be said on a shared channel."

Park put the draft on the wall display.

Speaker turns were timed. To interrupt, you had to ask first and say who was speaking. Raw audio recording was restricted to language work sessions only, and only with dual consent. Operational status exchanges used text headers with controlled vocabulary. Either side could suspend a session without explanation beyond *protocol hold*. Neither side could claim the other's silence as agreement.

Senka read it twice. "We accept the structure. We reject Fleet's request for complete raw session audio. Summary logs only, except during authorized language work."

"Fleet Council will dislike that," Rowan said.

"Strategic Security will dislike everything else," Senka said. "Choose which dislike you can live with."

Rowan did not argue the point long. "Accepted for Session One. Revisit after language work proves useful or dangerous."

"Both are likely," Orlen said, without looking up from her notebook.

Thessaly almost smiled. She did not.

They spent forty minutes on classification tags for shared traffic. Fleet wanted a clean ladder: routine, elevated, urgent, critical. Dominion wanted historical precedent tags and inheritance markers Fleet headers had no place for. Park translated between them until they had a usable compromise: urgency grades on top, provenance notes underneath. Private reading stayed private. It did not go into the shared tags.

"If we do not know," Calder said, "we say we do not know."

"My instructor called that the first honesty," Orlen said. "People forget it under pressure."

Vorin glanced at Senka before he spoke. "SSD will review every classified external-contact header before it leaves Dominion custody."

"Fleet Intelligence will do the same on our side," Rowan said. "Suspicion stays on the board today. That is how we work."

Senka poured more tea for her own side. Nobody asked Fleet to drink it. Park kept his coffee where it was.

Calder asked for a suspend drill before they closed the first block. If either side killed the channel under pressure, both needed the handshake already practiced. Thessaly ran it: Fleet suspends, Dominion acknowledges, both sides stop talking, no one treats silence as consent. Then they reversed it. The pedestals recorded the acknowledgments.

"Again," Calder said.

They ran it again. The second time was cleaner. Park noticed Vorin's shoulders drop a fraction after the second successful suspend.

By the end of the first hour they had a brittle protocol that existed on both systems and pleased no one completely.

"Language work begins after the break," Thessaly said. "Park and the Directorate team. Command presence reduced to on-call. Suspension stays available to both sides."

Senka stood. "We will not hand you our language whole. We will give you enough shared speech that neither side claims the other meant what it did not. That is the limit."

"That is enough for today," Calder said.

Rowan's channel closed on a short acknowledgment. The pedestals went dark.

Park closed his slate and looked at Orlen's notebook. She wrote in a dense hand, lines neat enough to be archival.

Calder stopped beside him on the way out. "Vorin kept the *Kestrel* intercept off today's table. You still looked like you were arguing with that archive the whole hour."

"Ortega called it distress on the bridge," Park said. "I refused the flag. I still reached for the nearest human category because I did not have another one. That reach is what people will keep quoting."

"Language work is next," Calder said. "Fix the classification when you have something better. Do not fake a cleaner answer."

Park nodded. That was the instruction he needed, and the one he disliked.

"Ready?" he asked Orlen.

"My grandmother used to say readiness is what you bring when the room is already wrong," Orlen said. "So yes."

***CONTACT NODE SEVEN — LANGUAGE SUITE***
*HUMAN CHANNEL WORK*

The language suite was smaller than the protocol room. One shared table. Two wall boards. A dual-consent recorder that stayed dark until both sides keyed it live. Tea warmer on the Dominion end. Fleet coffee urn on Park's — he had checked the pantry and decided he would not negotiate on Dominion hospitality alone.

They did not begin with words anyone needed for ordinary talk. Eighteen months had already produced greetings, traffic notices, and speakers who could carry a conversation. They began with the phrases that could move ships — or stop them from moving when they should not.

Park wrote the first contested line on the board: Fleet *engage*.

Orlen did not offer a single Dominion match. She wrote three inherited uses and boxed none of them. "Take up a duty. Confront a problem. Begin a watch. Yours means open hostilities under rules. If the interpreters gloss them as one word, someone dies believing the other meant procedure."

Vorin added *stand down* underneath. "Worse. Fleet means cease fire. We have three related phrases depending on whether the hold is temporary, honorable, or forced. Your first joint alert draft nearly told a Dominion ship to leave its watch when you meant stop shooting."

"That one kills people," he said, flat.

"Yes," Park said. He struck the draft. "We write it again."

They rebuilt the phrase until both sides could say *hold weapons* and mean the same physical action. Then they tested it by having Orlen speak the Dominion line and Park speak the Fleet line into the dual-consent recorder, then swapping and listening for drift. There was drift. They cut it again.

"No poetry," Park said. "No cleverness. If a word means two different actions, we cut it."

Orlen nodded once. "Accepted."

They worked for two hours without spectacle and with constant friction.

Coffee and tea stayed on the table. Orlen drank between entries. Park refilled once when his mug emptied and went back to the board. Thessaly looked in once, listened to three minutes of argument about the word *neighbor*, and left without comment.

"Neighbor," Orlen said, "implies shared boundary and mutual recognition. We have not granted that."

"Fleet used it in public statements," Park said. "I know. It was political language. For this room, we can use *adjacent traffic* or *contacting party*."

"Contacting party," Vorin said. "Less claim."

Park wrote it down.

False friends kept appearing. Fleet *secure* meant lock down and protect. Dominion *secure* in one older register meant make certain through historical precedent. Fleet *clear* meant empty of threat. Dominion *clear* could mean accounted for in the record. Park started a separate column titled *looks shared, is not* and filled it faster than he liked.

"Fleet mints a new term when the old one fails," Orlen said during a pause. Dry. Precise. "Dominion keeps the inherited term and forces the procedure to fit it."

"Both habits break under pressure," Park said.

"Yes. That is why we are here."

Senka visited at the ninety-minute mark and stood in the doorway. "Operational terms only on the open board," she said. "Words that move ships. Nothing that teaches doctrine or opens a border brief. If a line drifts past that, end it and note *out of scope*."

"Understood," Park said.

After she left, Orlen said, "She is not wrong. Soft language travels farther than people intend."

"Fleet worries about that too," Park said. "A vague word gets into a status line, then onto a ship, then into someone's hands when they do not have time to argue what it meant. So we only put a term on the board if both sides mean the same action."

Vorin almost smiled. It did not become one. "Old Commander Petran told my class that useful enemies are still enemies. He was not a cheerful man."

"Sounds accurate," Park said.

They broke for a short meal in the anteroom. Dominion rations were denser and more heavily seasoned than Fleet standard. Park ate what was offered and did not comment. Orlen watched how he handled the unfamiliar seasoning, then wrote one line when he looked away. Vorin talked about academy speech drills: inherited quotation as discipline, not decoration. Park talked about relay poker and how he played badly on purpose to keep people talking. Orlen found that interesting enough to write down too.

"Fleet asks what works now," Vorin said. "Dominion asks what held before. Both questions can be honest. Both can miss the thing in front of them."

"Both also miss tomorrow," Park said. "I can write *hold weapons* and *resume*. If the outer corridor goes bad next week, we have nothing ready for that."

Orlen set her cup down. "That is outside today's board."

"I know," Park said. "I am still going to tell Calder."

"Can you live with missing it until then?" Orlen asked.

"For today, yes," Park said. "I am not going to fake next week's readiness at lunch. I am also not going to pretend the hole is fine."

After the meal they ran a live turn-taking drill: Park speaking Fleet terms into the open channel, Orlen answering in Dominion, Vorin catching substitutions before they hardened. Three times they nearly accepted a phrase that sounded shared and meant opposite obligations. Three times they cut it.

By midday they had a working list of sixty-three shared terms for operational speech between their sides: hold weapons, protocol hold, status request, status reply, unknown origin, unresolved mass, provisional category, suspend, resume, map later. Map later was Calder's phrase, added when he stopped by and refused to let them pretend certainty about charts they had not yet agreed to show.

Park caught him before he left the doorway. "Vorin said Fleet asks what works now and Dominion asks what held before. He is right. Nobody here is ready for what comes after. If next week is worse than this week, someone will guess under pressure and ships will move on it."

Calder listened. "You are right. We are not ready for that." He glanced at the board. "Do not put fake readiness up there. Leave *map later* blank until we can talk charts without guessing. I will tell Rowan we have a hole."

"Do not schedule the map conversation inside a language session," he added, for Orlen and Vorin. "Language first. Geography when the words can carry it."

"Yes, Captain," Orlen said.

Park noticed she used *Captain* the way Fleet used it: as a role, not a courtesy flourish.

When they paused, Orlen closed her notebook and looked at Park's board work.

"You hear hesitation," she said. "That is useful. My instructor said speech is half what is said and half what is delayed."

"I remember conversations," Park said. "Who changed order. Who stopped joking. That kind of thing."

"Keep using that," Orlen said. "When someone demands a finished answer before we have one, do not give it to them."

"Our speech first," he said.

"Our speech first," she agreed.

Vorin keyed the dual-consent recorder off and packed the morning slate copies into separate custody sleeves, Fleet and Dominion. "SSD will want to read every term," he said. "They will look for anything that can mean two actions."

"Let them look," Park said. "If a term can mean two actions, we already failed."

Orlen poured tea for the afternoon session and set Park's coffee urn back within his reach without comment.

***CONTACT NODE SEVEN — ANALYSIS COMPARTMENT***
*INTERCEPT REVIEW*

They brought the *Kestrel* recording into the analysis compartment in the afternoon, under dual consent and dual custody.

Park had not wanted to be the one to open it. He opened it anyway.

The waveform had stayed in his head since the intercept: overlapping registers, intensity that looked like group pain if you forced a human analogy, structure that refused the analogy once you stopped needing it. Ortega's sensor package sat on one board. Petrov's Dominion analysis notes, sanitized by SSD for shared review, sat on the other. Vorin handled the Dominion side of the render. Park handled Fleet audio. Thessaly stood where she could see both faces. Calder sat at the end of the table with his arms folded.

A third relay line ran open beside Rowan's: Fleet Communications Command, validation desk, a voice Park would have known blind after ten years.

"Holt, you have the Ortega package?" Park asked.

"I have it," Dana Holt said. "I've had it since last night. I was hoping you'd call before the Dominion side told you what it meant."

"I'm calling now."

"Barely." A pause, drier than the line needed. "You look terrible. I can hear it."

"Long week."

"Eat something after this," Holt said, and then her voice went flat and professional in the way Park recognized from watching her work a board for a decade. "I ran the infrared and polarization overlays against three independent Fleet archive events. They're not noise. They repeat. I can't tell you what they mean. I can tell you Fleet instruments see the same structure the *Kestrel* saw, on our own equipment, without anyone across the table telling us what to look for."

Park felt something loosen that he hadn't known was tight. Not relief. Company. And under it: if those older events belonged with this recording, then the *Kestrel* had not invented the problem. It had walked into one already running.

"Good," he said. "Stay on."

"I'm not going anywhere," Holt said.

"Play the audible fraction," Orlen said. "Low."

Park opened it low.

The sound filled the compartment: layered, dissonant, multi-vocal, too much for a single throat. It was not loud. It was worse than loud — wet overtones stacked on dry ones, a crowd of voices tearing through the audio clip without agreeing on a note, like something being taken apart while it tried to speak. The dissonance in the tonal qualities went straight into the skull. It hurt. Calder's hand went over his ear. Thessaly followed. Park kept both hands on the board and felt his shoulders climb anyway. Orlen did not cover hers. Vorin watched the spectral bands through it, jaw tight.

"Stop," Orlen said after twelve seconds.

Park stopped it.

Orlen looked at Vorin. Vorin nodded once, small.

"We know this," Orlen said. She did not sound triumphant. "Not the meaning. The shape. Science Directorate has heard it before. You have not. To human ears it sounds like pain. It is not."

"It sounded like that on our bridge too," Park said. "We had no better account of it."

"Pain would be a petition," Vorin said. "This is not asking. It is structured emission on more than one band. We have never recovered meaning from it."

Thessaly said, "Then what is it?"

Orlen glanced at Senka's empty chair, then at the SSD review stamp on the Dominion packet, then at Calder. "We need Civil Government and SSD in the room before we open how much of our archive we can share. For this table, today: we recognize the behavior. We call the source Peligar. That is our name, not theirs. We have never translated them. Not in any working sense."

Park stared at the waveform.

Multi-channel. Separable bands. No destination-targeting. Infrared and polarization markers sat in Ortega's supplemental as unresolved overlays. Harmonic structure ran under the audible mess.

"Park." Holt's voice, low, over the relay. "The overlays I flagged. Look at the timing against the audible spikes."

He looked. The layers moved with the sound, ahead of it in places. Not random. Not any format he had a name for.

"What the hell is this?" Park said.

Nobody answered for a second.

"I see the same structure on three older Fleet archive events," Holt said. "Repeating. Linked. I still cannot tell you what it means."

Orlen did not look surprised. "We believe they are talking. We do not know with whom, or about what. You are hearing a fraction. Infrared, polarization, harmonic layers travel with it. Human ears take the part that sounds like screaming because that is what human hearing can hold. The rest is still there."

Park's hands were flat on the table. "And you still cannot translate any of it."

"No," Vorin said. Immediate. Absolute. "Pattern recognition. Latency maps. Partial science. Not meaning. Not after years of trying."

"So you recognize speech," Thessaly said, "and neither of us can read it."

"Correct," Orlen said. "Pain would at least be aimed at someone. This continues without you."

Park looked at Calder. "I want to put *probable language* instead of *consistent with language*. It is stronger."

"Keep *consistent with*," Calder said. "If someone later proves meaning, we can upgrade. Do not write more certainty than we have."

Park typed *consistent with* and left *probable* off the board.

Calder said, "Update the classification. Language without recoverable meaning. Origin unresolved. Threat undetermined. Provisional remains. Dominion designation Peligar may be noted as their name for the source — exonym only. No translation claimed."

"Senka will want share limits before that name travels wider," Orlen said. She stopped. Looked at Calder, then at the open Fleet relay. "And you will send it up anyway. Priority security. Rowan. Intelligence. Whether Civil Government has finished a permission list or not."

"Yes," Calder said. "As you will do with any sensitive information we may impart. I suggest we work together."

Orlen's mouth tightened. Then she nodded once. "On the recognition, yes. How deep into our archive you dig stays a later argument."

"Park, write what we can certify in this room," Calder said.

Park wrote the correction into the Fleet supplemental. He made it exact.

*Prior provisional unknown-origin classification updated. Reclassified: multi-channel structured emission consistent with language; meaning unrecovered; Dominion designation Peligar available as exonym only; no translation capability demonstrated or claimed by either side.*

He read it aloud. Orlen listened. Vorin checked the Dominion equivalent text. Thessaly watched for political landmines. Calder watched for false certainty.

"Is there any chance we recover meaning with better instruments?" Park asked.

Orlen shook her head once. "We have already put better listening gear on this channel than anything in your packet. More bands. Cleaner separation. Still no meaning. Better instruments give you more of the signal. Not the mind behind it. Do not buy that hope."

Calder nodded. "Log the correction. Keep provisional on threat. I will not upgrade unknown speech into known enemy just to make the record look finished."

"Log it," Thessaly said, quieter, to Park.

Park sent the corrected classification into the Fleet local system and the dual-consent shared packet. The old provisional unknown stayed visible in history as incomplete work.

Orlen poured tea for herself and Vorin. Neither celebrated. The corrected category sat beside the colder fact that the thing speaking was still out there.

Calder stood. He looked Orlen in the eye. "Is there anything we need to know now about the Peligar?"

Orlen met his eye. "For tonight: do not engage. Do not assume silence means they left."

Vorin said, "We cannot give you more than that from this room without Senka. Archive depth stays closed."

"And maps," Thessaly said.

"Tomorrow," Orlen said. "After we have spoken with our Directorate — and Senka — we may be able to expand on what we can share. Within their guidelines."

Calder nodded once. "My recommendation to Rowan: threat stays undetermined until we have more than a name. Keep the recording under dual custody. Park's classification goes up Fleet channels now — Rowan, Intelligence, need-to-know. We work together on what comes next. We do not wait for permission to warn our own people."

"Holt, still with me?" Park asked.

"Still here. I'll write up the Fleet-side confirmation tonight and route it to you before the morning session, not after, so you're not walking in blind again."

"You didn't have to stay on the whole time."

"I know," Holt said. "Eat something, Jun. That's not a request, it's a validation note." A beat, quieter. "Get some sleep."

"I'll try."

The relay closed on her end first, the way it usually did.

Park closed the waveform display. The sound was gone. The name was not. Peligar. Talking, maybe. Not to them. Not in any language anyone at the table could read or understand. Do not engage. Tell Rowan. Wait on maps until Dominion came back with what they would share.

He sat with that until the compartment emptied.

On the way back to the language suite, he stopped at the anteroom viewport and watched ordinary corridor traffic that still did not know what the room behind him had just named.

***CONTACT NODE SEVEN — OPERATIONS RELAY***
*JOINT STATUS EXCHANGE*

The near-miss happened before dinner and almost became a shooting problem.

It started as a routine joint status exchange under the new shared terms: two Dominion border watch vessels and one Fleet survey craft sharing position and posture on the outer corridor so nobody treated ordinary traffic as a threat. Park and Vorin had built the controlled vocabulary that morning. The software on both sides was supposed to map those shared terms into each side's native command language without changing what they meant.

Supposed to.

Park was mid-sentence with Orlen about turn-taking markers when the operations relay lit amber and Thessaly's voice came through from the protocol room.

"Park. Now."

He went. Calder was already there. The wall display showed three tracks and a status exchange that had gone wrong in the middle.

Fleet survey craft *Meridian Gauge* had sent: `STATUS REPLY / HOLD WEAPONS / UNKNOWN ORIGIN BEARING CLEAR`.

Dominion border watch *K-17 Node Three* had received a native render that, after software substitution, read as an order to cease watch and open approach corridor for unidentified traffic.

"That is not what we wrote," Park said.

"I know," Thessaly said. "The substitution table mapped *hold weapons* against an older Dominion phrase for *stand down watch posture*. Someone loaded a legacy lexicon overnight."

"We tested the table," Park said. "Live turn-taking. Both sides. It rendered clean. This is not that table."

Vorin's face went tight. "SSD review copy. Not Science Directorate. They pushed a security lexicon patch without telling us — after the test passed."

Senka arrived fast enough that she had clearly been monitoring. "Explain.  Now."

Calder did not raise his voice. "Your patch told a border watch to drop posture while our survey craft reported a clean bearing. If someone on that watch had been more obedient than curious, we would have an open corridor and a confused Fleet ship in the same space."

"And if Fleet software had been the one that changed the meaning," Senka said, "you would be blaming us for sabotage while your own table did the damage."

"Yes," Calder said. "That is also true. Which is why we contain this procedurally before anyone sells a story."

Thessaly was already writing the containment order. "Both sides freeze lexicon patches. Dual consent required for any substitution table change. *Meridian Gauge* holds position. *K-17 Node Three* restores prior watch posture manually, spoken confirmation only, no software render until we clear the table."

Park opened a live channel to both ships with Orlen beside him and Vorin checking Dominion phrasing line by line. His voice stayed level. His hands did not.

"Node Three, Contact Node Seven," Park said. "Disregard last software render. Confirm spoken instruction: restore standard watch posture. Hold weapons means hold weapons. It does not mean leave your post."

A Dominion officer answered after a pause. "Confirming. Watch posture restored. We nearly complied with the render. My duty officer questioned it because it contradicted standing orders."

"Good questioning," Orlen said into the channel. "Log it. We need that on the record."

On the Fleet side, *Meridian Gauge* confirmed they had never altered course and had not illuminated. Their captain sounded irritated. Calder thanked them flat.

Blame arrived on schedule.

Senka wanted SSD's patch logged as unauthorized interference with an approved language session. Rowan, when the relay caught him twelve minutes later, wanted Fleet's acceptance testing logged as incomplete. A Fleet Intelligence note appeared suggesting Dominion had tested whether Fleet would notice a dangerous substitution. A Dominion security note appeared suggesting Fleet had designed ambiguous shared terms to force Dominion into error.

Thessaly put both notes on the same board. Finger-pointing. She left them there and did not answer either one.

"We have a process failure," she said. "Sabotage is a guess. Incompetence is a guess. Doctrine clash is a guess. None of them get to skip the repair."

Calder nodded. "Repair first. Narrative later, if ever."

Park and Vorin rebuilt the substitution table by hand, term by term, with Orlen reading each line aloud and Senka forced to wait until the table could not kill anyone by accident. It took fifty-one minutes. It felt longer. Park's conversational recall helped more than pride: he could reconstruct which morning argument had produced which shared term, and which near-match had crept in while they were tired. Vorin caught two Dominion-side inheritances that still mapped wrong under stress. Orlen struck both without apology.

When they finished, both ships confirmed clean renders on a test exchange that said nothing more ambitious than *status reply / bearing clear / hold weapons*.

Park sat back. His hands were unsteady in a small way he hoped nobody measured. He thought of the BEST CAPTAIN EVER mug still sitting in the language suite and wanted, absurdly, to be back on false friends instead of watching ships almost open a corridor on a bad phrase.

"Language work can kill people if we get it wrong," he said. "It is not optional."

"Yes," Vorin said. "That was the demonstration. I would have preferred a quieter one."

Brenner stuck his head in from the corridor, tool kit under one arm, as if he had come for a panel and found a crisis instead. He glanced at the three tracks still frozen on the wall display.

"Seems to me," he said, "this keeps happening if both sides keep running the corridor through separate boxes. Put one shared outpost on the Neutral Zone line — between our Alpha Seven traffic and their Volume Seventeen — and route the status work through that. Spoken confirmation in one room. Nobody patches overnight into a ship without the other side seeing it."

He shrugged. "Not my department. Just saying."

Vorin did not dismiss it. "It might be acceptable. It would take time. Years, if Civil Government and SSD both have to bless the site. And I would not trust Fleet to build it alone."

"Then we build it together," Calder said. "Joint crew. Joint custody of the boards. Not tomorrow. Not this week. But that is the fix that survives more than one near-miss."

Thessaly was already making a note. "I will put it in the evening packet as a recommendation. Capitals will hate the cost. They should also hate missing the upside — denser trade through the buffer, navigable flight lanes both sides can trust, fewer near-misses eating ships. The corridor will keep needing it either way."

She looked up. "How long to build, if anyone actually authorized it?"

"Twelve to eighteen months," Brenner said.

"Twelve to eighteen months," Vorin said, almost on top of him.

They looked at each other.

"With the right people," Brenner added.

"And dual custody of the yard," Vorin said. "Not Fleet alone."

Brenner nodded once, satisfied he had been heard, and went back to whatever panel he had actually come for.

Rowan's final instruction on the channel was brief. "Continue protocols. Do not widen distribution of the near-miss beyond command need. Park, you and the Directorate team own lexicon custody jointly from this point. If either side patches alone again, I will suspend the venue."

Senka matched him without warmth. "Agreed. SSD will be reminded that Civil Government authorized Science Directorate method for this channel."

After the channels cleared, Thessaly left the Dominion tea service untouched and did not refill the Fleet urn. She did not have to. Kevin came through with a tray — coffee, tea, and a stack of cardamom buns still warm enough to fog the plate. The Cardamom Oven mark was on the wrapping. Calder saw it and did not ask how bakery stock from Helion had found a Neutral Zone corridor.

Park took a cup without looking up. Vorin reached for coffee instead of tea, tried a sip, and set it down at once.

"I do not know how you drink this," he said.

Park almost smiled. "Practice."

Orlen took tea and one of the buns. Vorin took a bun too, tried it, and looked almost offended that it was good.

"As bad as that black stuff is," he said, "the buns are delightful."

Calder smile knowingly. "Yes. Yes, they are."

Kevin left the rest on the sideboard and went out the way he had come.

Park walked the substitution table one more time with Vorin before they locked custody. "If SSD pushes another patch," Park said, "I need a hard stop on my side before it reaches a ship."

"You will have one," Vorin said. "I will have one too. If either of us is overridden, we suspend the venue and make the override visible."

"Visible is the only protection we have," Park said.

"Then we keep it visible," Orlen said, notebook still open. "Whatever that costs us with SSD."

They went back to work.

***CONTACT NODE SEVEN — PROTOCOL ROOM***
*DISCLOSURE LIMITS*

Evening session. Same two tables. Fewer people.

Rowan on relay. Senka in person. Calder, Thessaly, Park on Fleet side. Orlen and Vorin on Dominion side.

Senka opened the disclosure agenda. "What may be shared next. Maps. Warnings. The name Peligar in wider Fleet use. Depth of explanation."

Rowan said, "Fleet will not accept unexplained fear as orders. If you want us to move ships, you need more than a word and a corrected classification."

"And we will not explain everything so you can inventory our border," Senka said. "Science Directorate may share signal science under review. Civil Government may authorize limited warning maps. Strategic Security will not open strategic charts for curiosity."

Orlen spoke carefully. "We can schedule a map conversation. Lines, missing returns, pressure along the Peligar contact edge. Not a full strategic brief. Enough to show that line moves."

"Moves," Calder repeated.

"Yes," Vorin said. "Your scouts like a target that holds still long enough to measure. The Peligar contact edge does not. You chart it, and by the time the chart is finished, the pressure has already moved. That is not the Neutral Zone between us. That is the line we have been holding against them."

Park kept his mouth on language, where he belonged. "For the map session we need shared terms for *expanding pressure*, *unresolved return*, *warning without order*, and *help request* if that is coming. If you say *help* and we hear *surrender*, or you say *buffer* and we hear *trap*, we will fail the conversation before the charts open."

Senka's eyes narrowed. "Who told you a help request is coming?"

"Nobody," Park said. "The shape of this week suggests someone will have to ask for something costly soon. I am preparing vocabulary so the ask does not arrive in a broken sentence."

Calder did not contradict him. Thessaly marked the terms on the board.

Orlen wrote the Dominion equivalents, then stopped at *help request*. "We may need a different phrase. On our side, asking for aid sounds like surrender to half the room and strategy to the other half. Neither meaning is safe on a shared board."

"Then we write *request for cooperative warning posture*," Thessaly said. "Ugly. Clear. Less performance."

"Acceptable as a working phrase," Senka said. "Not a promise that the request exists."

Rowan accepted that with a silence that meant they could keep planning without pretending they agreed.

They spent the next hour negotiating the next disclosure layer.

Fleet already had the name. Calder made that plain. "Peligar goes up our channels as we need it. Classified, provisional, exonym noted. Dominion does not control Fleet communications."

Senka's jaw set. She did not argue the point she could not win.

What they could still negotiate was the shared board tomorrow: limited Peligar-contact pressure maps under dual veto, signal science under review as observed phenomena only — not a dictionary, not the strategic archive as the price of one chart. No alliance language on the joint channel.

Calder pushed once, carefully. "If the map shows a moving pressure and you will not say what it is processing, Fleet will still have to decide whether to treat the warning as threat."

"Then decide with incomplete information," Senka said. "That is what command is. We will not become your encyclopedia because you sat through one map talk."

"Fair," Calder said. He did not sound soothed.

Park added one more language constraint before Thessaly closed the agenda. "On the shared channel tomorrow, no one says *invasion* unless they can certify observed intent to conquer. No one says *ally* unless both Civil Government and Fleet Council authorize the word. If we need a placeholder, we use *contacting party under pressure*."

Senka studied him. "You are fencing us already."

"I am fencing both of us," Park said. "A bad word starts a war faster than a bad chart."

Orlen wrote the constraint into her notebook without looking at Senka for permission. Vorin glanced at Senka, received a short nod, and mirrored the note on the Dominion slate.

Thessaly converted the political mess into an executable next-day agenda: lexicon lock at start of watch, dual custody of any chart image, spoken confirmation behind every critical header, suspend drill before the first overlay opened, Park and Orlen as language owners, Calder and Senka as veto holders, Rowan and Dominion Civil on relay for escalation only.

"Enough shared words to schedule the map conversation," Thessaly summarized. "Not enough to call this trust."

"Correct," Senka said.

Calder looked at Park. "Are the protocols good enough to carry tomorrow without another software knife fight?"

"If nobody patches alone," Park said. "And if we keep spoken confirmation behind every critical render for the next sessions."

"We keep it," Orlen said. "Vorin and I will not release a table we have not read aloud."

Vorin tapped his slate. "SSD will hate the delay. They can hate it while people stay alive."

Rowan closed his channel with final instructions. "Session Two tomorrow: map conversation under these limits. Captain Venn holds Fleet authority for decisions at the table. Thessaly owns procedure. Park owns lexicon custody with Orlen and Vorin. If the map talk turns into a demand for total disclosure, suspend."

"If Fleet turns the map talk into an interrogation theater, we walk," Senka said.

"Then we understand each other," Rowan said, and the pedestal went dark.

Senka left with her coat already closed. Calder stayed long enough to approve the evening packet, then went to walk the node's outer ring alone.

Park remained with Orlen and Vorin in the language suite to lock the day's glossary.

Sixty-three shared terms. One corrected classification. One near-miss contained. One map session scheduled.

Orlen poured tea for herself and Vorin. Park updated the Fleet classification one last time:

*Speech. Meaning: none. Origin: Peligar (Dominion exonym). Threat: undetermined.*

He sat with that.

"You look as if you wanted a better ending," Vorin said.

"I wanted a cleaner answer," Park said. "Something we could act on tonight."

Orlen cupped her tea. "Clean answers are how people die prepared for the wrong emergency. Preparation honors those who come after us. It does not honor comfort."

Park nodded.

"Tomorrow," Park said.

"Tomorrow," Orlen said.

They shut the boards down. The dual-consent recorder went dark. Vorin sealed the custody sleeves. Orlen finished her tea.

On the Fleet systems, the corrected classification header sat where the provisional unknown had been.

Park sealed his own custody copy last. He did not replay the scream. The corrected header was enough.

The work continued.
