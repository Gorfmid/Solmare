# Chapter 5

## Glass Horizon

---

***KESTREL VEIL — SEGMENT 6 APPROACH***
*FOUNDERS' REACH*

It took a little over two days at maximum speed to reach Founders' Reach. The corridor stayed quiet the whole way — nothing on communications, no other traffic, nothing from the colony, nobody giving chase, no burned-out hulls. Then the approach opened.

Calder stood at the primary plot — and the plot was full.

Not battle wreckage. The colony itself, coming apart.

Dark hulls the size of yards hung over the industrial edge without formation and without haste. One had been turning for some time before anyone realized it was moving at all. Between them, bright threads of metal and stone moved in slow rivers from orbit and ground into open mouths that did not look like weapons. The mouths did not fire. They took.

An orbital station glittered along one edge where something was cutting it clean. A docking ring came apart in sections the size of warehouses and drifted into a stream that already held girder, plate, and what looked like a whole cargo crane, still recognizable. An enormous beam left the station the way cargo left a dock. Pieces moved in orderly lines. No fire. No tumbling kill spray. Just removal.

Below, the colony was still a place with names. Roads. Roofs. A processing shed with its roof intact. A strip of containers that had been a school. Then the display refreshed, and a whole block of the administrative grid lost its metal in one slow pass — walls left standing, cores gone, windows dark. A hauler road that had run beside the shed was suddenly wider because the shed wall that had bordered it was no longer there. Dust rose where something heavy had been lifted. It settled on ground that had been a parking apron an hour ago and was bare dirt now.

Farther out, trial fields still showed green in patches. Closer in, the green ended in scraped rectangles. Marker posts winked out one by one as whatever was working the secondary landmass took the metal and left the soil.

Thessaly's mouth stayed closed. Park's hands were on the console and did not move. Hale did not raise posture. Vex, at the rail where Calder had put him, only watched. The sensors kept updating.

*Kestrel Veil* held at the outer observation arc. Reyes had the approach locked — two passive entries, one clean abort. Tanaka stood at his shoulder with her color checklist and did not mark a drone release. Park sat at sensors. Thessaly held the secondary console. Okwelu stood in the hatch with a kit she did not open. Mehta waited behind her with filters. Orlen held the lexicon cart. Vorin kept the dual-custody feed.

Park zoomed the settlement strip — not the streams, not the dark hulls. The school containers. A yard behind them still held a small painted playhouse, and beside it a doll left face-down in the dirt.

Thessaly saw it. She did not point. She did not speak.

Okwelu did not look away from the display. "Where are the people?"

Nobody answered.

No muzzle flash. No pursuit trails. No emergency beacons. No one running on the surface that the sensors could find.

Calder, not seeing Brenner on the bridge, said, "Brenner, I need you on the bridge now."

"Mass returns on the primary approach," Park said. "Not ship-class profiles we train against. Large. Multiple. Holding in patterns that are not fleet formation. Orbital infrastructure — incomplete and changing. Atmospheric envelope altered. No challenge traffic. No distress bands. No battle illumination."

"Survivors," Okwelu said.

"None answering," Park said. "Relay mast signature intermittent — then gone. Administrative ping dead. Emergency override from New Meridian still unanswered."

Brenner arrived. "Yes sir?"

Calder pointed at the display.

Brenner studied it. A long minute. He rubbed a hand through his hair once.

"That's not demolition."

The bridge waited.

"Captain..."

He looked again.

"They're harvesting it."

Calder waited another thirty seconds. Someone behind him shifted weight.

Reyes checked the abort corridor again. Tanaka's stylus hovered over her checklist and did not touch down. Hale's hands stayed flat on the tactical rail. Okwelu's kit remained closed. Mehta watched the atmospheric strip. Brenner stayed at the rail.

Park rotated a sensor model once. Then again. Then a third time, slower.

On the plot, an orbital station that had been whole when they jumped into the corridor was no longer whole. A docking arm thinned while they watched — coming apart along clean lines. Another section simply was not there on the next refresh. Material moved away from it in a stream.

"They're still there," Park said. "Active. Not residual."

*Kestrel* slid deeper along the observation arc under passive preference. The G-type sun looked ordinary.

Huge dark hulls held station over the industrial edge — not warship profiles. They were attached to what remained of colony infrastructure, but nothing was being repaired. Matter streams ran from surface and orbit into those hulls. No visible weapons posture toward *Kestrel Veil*.

Park frowned.

"Captain..."

Everyone looked.

Nothing appeared to have happened.

Thirty seconds passed.

Then someone realized one of the immense dark hulls had shifted position — twenty or thirty meters from where it had been. No thrusters. No reaction mass. No engine flare. No visible propulsion. No one could say exactly when it had moved.

Hale spoke from the tactical rail. "No strike pattern. No saturation. No pursuit track on us. If they see us, they do not care."

"They see us," Vex said.

Calder looked at him. "Your people say they leave observers alone if the observers do not interfere."

Vex held his eyes. "That is the theory."

"We are going to test it," Calder said. "Passive only. No challenge. No heroics. Run as quiet and dark as possible. If they come for us anyway, abort corridor and we run."

Vex nodded once. "Do not illuminate. Do not challenge. They did not come for you."

He pointed once at a thinning corridor on the secondary plot — Fleet had logged it as incomplete debris. Dominion overlay, when Vorin laid it in, showed the same corridor as an active extraction path.

"Processing," Vex said.

Reyes tapped the abort corridor once. Tanaka still did not mark a drone release.

"Any readable content on that signal?" Thessaly asked.

"Multi-band return present," Park said. "Same family as *Graylane*. Louder than residual. No readable content. I still do not know what it is."

He did not play the audio. Nobody asked him to.

On the primary landmass display, a section of the administrative grid still showed buildings. Concrete. Roads. Then a strip of docking hardware at the edge of the settlement simply ceased to exist as useful metal — walls left, cores gone, the road beneath suddenly more visible because the structure that had cast its shadow was no longer casting one.

"This isn't a battle," Park said. "My training prepared me for raids. This is not a raid."

"We are watching a process," Calder said. "Live. Keep logging. Stay passive. Do not go active."

A Helion staff note arrived from Rowan on communications: *Confirm whether Founders' Reach remains recoverable under emergency logistics.*

Calder read it once.

"No," he said. "No recovery possible. Do not send additional assets."

Park sent it.

Thessaly walked the bridge once. She paused at Mehta long enough for Mehta to meet her eyes and nod. She paused at Hale long enough to say, "Anger later. Hands now." She paused at Park and did not say anything. She glanced once at Vex. His tea sat untouched. She left him alone.

Calder remained at the primary plot. He thought of Cade's morning packet: weather nominal, processing output steady, no elevated medical. The plot in front of him was that civilization being taken apart.

*Kestrel* held.

On the plot, another section of the orbital station simply ceased — girder and plate sliding into a waiting stream.

***KESTREL VEIL — OBSERVATION ARC***
*ACTIVE CONSUMPTION*

They did not land.

They watched.

Hours. Watch rotations. Docks thinning. Sheds thinning. The roads left behind when the metal beside them was gone. Thessaly kept the bridge on stages so nobody started a fight *Kestrel* would not survive.

Park rotated the model and stopped talking to anyone but the data. An orbital shed still held its full outline at the start of the hour — panels, strut lines, a docking collar Park had marked in yellow because it matched a Founders' Reach maintenance sketch from Cade's last packet. By the end of the hour the collar was gone. Half the panels were gone. The struts that remained connected to nothing useful. Material moved in slow streams into the dark hulls.

Orlen watched the same feed over Park's shoulder without touching the console.

"Contact Node Seven asks whether we have visual confirmation of units remaining," Orlen said on the net.

"Confirm," Park said. "They are still there. More than one. They are working — taking the system apart."

"Acknowledged," Orlen said.

Vex stood at the rail and did not sit. He pointed at a mid-orbit return Park had still marked as incomplete debris.

"That is a docking section," Vex said. "It is leaving."

Park checked. Confirmed. The docking section thinned while the bridge watched — plate by plate, then the arm, then the collar that had held ships.

Hale exhaled through his teeth. "I want someone to shoot at."

"They will not shoot at us," Vex said.

Reyes checked the abort corridor twice. Tanaka whispered, "Not yet," and kept the stylus off the checklist. Okwelu set a hand on Mehta's shoulder once, then took it away. Brenner watched the docking collar vanish and muttered, "That used to hold ships."

Park shifted the model down to mid-orbit. That was where the colony's industrial sheds should have been. That was where the relay mast should have answered.

The sheds were stripped. Useful metal moved into the streams. Empty framing left behind. The relay mast — the one Park had been mid-scheduling a visit through, the one Cade had used for morning packets — was gone as a structure before the second watch ended. Park watched the last strut leave the plot and then sat very still.

"The relay wasn't destroyed," Park said. "It was removed. We watched it leave."

Reyes exhaled once through his nose. Tanaka set her checklist down. "No landing. Not while that is still running." Hale's jaw set. Vorin authenticated the live feed — stamp, timestamp, dual custody. Orlen poured hot water into her thermos. Thessaly walked once behind the chairs and did not speak until people were working again.

On the surface strip, the administrative grid still showed concrete and roads for a long time after the metal left. Buildings stayed standing until they were taken apart in order — cores first, then fixtures, then anything else worth carrying. A road became wider as the walls beside it vanished. An irrigation line on the secondary landmass went dark on the plot. Then the metal markers vanished from the trial grids one by one. Calder watched plot three — Reachroot, open soil, the shoots Cade had crouched over — lose its markers and then its green. He did not look away until the rectangle was bare dirt.

"Hold," Calder said. "We watch. We do not land."

Thessaly repeated it for the net.

Park had run heat. Movement. Emergency bands. Personal beacons. Administrative pings. Nothing answered. No transport signatures. No mass evacuation pattern. Nothing moved on the roads.

A junior officer on New Meridian's ops loop asked whether Founders' Reach was still under attack.

Calder took that one himself.

"No," he said. "There is no fight. There is a process still running. We are here to document it."

He looked at Vex once. Vex did not ask for weapons free. His hands stayed on the rail.

"Commander," Calder said, low.

"Captain."

"Don't brief us yet. Let us see it."

Vex nodded once. "Fair."

Later — after the dark hulls had finished with the settlement core and moved their work toward the outer industrial scrap and the secondary landmass — Vex said, without looking away from the plot: "Settlement clear enough. Residual risk. Not zero."

"Abort corridor," Calder said.

"Clean," Tanaka said. "Short window if we go now."

"Limited landing," Calder said. "Primary settlement only. Drones first. People last. Abort if those hulls turn back toward the core."

"Aye," Thessaly said.

Okwelu picked up her kit. Mehta sealed the filter crate and checked the open time twice. Hale set his jaw at the tactical rail and stayed there.

---
***FOUNDERS' REACH — SURFACE***
*PRIMARY SETTLEMENT — AFTER THE HULLS MOVE ON*

The shuttle set down near the administrative grid.

Calder went with Okwelu and Mehta. Thessaly remained aboard *Kestrel*. Park stayed on sensors — still tracking the dark hulls on the outer edge. Hale kept the tactical chair. Vex stayed on the bridge rail.

Dust rose around the shuttle skids and settled again. Overhead, the dark hulls were still working the outer edge.

Calder stepped down onto the dirt road. Packed. Still usable.

The administrative relay hut was gone. Foundations remained. Utility stubs. A path worn by feet.

Calder stood at the empty footing where the status display had been.

"Calder," Thessaly said over the open channel.

"Moving," he said.

He thought of Cade's last packet once. Weather nominal. Processing output steady. No elevated medical. The colony visit he had been planning in two days. The day log entry he had marked *superseded*.

Then he moved.

The processing shed matched what they had watched from orbit: valuable stock removed, framing left. Conveyor mounts stood empty. The smell was dust and scraped metal and something faintly chemical. Mehta took a sample and did not speak.

A work roster was still clipped to a board on the wall. Names. Shift times. The last entry was signed and dated three weeks before *Kestrel* arrived. Nothing had been crossed out.

Park opened the channel from orbit.

"I don't know what to put in the first line."

Okwelu looked at the hut footprint. "What do we tell their families?"

Park's voice came back thinner. "I don't know."

They moved through what remained of the settlement grid.

The dirt road continued past the shed line toward the domestic cluster. Packed earth. Wheel ruts from a hauler. Ground hauler tracks ended in scraped soil. Partial walls. Missing utility cores.

A mailbox still stood at one lot, dented, empty, the nameplate half-scratched by weather. Calder put a hand on the post once and took it away.

An abandoned garden ran along the south side of a missing wall. Tomato stakes still upright. One vine dried brown on the wire. A watering can sat upright beside the row, half full of rainwater. Tools laid neat beside a half-finished fence post. Three. All metal. Left behind. He logged them for Park and moved on.

A broken coffee mug lay in the dust near a doorstep that no longer had a door. Calder stepped around it.

Inside one partial shell, a table remained. Two chairs. A third chair missing. A slate case without the slate. A coat hook with nothing on it. Beside the table, a high chair. Food still in the tray. Not cleaned. Not put away. In the kitchen corner, a magnetic board still held a grocery list in someone's handwriting. Two items circled. Neither crossed off.

Okwelu paused in the doorway and did not go further. Mehta went in far enough to confirm the utility core was gone, then came back out and wiped her hands on her trousers.

They came to the school.

The school had been made from shipping containers. Temporary on the paperwork. Still in use after years. The school lead had asked for real walls.

What remained was a rectangle of disturbed ground and one corner post that had not been worth taking.

Children's play markings survived in a patch of packed dirt nearby: a chalk orbit diagram half erased by weather, a hop grid still faintly visible. Beside the hop grid, a child's drawing on a scrap of plastic — sun, landmass, stick figures — pinned under a stone. The stone was still there.

Calder stepped into the school rectangle.

Near the post, a chalk stub lay in a crack in the packed earth — used down to a nub. He picked it up. Turned it once. Set it back where it had been.

Okwelu found a small wooden chair tipped on its side near the rectangle's edge — too small for an adult, scuffed on one leg. She righted it and left it there.

Mehta stayed at the rectangle's edge.

"No biological residual consistent with violence," she said. "Nothing I can certify."

Calder did not order her forward. Thessaly, listening from orbit, did not either.

He stepped out.

Mehta found a sealed cargo crate in a debris shadow near the old shed line. She opened it under Thessaly's remote procedure and then sat down hard in the dust.

Inside: Founders' Week commemorative kits. Banners. Children's sticker sheets with a sun and a small green landmass. A script for a community reading that ended with a joke about marble schools arriving someday.

Mehta held one sticker sheet between her fingers until Okwelu took it and put it back.

Calder looked once, then had the crate sealed for custody transfer to New Meridian's civilian affairs office.

"Log the kits as cargo," Thessaly said over the open channel. "Nothing else."

"Aye," Mehta said.

They took a second shuttle hop to the secondary landmass where the agricultural trials had been — only after Park confirmed the dark hulls had moved past that landmass into the outer industrial scrap.

From the air the trial grids still showed as neat rectangles. On the ground those rectangles were thinner: soil turned, moisture changed, marker posts missing. The windbreak still leaned. Irrigation timers sat dark in their housings. One hose lay coiled beside a half-finished row. Near the irrigation head at the end of row two, a kneeling mat lay flat in the soil.

Plot three — Reachroot — was a scraped rectangle. Green gone. Shape remaining.

Mehta sampled. Okwelu checked for medical residual. She found nothing. Calder kept his eyes on the causeway back toward the settlement core.

"Tell New Meridian," Calder said, then stopped. He wrote numbers instead.

Hale came through on the net. "I still want something to shoot at."

"Nothing to shoot at," Park said from orbit. "Those hulls are still working the outer edge. Stay on the plot."

"I know."

Okwelu found a medical locker frame with the contents gone and the frame left behind. "No medications," she said. "Not even basics." She logged it. Mehta sampled soil where the trial markers had been.

Calder kept the team moving on a grid Thessaly had drawn before landing: hut footprint, shed line, school rectangle, domestic cluster, then up.

Calder stood in the hut footprint one last time before returning to *Kestrel*. Far out on the plot, the dark hulls were still taking the outer edge apart.

"Captain," Mehta said. "The samples are clean."

"Keep sampling," Calder said.

Okwelu sealed her last kit and looked toward the school rectangle. "Three years for a school building. They taught anyway."

"They did," Calder said.

The shuttle left the dirt.

Beneath them, the primary settlement stayed quiet. Farther out, the dark hulls were still taking the outer edge apart.

***KESTREL VEIL — BRIDGE / READY ROOM***
*AFTER GROUND PASS*

Thessaly met him in the small ready room off the bridge before he could wash the dust off his boots. She had saved him some coffee. It had gone lukewarm in the wait.

"Mehta stayed on her feet," she said, not looking up from the post-mission notes. "Hale wanted to hit something. Park is already talking about industrial ecology."

"And you?"

"I kept the ship pointed at the work." She looked up. "You went into the school rectangle. Your notes say twice."

"Corner post and dirt. Nothing else." He drank the coffee. "Thank you for this."

"Drink it and go talk to Vex." A small, tired almost-smile. "I'll be right behind you."

On the bridge, Park was still at sensors. The orbital model was still rotating under his hand.

Cole's empty station sat to his left — Interface work now, not *Kestrel*. He glanced at it once and went back to the model.

Orlen stood at the lexicon cart with her thermos.

"Work first," she said.

"Work first," Park said.

Vorin authenticated what the ground team had brought up — stamp, timestamp, nothing else.

Reyes and Tanaka were still running the abort corridor. Hale had not left the tactical chair. Okwelu had already pulled Mehta aside for sixty seconds in the passageway and sent her back with water and no speech.

Vex was still at the rail.

Calder joined him there. Outside the viewport, Founders' Reach turned under ordinary light. Sorted orbitals. Scraped settlement core. Dark hulls still taking the outer edge apart.

For a long time neither man spoke.

Vex had fought many battles. He had seen what was left of other colonies after the Peligar finished with them. He had killed a few of their ships. On the *Iron Harrow*, that was the job — arrive, fire, make them pay.

This time he had watched a colony leave in real time — and done nothing.

His hands stayed on the rail.

"What you watched matches our older watches," Vex said at last. "They take what they need and move on. Your colony was not selected for insult. It was available on the path."

A pause.

"I have never watched it live," Vex said. "I don't think anyone has. Only after. Arriving to scrap. This — standing here, unable to fire — is worse."

Calder kept his eyes on the viewport. "Tell us everything we need to prepare. Ships. Weapons. What we need to fight back."

Vex inclined his head once. "You already saw what they do. I can tell you how to hurt their ships — a little. What has worked. What has not. I will not give you our fleet positions, or what sits behind our line."

Park, from his console, without looking up: "Hurting their ships does not bring anyone back."

Vex looked at him. "No. It does not. My grandmother told my class that a flood does not hate the valley. The valley still drowns. Hatred is not required. That is the part your Council will fight longest."

He reached for the tea he had barely touched.

"It is not a one-off," Vex said. "Founders' Reach is one system. The frontier does not stop because Fleet has finally watched one."

A younger Fleet officer on the channel said, "Why. Why do this."

Nobody answered him.

Rowan came through thinner than usual. "Helion will ask whether Dominion is using our dead colony to force dependence."

"Helion can ask," Senka said from her channel. "The colony does not become less gone because the question is politically convenient."

"You will be asked whether you trust us now," Vex said.

"I will be asked whether we need you now," Calder said. "Those are still different questions. I can still answer the second."

Vex nodded once. "Necessity without trust is an ugly operating condition."

"It is the one we have," Calder said.

"Helion will hate what we tell them," Calder said.

"I do not need them to like it," Vex said. "I need them ready for the next system. This path does not stop here."

A pause.

"Old Commander Petran told my watch class that useful enemies are still enemies. He did not tell us how to stand still while a system goes because your guns would only make it worse. I am improvising."

Thessaly closed the brief. Tea on one side, coffee on the other.

Park sealed the shared feed. Orlen mirrored it.

***KESTREL VEIL — COMMAND BRIEFING NOOK***
*UP-CHAIN*

Calder, Thessaly, and Park sat with Helion on the channel. Rowan stayed quiet until the end.

"Tell them what we saw," Calder said. "Not what they want to hear."

Helion asked for body counts. Calder refused. Prior census was not a body count. They had watched the colony leave. They would not invent numbers.

Helion wanted someone to blame. Park put the debris pattern back on the display and said nothing until the channel went quiet.

What went up-chain was short:

They had watched dark hulls take the settlement apart.
People were gone. Not dead that anyone could prove — just gone. No answer why.
Dominion said the pattern was old.
Other quiet systems on the same arc needed watching.
Shore leave on Segment 6 was already being cancelled.

"That will light every panic fuse in Helion," Rowan said.

"Good," Thessaly said.

Park mentioned the Founders' Week kits still in custody. Calder told him to leave them with civilian affairs — memorial work, separate from the report.

Thessaly watched Calder. "If Helion starts rewriting what we saw, I run the ship and the crew. You decide what we send back."

"If their orders fight what the plot shows, we stay with the plot," Calder said.

The report left *Kestrel*. Orlen and Vorin took their copy. Senka did not gloat. Rowan cleared the gates.

"Council will call an emergency session," Rowan said. "Doctrine will call three. FIB will call the rest."

"We were not ready when the colony was attacked," Calder said. "We were not ready when we watched it leave. We do the work anyway."

***NEW MERIDIAN — CIVILIAN AFFAIRS / KESTREL MESS***
*ORDINARY LIFE*

On New Meridian, a clerk who had queued Founders' Reach petition reminders the week before opened a memorial logistics ticket and stared at the commemorative kit divert order until her supervisor took the slate out of her hands and did the entry herself.

Leave cancellations posted on Segment boards: *rotation holds indefinite; personal travel deferred; contact family affairs for hardship exceptions*.

A teacher exchange that had been scheduled to visit the container school received a cancellation with no replacement date. The lead teacher read it twice, then told her class they would practice fractions anyway.

On a freight apron, wall panels and floor decking for a new schoolhouse sat under a delivery tag still addressed to Founders' Reach. The load had been scheduled for next week. The school lead had waited three years for real walls. Nobody moved the crates.

Freight holds stacked on holiday inventory that no longer had a destination. Two freight hands argued quietly about whether holding Founders' Week banners counted as respect or as superstition. Both kept working. A woman from agricultural liaison stood at a window and said the trial seed manifests for next season were still in her queue.

Families on Segment 6 rotations waited for messages. A husband refreshed a personal channel three times and then put the slate face-down. A mother packed away Founders' Week decorations she had bought early. In a small apartment above a freight office, an older man who had cousins on Founders' Reach sat with a cold cup and did not turn on the news.

A junior clerk in memorial logistics opened the population file and closed it again without generating a count.

Aboard *Kestrel*, the mess stayed ordinary. Coffee on the urn. Food that tasted like ship food. Someone told a maintenance joke. Someone laughed. Thessaly said, "Eat. Then back to stations."

She set a hand on Hale's shoulder for half a second and took it away. She told Mehta to sit. She told Solis the access map could wait until after the meal.

Kevin came through with a thermos and left it within reach of Park's hand without asking. Park nodded once. Kevin was already gone.

Tanaka finally put her checklist away. "I was supposed to be on ground leave next month. Family already bought the extra bed out."

"Write them," Calder said. "Tell them leave is cancelled. Tell them a colony was attacked, and we watched what was left of it leave."

"And the rest."

"That the border problem is larger than one system. That the work did not end at Founders' Reach."

She nodded and kept working.

Park sat with his sampling summaries and a sandwich he was not finishing.

Hale poked at his ration tray. "I keep wanting someone to shoot at."

"Those hulls are still on the outer edge," Park said. "Stay on the plot."

Outside the mess viewports, Segment traffic continued. Freighters. Patrol craft.

Founders' Week kits waited in custody.

***KESTREL VEIL — EXECUTIVE QUARTERS***
*XO BOOK*

Thessaly locked the cabin door and took the cloth-bound notebook from the recess in the bulkhead.

She opened it past the older hands. Past her own Incident page. Past Alpha Seven. Below the last line she had written, she uncapped her pen.

*Founders' Reach.*

*We watched a colony die today.*

*We stood here and let it happen. There was nothing we could do.*

*— Thessaly, XO*

She closed the book, put it back, and returned to the deck.

***KESTREL VEIL — BRIDGE***
*SECOND ALERT*

Helion took the report at 27:40 ship time. Calder was still on the bridge. Thessaly was still running the watch.

Rowan's confirmation came through first. "Helion has it. The arguments have started. They will want ships, answers, and someone to blame. Stay on watch."

"Acknowledged," Calder said.

He looked once at Thessaly. She was already splitting the bridge: Founders' Reach closed enough to report, outer corridor open, nothing mixed.

Park's console lit a second time.

"Outer-corridor alert," Park said. "Not Founders' Reach. Next system on the Segment 6 outer arc. Settlement beacons weak or missing. Dominion says the frontier is still moving. We cannot yet tell if the hulls have started work or only arrived."

Thessaly was already moving. "Keep it separate from Founders' Reach. Get confirmation."

Calder looked at the dual plot: one system they had watched leave in real time, another flickering at the edge of the same path.

"What is it," Calder said.

"Orchard Compact," Park said. "Small agricultural annex. Same class of place people ignore until something goes wrong."

"Confirm before we call Helion," Thessaly said.

Vex's voice came from the rail.

"Our adjacent watch shows the same. The frontier is still moving. Founders' Reach was not the end."

Calder looked at the second plot. "We're already on it."

"Helion will want you fighting before you know what this new alert is."

"I know."

On *Kestrel*'s bridge, the crew held stations. Park kept the new alert on a separate strip. Okwelu began packing sampling kits. Mehta did not open her filter crate. Hale watched the second plot. Reyes updated the abort corridor. Tanaka marked nothing and waited.

Calder stood at the plot.

"Acknowledge up-chain," he said. "Founders' Reach report delivered. Second outer-corridor alert received. We are watching. Awaiting guidance."

"Aye," Park said.

Outside, the G-type sun kept shining. Farther along the arc, another quiet place waited.
