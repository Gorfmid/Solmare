# Chapter 1

## Silent Frequencies

---

***IRON HARROW — VOLUME V17 / ALPHA SEVEN***
*V17 — AFTER THE EXCHANGE*

The silhouette contact held its visible bearing a little longer than courtesy required — weapons dark, traffic still rerouted along the corridor they had cleared, header exchange complete and intent unresolved. Then the mysterious scout began to leave.

Vex watched it go.

On the plot it was a small, ordinary withdrawal: using what appeared to be secondary thrusters, corridor discipline, no illumination, no second speech. The relay they had left burning behind them stayed live like a question neither side had agreed how to answer. Vex had ordered Standing Observation. Elenko had been told not to touch that link. For a stretch of quiet that felt longer than the clock allowed, there was nothing to do but watch a foreign hull fall back toward whatever map had made it.

They had not talked. They had exchanged messages — identification headers, navigation state, acknowledgment — in a stack that parsed on a Dominion command board as if both sides were running the same architecture at different ages. An older variant meeting a current one. Compatible enough to complete the exchange. Incompatible enough that neither side could pretend it was speech. No one had expected that. Not Strategic Security Directorate — SSD. Not Civil Government. Not Vex. And yet somehow these beings were using systems close enough to theirs that an exchange of basic system information had worked. That was a fact that would not sit still in him. SSD would call the contact contamination. Civil Government would call it opportunity. Neither word was large enough for what had actually happened. He wondered what would come of it. Whether the strangers would treat the relay as a door or a trap. Whether the next meeting would be another set of headers or a demand.

He was still on that thought — the useless, human kind of thought that doctrine did not budget for — when the corridor failed in a quieter way than Klimov's board knew how to announce.

Nothing on the plot. No mass return. No approach vector. The cloak envelope on *Iron Harrow* was holding post-exchange — rebuilt, imperfect, better than the trial night — and still it gave them no warning.

It happened in rapid succession. Something had come in cloaked — better than anything the Dominion had, by far. Then its field dropped. Suddenly there was another ship.

"Contact," Klimov said, and this time the voice did convey what the plot was showing.

Not the silhouette. Larger. Ambiguous mass curve, shifting, occupying more bearing arc than a consistent hull should — and Vex had the recognition before the archive finished drawing. He had seen that profile years ago, on a different border, in a different decade. Not here. Not in V17. Not this close to a corridor where strangers had just finished trading headers.

"Peligar," Petrov said.

The designation was already on the board. Known comparison class. Correct. And still out of place on this map.

Vex did not flinch at the designation. What changed the room was the map — Peligar this deep into V17 — and the Leviathan under his feet. Near sixty thousand personnel were distributed through decks and sealed routes the bridge would never see this watch. Command was only a small room at the front of that mass.

Closing already. Inside any honest engagement window. No identification. No challenge response. No navigation state.

Petrov spoke before the mass return settled. "They were cloaked. Detection path unknown — we do not know how they acquired us under envelope. They dropped the field to fire. Same hard rule we live under: you cannot discharge and keep the envelope. They chose the shot over the hide."

"Weapons live," Vex said. "Shields to full combat load. Hold our cloak as long as it still spoils their track — not all of that will land clean. Drevin, I want a lock ready. When we fire, the envelope dies. Not before."

"Working," Drevin said. "It will not hold still."

The first salvo was everything the contact had brought to the corridor. Not a probe. Not a warning. One motion: field down, weapons up, fire.

The envelope on *Iron Harrow* still held. Imperfect. It shimmered under the hits, and it cost the contact something. Some packages went wide. Some struck soft and failed to punch through. Some lost track in the last meters and spent themselves on empty space. Cloak was not a wall. It was enough interference that a clean kill solution would not hold.

Enough still landed — enough to mark the hull, spoil the envelope further, and give the contact a cleaner track for whatever came next.

Hull plating folded. Frames cracked. Whole sections sealed in sequence as compartments failed behind them. Vex knew the pattern before the damage circuit caught up: distributed failure, not a precision kill. He had watched compartments go quiet that way before. Different ship. Different year. Same math. People were already dying behind doors that would not open again this watch.

"Sections Nine through Fourteen," the damage circuit said, and then had to restart — more sections failing faster than it could name.

"Evasive," Vex said. "Off this bearing. No illumination. Petrov — full emissions, record this encounter. If we live, someone will need the proof."

Drevin got a lock. The first package left the rails, and *Iron Harrow*'s cloak envelope finished with it. No exceptions.

Plasma and missiles went out. Most of it did little. The contact took hits without a shield matrix anyone recognized — mass and a leading edge that kept working. Drevin lost lock twice, got it a third time, and put real damage into that leading edge. A bright wound. Still one mass. Still no clean vessel count.

"Confirm," Vex said.

"Damage on their leading edge," Petrov said, voice flat. "Not fatal. Not classed. They felt it."

The second wave of hits took Sections that had already been amber and made them black. Accountability reports came back incomplete, then stopped coming from whole frames. Thousands dead or sealed behind doors that would not open. Names he would read later, if there was a later. Before the engagement the complement had stood near sixty thousand. He did not have an exact loss — only sections that would not answer, and a hull he still meant to bring home with whoever remained. Vex kept the ship moving. Survival was the objective. Everything else was noise.

"Send on their relay," he said to Elenko. The corridor link the Fleet scout had left was still live. "One word."

Elenko sent: *Peligar.*

Not a classification. Not a plea. The designation, pushed onto the only line Fleet could still hear.

"All batteries," Vex said. "Everything you have. Now. Then cloak — rebuild if it will take — and maximum thrust. Full speed. We make a mess and we leave. Hope the chaos covers us. Hope we hurt them enough that they do not chase."

Drevin dumped everything still on the rails into the wounded leading edge. Then the cloak came back in stuttering fractions, imperfect, and *Iron Harrow* drove for speed.

Incoming fire from the contact. Behind them. It missed in the wake of the *Harrow* and streamed off into empty space.

The mass return stuttered. Changed profile. Held. Did not close.

Iron Harrow fell off their bearing under cloak, shields bleeding, compartments screaming. The contact did not follow.

Ahead: emergency medicine, emergency power, and the dead. Sections that would never answer again. Vex stood at the plot and did not lean. He was not afraid of what they had hit. He understood what it meant that it had found them here. The cost was already countable. The worse part was not the count. It was the map. Peligar had no business this far into V17 — not after this long quiet in the region. Someone on the damage circuit asked if they should log the engagement as a victory because the contact had broken pursuit.

"Log observed," Vex said. "No. We survived. That is not the same as victory."

He did not add the rest. SSD would get it in the packet anyway: survival report, and a boundary warning.

***EIGHTEEN MONTHS LATER***
***IRON HARROW — VOLUME V21, OUTER REACH***
*ENGAGEMENT WATCH*

The encounter began without a challenge.

Vex was reading damage assessments from V17 when Klimov's board lit in the orange band and Klimov said, "Contact," in the same voice he used for maintenance warnings and vessel traffic. The voice did not convey what the plot was showing. Vex closed the slate before the tone finished. Orange band, after V17, meant he already knew which archive drawer this might open.

V17 was no longer a live fight. The compartments that had sealed that night had never unsealed. The yard had spent most of a year putting the Leviathan back together, and unfinished work still lived in the seams. Civil Government still called him a hero for bringing her home. Many thousands had not come with it.

On the plot: the same shifting mass curve that had opened V17.

It came in on the fringe bearing. No headers. No navigation broadcast. Not closing like a ship making ordinary transit. Just moving through, and the mass curve kept shifting the way Peligar curves always did. Sensors were fine. They already knew the family.

"No challenge response," Klimov said. "No navigation state on any broadcast channel."

"Distance?"

"Closing. Slowly." A pause. "Commander, the mass return is changing profile."

"I know," Vex said. He stood from the command chair. Not V17's opening fire. Quieter. Still them. Still Peligar.

Major Petrov had moved to the secondary plot without being asked. He worked the archive compare, quiet and exact, putting on the record what the room already knew.

"Drevin," Vex said. "Weapons live. Hold fire."

"Live, Commander. No lock." Drevin's tone was flat. He was working the problem. "The return will not hold still enough for a targeting solution."

"Continue attempting. Log each attempt."

"Logging."

The contact came within thirty thousand kilometers before the first effect reached *Iron Harrow*'s hull. It was not an impact. Section Four reported a distributed pressure event across a forty-meter span of exterior surface: hull plating compressed inward over the full length of the section, uniformly, as if something very large had passed close and pressed without targeting. Not a beam. Not a kinetic round. Pressure, across an area too wide to be a weapon.

"Section Four," the damage circuit reported. "Exterior surface deformation. Amber status. No breach."

"Seal Section Four," Vex said. "Engineering on the loop. Full structural lock."

"Sealing."

The contact had not adjusted bearing to fire. Had not illuminated weapons in any spectrum Klimov could identify. Had continued on its original track, the same track it had been holding when Klimov first called contact, as if *Iron Harrow* were terrain.

"I cannot determine whether this is one contact or multiple in close proximity," Klimov said. "The return profile is inconsistent. One pass reads it as a single mass. The next reads it as distributed mass. It is not behaving like a single hull."

"Log it as stated," Vex said. "Note both possibilities. Continue track."

Petrov had the archive comparison running. He turned toward Vex without speaking, which was how Petrov announced the packet was ready for the record.

"What do you have," Vex said. He already had the answer in outline. He wanted Petrov's version on the record.

"V17 contact, eighteen months ago. V18 contact, two years before that." Petrov aligned the archive entries beside the current return on the secondary plot. The mass curve shapes failed in the same specific way: ambiguous, shifting, occupying more bearing arc than a consistent hull should. "Same family. Similarity notation for the packet. The return characteristics are consistent across all three events."

"Total archive entries with these characteristics?"

Petrov worked for a moment. "Twenty-one entries. The oldest is eleven years."

The second pressure event reached Section Seven. Same character: distributed, uniform, extended across a span that no precision weapon would occupy. Hull plating compressed along the outer surface, not reaching the structural layer.

"Section Seven, structural warning," the damage circuit said. "Margin at eighty-six percent nominal. Holding."

"Seal Seven. Structural lock full. Section Seven crew accountability, forward of frame thirty."

The damage circuit acknowledged.

The accountability report came back in under a minute: full crew forward, no personnel in the compromised section.

Vex watched the bearing trace on the main plot. The contact had not changed course. Not to engage, not to evade, not to orient toward *Iron Harrow*'s weapons or command spaces or propulsion. In the time it had taken to absorb hull contact twice, ready weapons, begin damage control, and work a sensor return that would not stabilize, the contact had been on one line.

It was going somewhere. *Iron Harrow* was in the path.

The contact had not been looking for a fight. Had not, as far as his sensors could determine, looked at *Iron Harrow* at all. The pressure on the hull was not a weapon. It was proximity: something large passing close enough to press. V17 had been a fight. This was the other behavior, and it was still them, still too far into V21 for comfort.

"Drevin," Vex said. "Prepare evasive maneuvering. Off this bearing. Minimum signature. No weapons discharge."

Drevin said, "Commander," and then started the maneuvering calculation. He knew the difference between an objection worth pressing and a decision already made.

"Petrov," Vex said. "Audio of the contact's emission signature. Pipe it low."

Petrov reached for the signals panel, then paused with his hand on the control. He looked at Vex.

"Low," Vex confirmed.

Petrov opened the audio render.

The bridge held through three seconds of it. Four. Five.

Drevin's hands stopped on the maneuvering board.

"Close it," Vex said.

Petrov closed the channel.

Silence.

Drevin's hands resumed the maneuvering calculation after a moment. He did not ask about the sound. He looked at the plot and worked.

*Iron Harrow* came off the contact's bearing in a slow arc, minimum signature, no weapons discharge. The Peligar return did not adjust. Its bearing trace continued past the space the Leviathan had vacated and kept going, still cycling through that ambiguous profile that would not confirm a vessel count, still on its original heading.

The mass return drifted past on the plot.

It did not turn. It did not fire. It continued.

"Hold the track," Vex said. "No illumination. Full emissions profile recorded at maximum resolution. Audio intercept recorded from initial contact. Confirm duration."

"Thirty-one seconds captured before we came off bearing," Petrov said. "Recording complete."

"Hold it until it drops below sensor threshold."

"Aye, Commander."

Vex stood at the main plot and watched the return drift away. In twenty-two minutes it fell below the sensor edge and the bearing was clean.

"Contact lost below threshold," Klimov said. "Bearing clear."

The damage circuit had Section Four at amber stable and Section Seven at amber holding. No breach. Full crew accountability confirmed. No casualties.

Vex looked at the twenty-one archive entries on Petrov's secondary plot and the two new ones they had just added.

Twenty-three contacts across eleven years across three volumes.

Eleven years of reports routed to V21 command and Strategic Security. Somewhere in the early entries someone had attributed the anomalous returns to instrument error or unknown vessel types and moved on. Somewhere around the seventh or eighth entry someone had started a pattern notation that grew slowly into a recognizable shape.

He had read the full archive once, when he took command of *Iron Harrow* and asked to be briefed on the volume's unresolved contacts. Then he had asked Petrov what the strategic guidance said. Petrov told him: hold the watch, survive if engaged, log everything, do not chase a fight you do not need, and do not put the archive on Fleet channels without Strategic Security clearance.

He had accepted that guidance then.

He was looking at the twenty-third entry now. Same family. Different behavior from V17. Same problem on the map: they were still operating past the quiet he had been taught to expect here.

"Log everything," Vex said. "Full detail. Return characteristics, emissions profile, audio intercept, hull damage report, bearing trace from initial contact to sensor loss. V21 command and Strategic Security, both copies urgent-flagged."

"Classification?" Klimov asked.

"Peligar," Vex said. "Comparison class. Note transit behavior, not engagement. Similarity to prior archive returns. Log observed under that heading."

Klimov logged it.

***IRON HARROW — BRIEFING COMPARTMENT***
*POST-CONTACT ASSESSMENT*

Petrov had both hands flat on the table.

Lieutenant Sorova from Engineering gave the structural summary first, precise, without editorial. Sections Four and Seven: exterior deformation, hull plating, no penetration into the structural layer. Repair category three. Four days at transit speed with materials on hand. Section Seven's structural margin stood fourteen percent below nominal, within operational parameters. Another contact before repair would cut it further.

"What margin would require us to break observation posture?" Vex asked.

Sorova ran the numbers without being asked for elaboration. "If Section Seven drops below sixty-five percent before repair, I would recommend anchor access for proper work. At the current damage state, we are well above that."

"Then we hold posture," Vex said. "Repair continues on station."

"Yes, Commander."

Sorova left the briefing compartment. The table held three people now: Vex, Petrov, Drevin. The tea on the wall bracket had been sitting since before the contact. Nobody had moved to reach for it.

"Twenty-three entries," Drevin said.

"Twenty-three documented contacts with matching or similar characteristics," Petrov said. "The early entries are thin: mass curve notation, emissions anomaly, no damage report, no audio intercept. Some of them were probably logged under general anomaly and cross-referenced to this file by the archive analysts years after the fact. The number of actual contacts in eleven years is likely higher."

"And Strategic Security has all twenty-three."

"Yes."

Drevin studied the archive summary on the table display. The entries spanned V17, V18, and V21 across eleven years. The earliest ones were two lines. The most recent entries, the last six or seven, were dense with technical data, damage reports, cross-referenced mass curve analysis. V17 sat near the top of that dense stack.

"This was not V17," Vex said.

Drevin nodded. "V17 was a fight. Today I am not even sure they knew we were here."

"Both belong in the same comparison file," Petrov said. "They are not the same behavior."

Drevin had come aboard during the emergency refit. He did not need the battle recited. He needed the present watch kept honest.

"And they still call you a hero," Drevin said.

"Let them," Vex said. "I brought the ship home. That is the whole of it."

Nobody argued with him. Nobody agreed out loud either.

"What is the current strategic guidance?" Drevin asked.

Petrov kept both hands flat on the table. "Peligar posture. Hold the watch. Survive if engaged. Log everything useful. Do not chase a fight you can't win. And none of this archive goes to Fleet without Strategic Security clearance."

"That last part is new," Drevin said.

"Since first contact," Petrov said. "The guidance for survival is older than any of us. The Fleet restriction is the unresolved political question."

Drevin said, "The Fleet border station covering this volume has coverage of the outer approach corridors."

"Thin coverage," Petrov said. "They are at the edge of what their sensors can hold. They may have caught something. They would not know what they were looking at."

"What would they call it?"

The question was genuine. Drevin had been on *Iron Harrow* for eighteen months. His arrival brief had covered threat, guidance, and contact protocols. Not the full archive.

Petrov considered how to answer.

"Anomaly," Petrov said. "Or distress." He paused. "At range, their instruments would only catch the part that falls in human hearing. Loud. Many voices at once. No clean pattern."

"Distress," Drevin said.

"Closest category they have, if they have no context."

Drevin said, "If they caught even part of this on their boards, they have thirty-one seconds of audio they will call distress, a mass return that will not class, and no archive to tell them what either one is."

Petrov looked at his hands. "Yes."

Drevin looked at Vex. Vex did not look back.

It was not a decision that sat with V21 command, or with *Iron Harrow*. Channels existed. The archive did not travel on those channels. That question had sat with Strategic Security since first contact without an answer. That alone told him how hard the question was.

"Secure the intercept," Vex said. "Full chain. V21 command and Strategic Security. No other routing."

"Understood," Petrov said.

"Drevin, I want a full structural inventory from Sorova. What Section Seven needs if we put in at the nearest anchor, and what the schedule looks like at current pace. End of watch."

"Yes, Commander."

"And the weapons solutions you attempted. Log the full attempt record. Not just that a solution was unavailable. I want the specific parameters it failed on."

Drevin said, "You want to know whether the failure was instrument limitation or contact behavior."

"I want to know what the record can prove," Vex said. "Yes."

The briefing ended without summary. The work was clear: Drevin to Engineering for structural inventory, Petrov to the signals archive to run the close-range intercept through whatever analysis tools V21 had access to, Vex to the routing confirmation and the operational log entry. Peligar classification. No Fleet copy.

The tea on the wall bracket was still there when the room cleared.

Vex glanced at it. Then he left it and went back to work.

***KESTREL VEIL — COMMAND DECK***
*WATCH, FRINGE APPROACH — ROUTE SEVEN*

Park almost missed it.

The archive comparison ran in the background during observation windows: routine, automated, mostly nulls, and once in a while something worth a second look. Eighteen months on this assignment had taught Park to watch that background instead of waiting for the board to shout. The fringe had its own noise. The noise had patterns. The patterns were worth understanding even when they turned out to be nothing.

He was running a spectral density comparison against the survey baseline when the anomalous signal arrived at the edge of resolution. Fringe-bearing, fourteen degrees past the surveyed frontier. Faint at first. Faint enough that Park almost tagged it for the null archive and moved on. He stacked the returns. The second stack showed it stronger. The third showed it stronger still, and by the third stack there was no honest reading of the data that said sensor noise.

Something was out there.

"Captain," Park said.

Calder looked over from the corridor plot. In eighteen months Calder had developed the habit of checking the plot when the ship had nothing to report — a habit Park had come to recognize as a sign that the captain was watching the larger picture rather than waiting for someone to bring him problems. He came off the plot now with the unhurried attention that meant he had already begun working the question.

"What do you have?"

"Long-range intercept, fringe-bearing." Park sent the waveform to the rail display. "Multi-spectral. Nothing in the current library."

The waveform sat on the display and looked like nothing Park had clean language for. It moved in overlapping registers — some frequencies rising, some falling, some cycling through patterns that suggested periodicity without confirming it. The spectral range ran from frequencies his instruments could resolve clearly down into bands where the rendering went approximate and then uncertain. Park had been working signals long enough to know the difference between a transmission that fit his instruments and one that had been reduced to fit them.

This had been reduced. The original, at source, was larger.

Ortega had moved to the sensor station without being asked. Thessaly crossed the command deck in the deliberate way that meant she was already working the problem before anyone said it out loud.

"Mass return?" Calder asked.

"Approximate," Ortega said. She ran the comparison library while she talked. "Range is at the limit. I have a probable presence on the fringe bearing. Large. Possibly multiple objects in close proximity. The profile is inconsistent across my sweeps. It will not lock into a class-confident return."

"Closest match in the library?"

"The Alpha Seven contact," Ortega said. "*Iron Harrow.* Similar return pattern. Not a lock." She worked the board. "But the profile changes. Three times in the last forty minutes it has shown a different mass distribution. Not random drift. Not instrument noise. Something in the contact itself. Ships do not do that."

"And it is bigger," Park said.

Calder looked at him. "Bigger?  than the *Harrow*.  How is that even possible."

"I don't nkow sir, but it is absolutly bigger than *Harrow*s return," Park said. "If the library entry is right, this one is larger."

Calder said, "Is it moving?"

"Yes. rapdily sir.  Bearing roughly parallel to the frontier edge. Not on an intercept with us."

Brenner checked his main-engine reading without being asked. Kept course.

Calder said, "Park, audio render. Low."

Park opened it.

The bridge held through six seconds of it.

Then Calder said, "Cut," and Park cut it.

Brenner kept his eyes on the helm card. The main-engine needle was very steady.

"What the hell is that?" Ortega said. "Distress. More than one voice."

"Loud," Park said. "Many voices at once, or one source doing more than one thing. It does not match our standard distress formats. Frequency, pattern, duration. None of it matches. But if I had to put it in a bin—"

"We would be matching it to the nearest human analogue," Thessaly said.

"Yes."

"Which may not be accurate."

"I know." Park considered the waveform on the display. "I have no other analogue."

Calder said, "What is your honest read?"

Park looked at the signal. Eleven years of communications work. Distress formats he could recite in his sleep. Five real calls answered. Dozens trained.

"I do not know what that is," Park said. "It is nothing like we have heard before."

"Don't know what it is either," Brenner said. "Gives me a headache."

Calder said, "Log it as provisional. Origin unknown. Mass return unresolved. Audio attached. Classification pending further data." He looked at the sensor display. "Stay dark. No illumination. No response."

"Aye, Captain."

The contact held its bearing for the next hour and twenty minutes — not toward them, continuing its parallel track along the frontier edge. The mass return cycled through its inconsistent profile states: large, very large, ambiguous, shifting back. Nothing in Ortega's comparison database produced a class-confident return.

The audio signal remained on the board.

Park kept the audio channel closed and watched the waveform instead.

He was thinking about the layers in it. Not one signal modulated across frequencies, but multiple things happening simultaneously at different spectral addresses. He had seen ships use multi-band transmission for different receivers in different locations — one band for one destination, another for another. This was not organized that way. Every available channel carried the same source signal at once, with no apparent destination targeting.

He noted that in the technical supplemental without forming a conclusion. He did not have enough for one.

Twenty-three minutes after Ortega logged the contact's bearing shift, the mass return turned away from the frontier edge and moved into the fringe on a heading that took it beyond sensor threshold over the following half hour.

The signal fell off with the distance.

Then it was gone.

"Contact departed below threshold," Ortega said. "Board clear."

Brenner checked his helm card. Thessaly made a note at the XO station. Park looked at the empty waveform panel and then closed it.

The contact had been present on the bearing for an hour and forty-nine minutes. It had moved parallel to the frontier edge the entire time. It had not approached. It had not altered bearing when — as far as Park could determine — it might have detected Kestrel Veil's position. It had not addressed any signal toward them.

It had simply continued.

"Do we relay?" Park asked.

"Yes," Calder said. "Provisional. Thin as it is. Unknown origin, mass return that will not confirm, audio that does not match anything we know. Fleet needs the contact on the record. Build the packet."

Park closed the display. The archive had the intercept. The mass return data was saved. The provisional classification was in the local log.

He sat with the clean board and thought about what he had heard. Then he went to build the packet.

***KESTREL VEIL — COMMUNICATIONS COMPARTMENT***
*RELAY REPORT — WATCH THREE*

The standard Fleet observation relay packet was twelve pages of fields, dropdown categories, and structured free text, and Park had completed it hundreds of times across his career without ever fully trusting it to carry what the work actually needed.

He was on his third attempt at the classification section.

The contact-type dropdown offered: Fleet vessel, allied vessel, commercial traffic, unaligned civilian, suspected hostile, unknown origin catalogued, unknown origin uncatalogued, astronomical anomaly, sensor artifact.

He had been using *unknown origin uncatalogued*.

The threat assessment field offered: confirmed hostile, probable hostile, possible hostile, neutral observed, neutral assessed, undetermined.

He had been using *undetermined*. The form required him to explain this in a minimum-fifty-word free text field.

His fifty words, honest, read: *Contact does not match any catalogued class in Fleet survey library. Closest mass-return comparison is Alpha Seven contact Iron Harrow; observed contact appears larger. Mass return inconsistent and non-confirmable across observation window. Behavior: continued bearing parallel to frontier edge, no approach, no engagement, departed without incident. Audio intercept attached. Signal does not match known distress or traffic formats. No distress flag applied. Threat undetermined. Recommend signals technical review of attached intercept.*

That was more than fifty words. He had counted twice.

Thessaly reviewed the draft from the chair across the table.

"No distress flag," she said.

"I will not put one on," Park said. "Ortega said distress on the bridge. The recording does not match the formats. I am not going to tell Fleet someone is dying when I do not know that."

"They will still want a cleaner category."

"Then they can ask," Park said. "The honest answer is unknown. I will not improve on that by pretending otherwise."

Thessaly considered this.

"Add a cover note," she said, "that the standard fields are insufficient for the observed contact type and that the attached supplemental record contains the full technical detail. That way the processing queue knows it is a genuine unknown rather than a lazy undetermined."

Park added the cover note. Then he added one more line to the technical supplemental: *Audio intercept does not match known distress or traffic formats; multi-band simultaneous transmission; no apparent destination targeting; mass return larger than Alpha Seven Iron Harrow library entry and will not hold a stable profile; further analysis required.*

He read it back. It was accurate. It was also not going to be read by anyone in signals intelligence for several weeks, if then.

He sent the packet.

The relay acknowledgment came back in four minutes. Received, processing. No immediate query. The queue would work through it eventually.

Calder appeared in the compartment doorway. "Packet sent?"

"Sent," Park said.

"What classification did you land on?"

"Unknown origin, uncatalogued. No distress flag. Undetermined threat. Closest mass comparison is Alpha Seven *Iron Harrow*, and this one runs larger." Park looked at the sent confirmation. "They will ask me to be more specific."

"Then they will need to add more specific options to the form," Calder said.

He did not say this dismissively. He said it the way he said things that were simply true.

Park closed the relay interface. He kept the local archive open and pulled up the audio intercept. Not to play it. Just to look at the waveform thumbnail. He had been looking at it periodically since the watch and he was no closer to a better answer than the one he had logged.

The thumbnail was labeled: *origin unknown, uncatalogued, spectral range exceeds standard parameters*.

He thought about the width of the spectral range. His instruments were good — the Kestrel's sensor suite had been updated during the refit eighteen months ago and Park had been pleased with the spectral resolution improvements. But the audio intercept had pushed past the top of his useful range and well past the bottom. What he had was a slice. The original signal, at source, occupied channels he could not fully measure.

Whatever it was doing with all that bandwidth, it was doing more of it than he could read.

He closed the archive and went back to the board.

***KESTREL VEIL — COMMAND DECK***
*END OF WATCH*

Calder sat with the watch summary open and did not touch it for a while.

Park had already done the hard part. The packet was gone. Unknown. Larger than *Harrow*. Audio that did not fit. That was as honest as the forms would allow.

What sat with Calder was simpler and worse: he had watched something move along the frontier for nearly two hours, stayed dark, taken no risk, and still could not say what he had seen. Route Seven had taught him to refuse a clean story when the data would not support one. That lesson still felt like good command.

It also left a gap he did not like.

Honesty not having a finished picture did not sit well in him. Not as fear. As unfinished work. He could approve a provisional packet and still keep the contact open on his desk, waiting for the next return that would let him close it. Other captains lived with that. Calder had never learned how. He carried incomplete watches the way some men carried debts: quiet, exact, and still owed.

He wanted another pass. A clearer mass return. A second ship confirming the bearing. Something that would let him stop carrying the unfinished shape of it. He told himself that was diligence. On Route Seven, diligence had usually been the difference between a useful report and a dangerous one.

He approved nothing yet. He waited for Thessaly.

Thessaly brought the department check-ins from the end of watch. She did not ask about the contact. She had been on the command deck for the full observation window. Her method was to let him finish his thinking before she offered hers.

"What is your read?" Calder asked.

"Same as Park's," she said. "Unknown is honest."

"And if honesty is not enough."

Thessaly thought about it. "Then we keep listening. We do not invent the rest."

"The mass return."

"Would not lock," Thessaly said. "Larger than the *Harrow* library entry, if Ortega is right. Put it on the summary separately from the audio. Two anomalies, not one."

Calder added it to the summary: profile inconsistency across the observation window, larger than Alpha Seven *Iron Harrow* comparison, no class match, departure bearing not logged before threshold loss. He wrote it cleanly and still felt the unfinished edge of it under the words.

"Ortega wants more time on the fringe bearing," Thessaly said.

"She has it. Flag the bearing for priority listen on the next observation window."

"Already done." Thessaly set the department check-ins on the console. "Brenner's helm log shows two course adjustments. Standard. Nothing outside normal."

"Good."

"Engineering has the Section Nine gravity calibration scheduled for next watch, per the maintenance rotation."

"Confirm it."

He went through the check-ins. Ship's business, the ordinary accumulation of maintenance and watch transitions and department status reports that was what a ship's day mostly consisted of. The contact had been an anomaly in the middle of a routine watch. The routine continued.

When the check-ins were complete and Thessaly had gone back to the XO station, Calder sat with the watch summary on his display.

The contact section read: *Unknown-origin contact observed, fringe-bearing Route Seven Sector 14, classification provisional, mass return inconsistent and larger than Alpha Seven Iron Harrow comparison, audio intercept recorded and transmitted, relay packet submitted. See attached supplemental records.*

It was accurate. It left him wanting one more confirmation he did not have.

He approved the summary anyway.

The watch continued.

***IRON HARROW — COMMAND STUDY***
*END-WATCH*

Vex worked from paper.

The habit was old. The paper slowed him down in a useful way — forced him to commit to a word when the terminal offered endless revision.

The reports were stacked on the desk: hull damage, archive summary, emissions profile, audio intercept duration log, routing confirmation. All sent. V21 command receipt pending. Strategic Security receipt confirmed.

He had the terminal routing pane open to the V21 volume index. The Fleet border station routing code for their sector was in the recent lookup history. He had looked at it twice in the past two hours.

He understood the political question. Channels to Fleet existed. He did not understand routing the same assessment through Strategic Security since first contact without a decision on whether to share the archive. Contact density across V17, V18, and V21 in the most recent eighteen months was higher than the preceding three years combined. The archive was not staying stable.

He looked up from the paper.

And stopped.

He had spent the watch answering how to survive them, how to log them, whether to tell Fleet. The contact had already answered something he had not asked. They were not here for *Iron Harrow*. Not holding the volume. Transit through. Engagement only because the hull was in the path.

Where were they going?

The question arrived late and whole, shocking his system to its core. Where are they going?

Survival doctrine told him how to live through contact. It did not answer destination.

He cleared the Fleet routing address from his recent lookup history.

He was not the rank to force the political question, and he would not act outside authorization while authorization was still theoretically coming. He had documentation. He had routing. He had done the work the guidance described.

He pulled the paper assessment sheet across the desk and wrote one additional line at the bottom in his own hand: *Contact behavior consistent with transit priority. Iron Harrow was not the objective of this engagement — it was an obstacle; they didn't even acknowledge our existence. Destination unknown. V21 outer reach falls within documented contact range. Recommend strategic review of current dissemination guidance in light of increasing contact density and today's close-range data. Recommend destination analysis as a standing question, not a closed encounter.*

He dated the line. He added the paper to the routing envelope that would go to V21 command with the electronic copies.

That was the honest record. Survival report for today. Boundary warning for the same reason V17 had been a warning: they should not be this far in, this often, and the quiet in this region was finished.

He picked up the audio intercept duration log.

Thirty-one seconds of close-range recording — the longest and highest-fidelity intercept in the archive's twenty-three entries. Petrov had the analysis running. Multi-channel, separable spectral bands, no apparent destination-targeting: consistent with every prior intercept, only clearer.

It was communication. He could not prove it yet. Nothing else fit.

Somewhere in V21 outer reach, or past its edge into the fringe, there were more of them. Moving. Continuing. The question stayed open behind him. Not whether they would return. What they were moving toward.

He turned off the desk lamp.

The Fleet routing address was cleared from the history. It would be there again the next time he accessed the V21 volume index.

He left that alone and went to bed.

***KESTREL VEIL — COMMAND DECK***
*WATCH CHANGE*

Ortega held her station through the watch change without being asked.

She ran the fringe bearing for ninety minutes after the contact dropped below threshold — not under orders, because clean data after an anomalous observation was useful. The bearing returned nothing. No recurrence. No secondary signal at lower intensity.

She pulled eighteen months of Route Seven observations and cross-referenced the mass return against every logged contact in the corridor. No prior observation with matching parameters. No flag from any other Fleet observation asset on this route describing a return with inconsistent profile across the window.

Whatever had been on the fringe bearing, Route Seven had not seen it before — or had not recognized it.

Calder came back to the command deck an hour into the second watch. He looked at the sensor display. He looked at the frontier edge on the chart and the thin notations beyond the survey boundary: provisional, limited data, gaps.

"Anything?" he asked.

"Bearing clean," Ortega said. "Eighteen months of route data checked. No prior comparable flag."

Calder nodded. "Keep the fringe bearing flagged for priority listen on the next window."

"Already flagged."

Thessaly came off the XO station and stood with him for a moment, neither of them saying anything, both watching the same clean bearing.

"You logged it the way I would have logged it," Calder said. "Provisional. No invented category. I noticed."

"Is that a compliment or a review?" she asked.

"Both. Mostly the first one." He looked at her, not the plot. "If I'm not on the deck when the next one shows up, I want you calling it the same way. Not waiting for me to confirm your instinct first."

Thessaly considered that longer than the sentence deserved. "You'd trust that."

"I already do," Calder said. "I'm telling you so you stop checking."

She didn't have an answer ready for that, which was itself an answer. She went back to the XO station a little straighter than she'd left it.

Park was still at the communications station, which was not his watch but Calder did not send him off. He had the local archive open — the audio intercept waveform on the technical display, not playing, just visible. He had been looking at it on and off since the watch ended and he had not reached any conclusion that improved on unknown.

Calder brought coffee from the wardroom and held it while the sensor array ran another cycle.

The fringe bearing returned nothing.

The frontier edge on the chart did not move.

The watch summary was approved and logged. The relay packet was sent. The observation flag was on the next window's work list. Everything the job required had been done.

"It is still on the recording," Park said.

Calder looked over.

"The signal," Park said. "Whatever it was. It is still in the archive. We filed it as unknown." He paused. "I keep thinking about the part I couldn't hear. The lower registers. The full spectral range." He looked at the waveform thumbnail. "What I captured is a fraction. The source was bigger than my instruments."

Calder watched the board cycle.

"Log that," he said. "In the technical supplemental."

"I did."

"Then it is in the record," Calder said. "When someone with better instruments looks at the recording, it will be there."

Park closed the waveform display. The communications board showed only clean status and the relay sent-queue.

The fringe bearing was empty. The recording was in the archive — labeled unknown, provisional, pending. The source was already beyond the range of any instrument on the ship.

The watch continued.
