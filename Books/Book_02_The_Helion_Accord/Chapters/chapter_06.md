# Chapter 6

## The Burning Fringe

---

***HELION — FLEET COMMAND OPERATIONS FLOOR***
*FOUNDERS' REACH PRODUCT CLEAR*

The packet cleared the last gate at 04:12 Helion local.

On the operations floor, three people saw it at once.

Lieutenant Tamsin Corven had spent six years on Segment watch making empty space behave itself. Freighter slots. Training lanes. Weather overlays that meant nothing. She was mid-keystroke when the Founders' Reach product unlocked — *Kestrel Veil* field feed — and the strip filled with images that did not belong on a peacetime board: yard-scale ships hanging over a colony without formation, open mouths taking metal in slow streams, roads that got wider as walls vanished, a school rectangle of dirt and one corner post.

She said, "Sir."

Her voice was too quiet. She said it again, louder.

Around her, chairs scraped. Someone dropped a stylus. A runner who had been carrying coffee froze, the tray slowly falling from his hands, spilling coffee everywhere; nobody noticed. The floor supervisor came out of the glass booth at a walk that became a run when he saw faces, not screens.

"Lock the corridor," he said. "No open gallery traffic until Command clears. Get Rowan. Get FIB. Get Doctrine. Now."

There were contingency forms for when a colony was lost — weather, abandonment, natural disaster, some other peacetime means. The templates were rarely used, and nobody on this watch had ever opened one for real. File a residual. Open memorial logistics. Notify next of kin when names cleared. Those forms assumed the worst had already finished somewhere else and arrived here as paper you could put in order. Nobody had a form for this. The strip was still updating. The ships were still taking materials from the colony. Somewhere on the outer arc, *Kestrel Veil* was still watching — and Helion's useful motion was to lock a corridor and wake people who were too far away to stop any of it.

Alarms did not scream. Helion did not do that for packet clears. What happened instead was worse in its quiet. Status lights shifted from green to amber across six desks. The Segment watch bill began rewriting itself. A maintenance channel opened on the wall display without anyone asking for it — yard availability, spare parts, which hulls could get underway in twelve hours, which needed forty-eight.

A woman at logistics said, flat, "Founders' Reach census was thirty-one thousand."

Nobody answered her.

A man at fleet movements said, "How many Segment craft are on leave."

Nobody answered immediately.

Not because nobody knew.

Because everyone was calculating the same thing.

How many ships existed.

How many were ready.

How many were already too far away.

Corven looked at her own strip and found she did not have a finished number either — only leave flags and berth codes that refused to become an answer under her hands. "Too many," she said, because that was the only honest count the floor could give itself yet.

She would remember the first thing she said for a long time after. Not the census. Not leave. *Sir.*

Outside the sealed operations floor, runners started. Coffee urns emptied faster. Staff officers who had believed ordinary pace was competence found themselves jogging.

***HELION — YARD THREE / PATROL BERTHS***
*STATUS CHANGE*

On Yard Three, the berth lights for *Ardent Measure* went from stand-down amber to elevated observation white.

Petty Officer Second Class Iren Kade was halfway through a coupler check when the status tone hit. She wiped grease on her trousers and looked up at the board. Elevated observation. Segment 6 outer arc. Divert order pending.

"That's us," her chief said.

Around them, the yard woke the way yards woke when the board meant it: tool carts rolled, seals got rechecked that had been good an hour ago, a fueling crew that had been on break came back without being called. Two hulls over, *Quiet Ledger* went to the same white. Farther down the line, a freighter conversion berth stayed dark — not their problem yet — and the people standing under it watched the white lights climb and did not talk.

Kade's slate chimed. Family affairs notice: leave windows on Segment 6 rotations deferred indefinite. Hardship exceptions through channels. Do not circulate unverified system names.

She thought of her brother on New Meridian, who had been planning a visit next month. She did not message him. She put the slate away and went back to the coupler. Her hands were steady. Her mouth was dry.

"What happened," a younger tech asked.

"Founders' Reach," the chief said. "Read the sealed summary when you get clearance. Until then, we make this ship ready. Whatever happens next, we need to be there."

***HELION — FLEET COMMAND ANNEX***
*ROWAN*

Admiral Elias Rowan had slept in the annex chair once in the last two days, and his body had not forgiven him for it. His neck was stiff. His left shoulder had a knot he kept rolling without thinking. The slate stack on the side table was taller than when he had last looked at it — unfinished briefs, unanswered notes, three requests for a statement he had not written yet. His wife's last message sat in his personal queue, unread past the first line. He had meant to answer between meetings. The meetings had not stopped.

The operations floor call reached him before the coffee did.

He walked the short corridor with his knees already complaining and found the Founders' Reach product open on the primary display: emptied system, processing signature, no survivors answering, no certified motive. Calder and Thessaly had sent what they saw. Massive ships hung over the colony like yards that had come unmoored — no formation, no haste, the ships open.. hatches? Taking in all the  materials while the settlement came apart under them. People gone — no bodies found, no answer why.

Beside it, still unconfirmed, sat the second outer-corridor alert from Segment 6.

Quiet agricultural annex. Low population. Orchard grids and cold-storage sheds, a single freight pad, a weather mast that sent boring packets on a schedule nobody really cared about. The kind of place Segment watches treated as background — ordinary when it answered, ignored when it did not make trouble. No Founders' Week committee. No schoolhouse freight waiting on a New Meridian apron. Just a catalog entry and a thin traffic history.

Orchard Compact sat beside Founders' Reach on the operational display, an unfinished alert blinking next to a confirmed loss. Rowan found himself watching the unfinished one more closely. Founders' Reach was a completed disaster. They could count it. Name it. Begin the long work of mourning it. Orchard Compact offered none of that. Only fragments. Missing returns. Conflicting sensor data. A growing list of things they could not confirm.

The worst possibility was not that Orchard Compact was next.

The worst possibility was that it had already happened.

Park's packet from *Kestrel Veil* had used the catalog name. Rowan ordered it kept off public briefs for now — one Founders' Reach speech was already enough noise for open rooms. In sealed rooms, nobody needed a second speech. Founders' Reach had shown what the advance did. Orchard Compact sat on the same path. That was corollary enough to raise the watch while confirmation finished its work.

Colonel Neve of Fleet Intelligence Bureau was already in the room. Commander Hale of Doctrine Bureau arrived with the First Doctrine strip open on his slate like a man who knew he was about to spend the day defending it.

For a moment none of them spoke. Founders' Reach kept updating on the primary display. Orchard Compact kept failing to.

Rowan pointed at the emptied system, then at the unfinished alert.

"Founders' Reach is gone," he said. "We watched it happen. If we sit here until every desk agrees on Orchard Compact, we may lose that one the same way. Same ships. Same people gone. Same  description, same ending, just a different name."

Neve's jaw tightened. "If we move populations on what we have, we may empty a living annex for a fear that turns out to be noise. That is irreversible too. I will not let dread write the brief. Not for you. Not for Dominion. Not for the gallery. We report what the sensors can support."

Hale kept his hand on the First Doctrine strip. "Observation before judgment is not a slogan. It is how we stop ourselves from starting a war based on bad information. Elevate the watch. Divert the ships. I will support that. Do not use Founders' Reach as a reason to open fire. Losing one colony does not give us the right to start a war on the next guess."

Rowan's hand hit the table hard enough that Neve's slate jumped.

"Do not call that a guess," he said. His voice was low and ugly with fatigue. "Thirty-one thousand people. We watched them go. Live. From one of our own scouts. Founders' Reach is not a theory. It is not 'one colony' on a strip. It is thirty-one thousand people gone while we watched and did nothing — and Orchard Compact is the same silence on the same path. Same missing returns. Same kind of quiet. If you want to stop me from opening fire, say that. Do not talk about Founders' Reach like a filing error we should not overreact to."

Hale did not raise his voice. "I am not minimizing the dead. I am stopping us from turning their deaths into an attack order on an unfinished alert. Elevate. Divert. Evacuate if you must. Do not open fire because we are angry and afraid."

The air stayed thin for a breath.

An analyst near the wall cleared his throat. He looked like a man who had been sent to get an answer and knew he would be yelled at either way.

"Segment logistics needs a decision, Admiral," he said. "Do we start evacuation staging for Orchard Compact or not. Freight is waiting. Lift crews are waiting. Yard Three already put *Ardent Measure* and *Quiet Ledger* on elevated watch — leave is being cancelled as we speak. If we stage people out and the annex turns out to be fine, Command owns the panic. If we do not stage, and it goes the way Founders' Reach went—"

He stopped. He did not need to finish it.

"I know what could happen if we do not," Rowan said.

He looked at Orchard Compact until the unfinished strip hurt worse than his shoulder.

"I am not asking you for certainty," he said. "I am asking you for the cost of waiting. Founders' Reach was destroyed while rooms argued. I will not protect Fleet from criticism while an annex flickers on the same path."

Neve did not look away. "Then understand what comes with that. Incomplete evidence. Political exposure. A premature evacuation that may ruin trust for the next real one. FIB will not pretend the sensors agree because you need them to."

"Write or say whatever you are going to," Rowan said. "send them off to whomever you want. I am still moving. And if this turns out to be premature, if Orchard Compact is still standing tomorrow, I believe that will only increase trust. It tells people we will not sit on our hands after Founders' Reach. At the end of the day this is my call."

Hale's hand stayed on the First Doctrine strip. "Admiral, this is the line. Once we decide fear is enough to move populations, we change what Fleet is allowed to do. We cannot pretend that choice does not matter."

Rowan looked at him long enough that the room went quiet.

"Commander, I am aware of the doctrine."

The anger was gone from his voice now. What remained was exhaustion.

"I helped write the last review. I know exactly what is at stake."

He looked at the First Doctrine strip still open in Hale's hand.

"But doctrine exists to protect people. It was never meant to become a reason we stop protecting them."

He looked back at Orchard Compact.

"There are people on that world who do not have the luxury of waiting for us to become comfortable with a decision."

A pause.

"Your objection is recorded."

Rowan looked around the room.

"The decision is mine."

He drew a breath. Tired. Deliberate.

"If we move too soon and Orchard Compact turns out fine, that decision belongs to me. If we wait for permission and those people die, that decision belongs to them. I can answer for moving early. I cannot stand in front of another set of families and explain why we waited too long."

Silence held the room.

"Elevate observation," Rowan said. "Divert the nearest Segment craft. Calder has field judgment over the diverted sector scouts — support watch under his call until Helion can catch up."

He looked at Orchard Compact's fading marker.

"Passive first. No illumination unless someone is alive and needs us to see them. No attack language. No war declaration."

Neve looked up. "Evacuation?"

Rowan did not hesitate.

"Prepare for it."

The room went quiet.

"Freight. Lift. Medical. Family affairs. Stage everything we would need if we are wrong about how much time we have."

The orders went out.

For the first time since the packet arrived, the room moved with purpose.

Rowan watched the Orchard Compact marker.

It was still unresolved.

That was the part he hated most.

Hale opened his mouth.

Rowan stopped him with a glance.

"I know what that means."

He looked back at the display.

"If Orchard Compact is still standing, I will answer for moving too soon."

A pause.

"I will not answer for leaving people there because we were afraid to be wrong."

"The name stays off Founders' Week channels," Rowan continued. "Public rooms get outer-corridor alert under review. Nothing cleaner. Nothing that claims we know more than we do."

Neve closed her eyes once, then opened them. "I will write the uncertainty into every packet that leaves this room."

"Good," Rowan said. "Write it. Do not use it as a reason to stand still."

Hale did not put the First Doctrine strip away. "Then the amendment hearing opens on your signature as well as Council's. Observation still means something — or this becomes the day we stopped meaning it."

"Yes," Rowan said. "It means we look before we shoot. That is not an excuse to stand still."

A junior staffer at the door cleared his throat. "Admiral. Council open gallery is already asking for a statement. Freight disruption notices are stacking on New Meridian. Families want Founders' Reach memorials and names faster than sealed forensics can clear them."

Rowan stood. His knees protested. He locked them and kept moving.

"We work both rooms," he said. "Sealed brief first — Ellis, Neve, the people who have the Founders' Reach product and the Orchard Compact watch. Gallery second — Council, freight, families who want names. Same facts in both. No cleaned-up version for the cameras. No harsher version for the sealed table."

He looked at Hale, still holding the First Doctrine strip like a shield.

"Hale. Unless you have something that actually helps move ships or people, get out of this room."

Hale held his eyes for a beat, then put the strip away and left without another word.
