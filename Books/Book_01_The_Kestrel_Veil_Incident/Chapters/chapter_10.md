# Chapter 10

## Running Dark

❦



Thirty-one hours had passed since Walsh matched the third encrypted stub to a suppression header Park had already logged in buffer six cross-reference.

The match was not a conspiracy map. It was worse in the way operational facts were worse than theories: a dated Fleet Operations relay tag—**ANOMALY REPORT / HOLD FOR INTERIOR REVIEW**—sitting on top of a Korrath interior survey packet that should have propagated to L-3 in 2224 and had instead vanished from every merge path Okonkwo's core store said existed. Walsh had found it because his watch notebook tracked handshake windows Thessaly used for scheduling and Park used for narrow-path verification, and the windows did not align with the timestamps on the encrypted captures. Cole had heard the congestion profile first. Park had verified checksum. Thessaly had ordered the comparison logged without outbound transmission.

Suppression was no longer suspicion. It was something the crew could act inside—the same way they acted inside contradictory star fixes.

Calder had not slept since Walsh closed the loop at 0419 on the previous cycle. Okwelu would note it. He would acknowledge and change nothing. Some patterns survived because they worked until they did not.

The *Kestrel Veil* had departed KR-14 vicinity at 0912 on minimal thruster burn after Okwelu cleared Okonkwo for ship transfer to secured medical quarters and Koss certified buffer six isolation through departure load. SCV-219 *Watcher's Mark* remained on passive track astern—a damaged silhouette the redundant array sometimes placed closer than primary and sometimes forgot entirely. Calder had stopped asking which array owned the ghost. He asked what action remained valid regardless.

Shadow contacts had returned.

Not the inconclusive blip Halden logged during boarding extraction. A pattern. Three passive tracks on primary bearing solutions that held relative geometry too cleanly for drift error. One additional contact on redundant sweep that primary did not always see. No emission spikes. No hails on fleet bands. No narrow-path traffic except the verified stub Park maintained on forty-minute cycle—thin, honest, and useless for asking Fleet whether three unknown hulls had permission to follow a scout that official records still believed held at Korrath fringe.

Reyes called them **followers** in the shadow log and refused to merge the term into master navigation.

Halden called them **superior** once, in a department head brief Calder still tasted: mass profiles consistent with corvette or light escort class, closure rates ambiguous between arrays, weapons signatures absent because passive posture did not grant weapons truth and active sweep would grant position.

Calder had authorized continued outbound drift on thrusters-only separation stack—no jump prep, no high-emission maneuvering, no fleet ping. Thessaly held command timeline. Park kept the narrow path alive without using it to shout. Koss held engineering ring draw below three percent and watched Sharma's life-support readouts the way she watched bearing fatigue in the drive harmonics.

Sharma had been on berth environmental control since 1147—twenty-two hours by Calder's count, though Koss would have corrected him to twenty-two hours and eleven minutes because Koss corrected time the way Brenner corrected torque sequences. Sharma's certification tablet showed atmosphere scrubber duty cycles shifted into emissions-adjacent load management: CO₂ reclamation staged down, thermal vent routing through passive baffles, galley alcove air handlers on minimum exchange because even filtered exhaust left a thermal signature Koss would not certify clean at scout-class resolution.

Calder had learned Sharma's name during boarding extraction. He was learning her competence now the way he learned Park's—by watching what broke when she was absent.

In the galley alcove an hour before departure, Park had refilled Halden's coffee. Halden had not joked. Park had not commented. Calder had eaten standing and reviewed Walsh's notebook photocopy on his tablet—not the original; Walsh had not surrendered the original since Thessaly borrowed it once without telling him and he had noticed anyway.

"Brenner's birthday," Park had said quietly, passing Calder on the way to relay.

Calder had looked up.

"Fourth of cycle. You still don't know."

"I know now."

Park had almost smiled. Almost. Then the shadow contact update had come from Halden and the almost had gone with it.

Okonkwo remained under Okwelu's authority in medical bay—limited consciousness hours, full debrief deferred. Her name sat in buffer six personnel strings beside Kellan and Matu and Lieutenant Reyes who was not their navigator. Calder carried that fragmentation without merging it.

Buffer five held platform reconstruction. Buffer six held KISU-7. Four KR-12 buffers unchanged. Master log reconciliation eighteen percent uncorroborated. Relay mostly lost. Narrow path verified. Suppression confirmed as operational fact.

And three—possibly four—contacts holding geometry on a scout-class hull that had just proved Fleet headers lied.

Calder stood at the command console in second watch with corridor lighting at full and every display alive and understood, with the slow cold clarity that had replaced institutional certainty, that alive displays were an invitation.

He had not yet ordered darkness.

He was almost ready to decide he did not need more certainty to do it.

---



Halden's brief at 0633 was not dramatic. Calder trusted that about him.

"Primary array holds three contacts, bearing cluster aft-port relative, closure rate zero on last four sweeps if primary range is authoritative. Redundant array holds four contacts, same bearing cluster, closure rate low positive on two of four over the same interval." Halden's voice did not rise. "Mass profiles escort-class or better. No emission spikes. No fleet authentication on any band Park monitors. Shadow log preserves both solutions. I am not merging."

Reyes, without looking up from the local chart overlay: "If they are not closing on primary, they are pacing. If redundant closure is real, they are herding."

"Herding toward what?" Thessaly asked.

Reyes: "Unknown. I can give you local observation fixes on KR-13 segment two landmarks—platform debris scatter from KISU-7 charts, thermal null zones Okonkwo's store marks as **stable dark**. Not fleet charts. Local."

Ortega: "Primary places the nearest landmark fix at six hours sublight on current drift. Redundant places it at three."

Calder heard the familiar disagreement and did not treat it as novelty. He treated it as weather.

"Park. Narrow path status."

"Verified on last handshake. No inbound fleet traffic on path. Fleet bands silent except intermittent fragment on low sideband—" Park paused, head tilted a degree Cole would have recognized. "—checksum unstable. I am not certifying content. I hear presence. I do not hear words yet."

"Copy." Calder looked at Halden. "Defensive posture."

"Passive held. Hale on emitter standby—not hot. I will not joke about it."

He had not joked. Calder noted the absence the way Park did.

"Koss."

Engineering channel, flat and immediate: "Ring draw at two-point-eight percent. Sharma has scrubber staging ready for your order. Brenner is on vent baffle alignment in ring four. We can go dark on your mark. Going dark is not free. Berth CO₂ rises. Thermal sinks slower. Okwelu should hear the life-support trade before you authorize."

"Summarize trade for command."

Sharma's voice—younger than Brenner's, careful: "At full emissions control, atmosphere margin drops from nine-point-two days to six-point-eight at current complement if galley and medical bay hold reduced exchange. Thermal vent through baffles adds four hours to sink equilibrium per Koss's model. I can keep medical bay on priority scrubber if Okwelu confirms Okonkwo's compartment isolation."

Okwelu, from medical channel without being summoned: "Confirmed. Medical holds priority. I want Sharma's numbers every ninety minutes logged to my tablet, not summarized."

Sharma: "Yes, Doctor. I— copy."

Calder did not correct the apology reflex. He had larger failures to prevent.

"Thessaly. Recommendation."

She had already built it. "We cannot outrun escort-class on thrusters if they commit. We can deny them a clean lock if we stop advertising. Shadow contacts have held geometry eighteen hours without hail— that is not Fleet courtesy. That is surveillance. Egress on local observation to KR-13 dark landmarks buys time and distance from KR-14 volume where Interior Mandate fiction still has infrastructure. Walsh logs watch windows. Park keeps narrow path cold—listen, do not speak. Calder decides emissions mark."

Calder looked at the forward display—stars, drift vector, three contacts rendered as probability cones rather than icons because icons pretended certainty.

He did not have unified sensor truth. He had a crew that had extracted a survivor, proven suppression, and learned to act on enough confidence.

"Prepare emissions control package," he said. "Reyes plots local observation egress. Halden preserves shadow log on both arrays. Park—passive listen only on narrow path after mark. No outbound. Koss and Sharma execute on my mark. Command deck lighting to dark-watch preset at mark plus thirty seconds. Thessaly holds timeline."

"Aye, Captain," Thessaly said.

Tanaka had already opened her color-coded checklist to the page Reyes had not yet turned to confirm—the current page because the ship was always one confirmed segment behind where it needed to be.

---


Calder waited eleven minutes after the prepare order—not for certainty, for Brenner's confirmation on vent baffle torque and Sharma's final scrubber staging read.

Koss: "Ring four baffles seated. Harmonic clean. Sharma?"

Sharma: "Atmosphere staging at dark-run profile. Medical priority online. Galley exchange at minimum. Berth temp will feel wrong before it becomes wrong—I will tell you when it becomes wrong."

Calder appreciated that distinction. Wrong-feeling was weather aboard a scout. Wrong-measured was his problem.

Reyes: "Local observation plot locked to KISU-7 segment two scatter and thermal null **stable dark** waypoint. No fleet chart merge. Tanaka has sequence on my mark."

Halden: "Contacts holding. Primary closure still zero. Redundant low positive on two tracks. If they are herding, darkness is the correct counter."

Calder did not ask Halden to repeat it. He asked himself whether he trusted interpretive models from people who had been right when merge authority was not.

He did.

"Mark," he said.

Koss: "Emissions control engaged."

The command deck changed before the displays did—a subsonic shift in airflow as scrubbers stepped down, a faint drop in lamp output as lighting cycled to dark-watch preset thirty seconds later, the almost-silence of a ship that had stopped explaining itself to the vacuum.

Sharma on channel, voice lower as if volume could leak signature: "Scrubber profile confirmed. Thermal vent routing through baffles. First sink equilibrium estimate in four hours twelve minutes if nobody opens a hot compartment without clearance."

"Authorized personnel only," Thessaly said. "Walsh, post on environmental doors. Brenner exception for ring four."

Walsh: "Posted."

Calder watched passive sensor overlays populate—no active sweep, no ping, only what the hull could hear when it stopped shouting. Three contacts on primary. Four on redundant. Probability cones widening, not narrowing. Honest uncertainty.

Park at relay, headset adjusted: "Fleet bands flat. Narrow path listening. Sideband fragment returned once—dropped before checksum. I hear traffic the way you hear weather before rain. Not words."

"Log presence. Do not chase."

"Copy."

Reyes leaned into the local chart—not the master navigation merge, the shadow overlay he and Tanaka maintained in margin notation Reyes had carried since *Meridian Echo*. His finger traced a line that did not exist on fleet charts.

"First segment on thrusters only," he said. "Ninety minutes at current drift adjustment. Ortega confirms thermal null bearing on primary when we have enough passive accumulation."

Ortega: "Accumulation window eight minutes minimum at this posture. I—" Reyes's look. "—eight minutes minimum, Captain."

Calder: "Execute."

The *Veil* did not burn. It adjusted—a whisper of thruster exhale Calder felt through the console rail more than heard. Scout-class ships were built to be subtle when survival required it. The crew had simply never required it at this scale.

Halden began building threat models on the tactical side display—not weapons solutions, because they had no clean targets and no authorization to create them. Closure envelopes. Bearing drift. Scenarios where redundant range was truth versus scenarios where primary pacing was truth. He worked without commentary. Without jokes.

Calder watched Halden's silence and understood the humor had not stopped because the situation was dire. It had stopped because Halden had decided jokes were a form of emission.

---


Hour two of dark running was Sharma's hour in the way boarding extraction had been Cole's— not because Calder gave her the deck, but because every department head eventually routed through her numbers.

CO₂ crept from zero-point-four to zero-point-seven percent berth aggregate. Thermal sinks lagged four hours behind Koss's model by twenty-one minutes. Sharma reported both without dramatizing.

"Galley alcove feels stale," she told Koss on the engineering channel. "Not out of spec. Stale."

Koss: "Keep exchange at minimum. Tell Calder if spec approaches."

Sharma: "I will tell Captain Venn at zero-point-nine aggregate or if medical bay pressure delta exceeds my tablet threshold."

Calder heard the exchange because engineering routed through his console by default and because Sharma had learned to say his name without apologizing for saying it—a small victory Okwelu might have classified as morale and Koss would classify as correct procedure.

In the passageway outside ring four, Brenner passed Sharma a wrench she had not asked for—the correct wrench, pre-staged the way Brenner staged everything he called optimized retrieval.

"Baffle harmonic shifted two minutes ago," he said. "I fixed it. You did not owe me a call because your scrubber staging caught the load before the harmonic became a fault. Good work."

Sharma's pause lasted one second. "Thank you. I had the staging read early. Koss wanted margin."

"Koss always wants margin. Margin is why we're breathing."

He left. Sharma logged the exchange in her certification tablet under **dark run / emissions trade** and did not apologize to the tablet.

Calder read the log entry because Sharma's tablet synced to engineering summary by protocol. He filed Brenner's sentence under crew gravity—the way Park knew birthdays and Cole knew congestion by sound and Walsh knew who would refuse to leave a station.

Okwelu appeared in the command deck doorway at hour two plus forty—not entering, not summoning, documenting.

"Berth aggregate acceptable," she said. "Medical bay stable. Okonkwo sleeping. Sharma's ninety-minute logs are accurate. I am adding one hour to Cole's next rest window because Park has not rotated off listen and her blink rate is wrong."

Park, without turning: "Blink rate is fine."

Okwelu: "No."

Park rotated off listen long enough to meet Okwelu's gaze. "Copy."

Calder: "Status."

Okwelu: "You are running dark successfully. That is not the same as safe. Neither is medical."

She left. Calder returned to the passive overlay.

Reyes reported segment one complete on local observation—waypoint not reached, but drift correction consistent with KISU-7 scatter chart, not fleet merge. Tanaka logged the sequence without celebration. Ortega's thermal null bearing on primary matched Reyes's margin fix within tolerance Calder would once have called insufficient. He now called it enough to act.

Halden: "Contact geometry update. Primary still pacing—closure zero. Redundant closure on two tracks increased low positive to low-medium over last sweep window. If redundant is herding, they expect us to run where they can measure run. Darkness deprives them of half their argument."

"And the other half?" Calder asked.

Halden did not joke. "Mass and motion. We still have both. We are not advertising. That is Sharma's work and Koss's baffles. That is Hale keeping emitters cold. That is you not pinging Fleet for permission."

Calder felt the rebuke without resentment. It was accurate.

Park, barely audible: "Sideband fragment again. Three syllables maybe. Checksum failed. I cannot certify words. I hear—" Another pause. "—intermittent traffic. Like someone testing the edge of the lane without committing. Not fleet hail. Not narrow-path handshake."

"Log it."

"Logged."

Cole, at secondary relay in the dim, mug absent for once: "Congestion profile on the fragment is thin. Real thin. If they're shadowing us, they may be listening to the same weather."

Calder: "Assume they are. Assume everyone listens."

Thessaly moved along the deck perimeter—the XO walk, presence without noise. Walsh swapped with Hale at defensive secondary without conversation beyond the notebook handoff. Brenner checked ring four once and returned to passageway station. The ship contracted to whispers and function.

Calder realized he had not checked fleet contact in ninety minutes because there was no fleet contact to check—only Park's intermittent traffic like rain on a roof you could not yet see.

---


Hour four brought the first moment Calder understood they were being driven.

Reyes saw it in local observation first—not in the contact cones, in the drift corridor. Thermal null zones that should have offered cover skewed relative to passive thermal background when Ortega stacked eight-minute accumulation windows.

"They are not closing on primary," Reyes said quietly. "They are shaping our drift. Redundant closure tracks correlate with null zones shifting aft. Something active at extreme low power—or something using mass shadow we cannot resolve—pushing thermal background."

Halden: "Threat model update. Scenario B—herding confirmed on redundant interpretation. Primary pacing remains plausible decoy. Recommend segment two adjustment seven degrees port relative to KISU-7 chart. Uses debris scatter occluder Okonkwo's store marks at segment two boundary."

Calder looked at the plot. Seven degrees port on thrusters-only was not evasion in the heroic sense. It was a clerk's move—small, documentable in local observation, invisible to anyone who trusted fleet charts that said this volume was empty.

"Authorize," he said.

Thruster whisper again. Sharma reported vent baffle load spike within tolerance. Koss: "Handled."

Primary contacts held pacing. Redundant contacts on two tracks—brief closure spike, then relaxation.

Calder's pulse did not spike. His decision cycle did—shorter, almost blind, built from Halden's scenario B and Reyes's occluder and Sharma's margin numbers and Park's intermittent traffic rather than from any institutional merge.

"Did we show?" he asked.

Halden: "No weapons lock. No active sweep from contacts. Passive only on our side. On theirs—unknown. We did not illuminate."

Thessaly: "That is the victory condition until it is not."

Calder almost asked her to define victory. He did not. He knew.

Human victory was not destruction of followers. It was denial—no engagement, no capture, no clean identification transmitted to whatever Fleet layer had suppressed anomaly reports and compartmentalized Okonkwo's crew into incompatible clocks.

Segment two adjustment completed at 1447. Ortega confirmed occluder thermal profile on primary within Reyes's tolerance. Redundant array placed the occluder closer and thicker—a familiar argument.

Park: "Traffic again. Longer burst. Still no checksum. I think—" She stopped.

Calder waited.

"I think I heard a callsign fragment. Obsolete prefix. Not our narrow path. Not fleet band. I am not certifying."

Halden's jaw tightened once. "Ghost Fleet weather."

"Log as uncertified presence," Calder said. "Do not merge into threat model as confirmed hostile. Log as pressure."

Halden: "Logged."

Okwelu sent one line to Calder's tablet from medical: **Okonkwo awake. Brief window if needed. Do not wake ship for it.**

He did not wake the ship for it.

---


Hour six was when redundant closure spiked and primary did not—and the crew chose darkness over battle without discussing it as philosophy.

Halden: "Redundant reports two contacts accelerating closure—medium range on redundant, long on primary. Divergence at maximum since KR-12. If redundant is right, we have eleven minutes before their passive solution tightens to escort intervention range. If primary is right, we have an hour."

Reyes: "Segment three option—thermal null **stable dark** waypoint at KISU-7 mark. Eleven minutes on thrusters at whisper if we accept CO₂ margin burn from Sharma."

Sharma, already speaking before Koss could: "CO₂ margin burn for eleven minutes drops aggregate from six-point-eight days to six-point-one if we restore exchange after segment. Medical bay holds priority. I can do it. I need Brenner on ring four baffles for thirty seconds at peak vent."

Koss: "Show me the tablet."

A pause—Sharma pushing her certification screen to Koss's engineering console. Calder did not see the numbers. He heard Koss's silence, the good kind.

"Margin is tight but real," Koss said. "Not fiction. Sharma, you are authorized to spend it."

Sharma: "Copy. Brenner on baffles at peak."

Koss: "Brenner."

Brenner: "Ready."

Calder had eleven minutes of contradictory range and six-point-one days of air and no fleet authority and a suppression header Walsh had matched to a lie.

He did not have certainty. He had enough confidence.

"Execute segment three," he said. "Halden—track both solutions. No active sweep. No weapons hot. Hale stays cold."

Hale: "Cold."

Thruster whisper became thruster breath—still not a burn, not engagement, the scout equivalent of holding your mouth open and moving anyway. Sharma's tablet spiked yellow, then green. Brenner reported baffle peak handled. Koss reported harmonic clean.

On passive overlay, redundant closure tightened—then broke geometry.

Primary contacts held pacing.

Redundant contacts—two of four—dropped below threshold on redundant sweep entirely while primary still placed them aft-port at long range.

Calder watched probability cones diverge and did not merge them.

"They lost redundant solution," Halden said. Voice flat. "Primary pacing unchanged. We are still in the window."

"Or they stopped herding on redundant because we stopped feeding the array they expected," Reyes said.

"Or weather," Park said.

Calder: "We do not need motive. We need distance."

Segment three completed. Local observation placed the *Veil* at KISU-7 **stable dark** waypoint margin—not inside the null, adjacent, the way Reyes navigated by occluder and scatter rather than by fleet certainty.

Halden ran threat models again. Closure envelopes widened on primary. Redundant remained inconsistent—contacts absent, then one returned, then two.

"No weapons lock," Halden said. "No hail. No emission spike from contacts."

Thessaly, almost whisper: "Human victory is ugly today."

Calder heard the boarding extraction in her sentence. He answered the dark run they were still inside.

"Today it counts."

---


Hour eight. Emissions control engaged. Lights dimmed on dark-watch preset until the command deck existed in the blue-gray of instruments and faces reduced to cheekbone and habit. Calder's console cast enough glow to read threat envelopes without painting the bulkhead. Somewhere in ring four, a vent baffle ticked once—metal cooling, Brenner's work settling into the new equilibrium Sharma had bought them.

No Fleet contact—flat bands, silent relay, narrow path listening cold. Calder had stopped expecting acknowledgment. The absence was its own kind of answer: whatever Fleet had been protecting with **HOLD FOR INTERIOR REVIEW** did not include telling a scout in Korrath whether darkness was permitted.

Passive sensors only—primary and redundant preserved in shadow log, never merged, never used to shout. The ship listened the way Park listened—patient, uncertified, unwilling to pretend silence meant empty.

Unknown contacts on scope—three on primary, four on redundant, probability cones like bruises aft-port. They had not vanished. They had not committed. They remained geometry, and geometry was what superior forces used when they believed time belonged to them.

Crew whispering on deck—Tanaka confirming segment markers to Reyes in words Calder had to lean to hear; Hale reporting emitter temperature to Halden in monosyllables; Cole telling Park the sideband was quiet for now, for now being the only promise the relay could keep.

Reyes at navigation, local chart overlay glowing at minimum luminance, navigating by observation fixes Okonkwo's store had smuggled out of Interior Mandate compartmentalization—thermal nulls, debris scatter, occluder marks Tanaka sequenced in color only she and Reyes fully shared.

Halden at tactical secondary building threat models without jokes—closure scenarios, herding vectors, weapons-lock thresholds they had not crossed because Hale kept emitters cold and Calder kept authority off fleet ping.

Park at relay with Cole shadowing one station back, barely hearing intermittent traffic on sideband fragments—presence without words, checksum failures logged as weather.

Calder at the command console making decisions almost blind—trusting Sharma's CO₂ margin at six-point-one days, trusting Koss's baffles, trusting Reyes's local waypoint over fleet merge, trusting Halden's silence as seriously as he had once trusted institutional headers.

Sharma reported from ring environmental: "Aggregate stable post-burn. Exchange restoring to dark-run minimum. Medical bay priority holding. Berth still feels stale. Not wrong yet."

Calder: "Copy. Log every ninety to Okwelu."

"Yes, Captain."

She did not apologize.

Walsh passed along the deck perimeter with his notebook closed for once—watch assignments suspended in dark run except where Thessaly posted them. Okwelu sat in the auxiliary chair angled seventeen degrees from vertical for six minutes before returning to medical without comment. Brenner's heel strike did not appear on environmental door 7-C; Calder had learned not to test that door alone.

The ship held breath.

Contacts paced on primary. Redundant flickered—one contact, two, none, two again. No weapons lock. No battle. No engagement—only shadowing and darkness and professional fear without panic.

Calder looked at the forward display—not stars as certainty, stars as context. The *Kestrel Veil* was uncaught. Not safe. Uncaught in a volume where suppression was fact and followers were geometry and Fleet would not answer because Fleet had been holding anomaly reports for Interior Review since before Okonkwo sealed her vault.

He did not order lights up. He did not order active sweep. He did not ask Park to speak on the narrow path.

Emissions control engaged. Passive only. Unknown contacts on scope. Crew whispering. Reyes navigating by local observation. Halden building threat models. Park barely hearing intermittent traffic. Calder deciding almost blind.

Held breath.

Calder did not look back at KR-14. The damaged *Watcher's Mark* and the platform vault and the compartmentalized lies were behind them in distance if not in consequence. Ahead was darkness the charts disagreed about and followers who had not introduced themselves. He thought of Lisette on Helion Prime receiving his scheduled relay messages into a void he could not yet explain to her without merging truths he had not earned.

He thought of Sharma's grandmother on Helion Prime getting weekly messages from a granddaughter who was currently spending CO₂ margin so the ship would not shine.

He did not think long. Long thinking was a luxury emissions control had revoked.

The unanswered question was what the followers wanted them to run toward in the dark—and whether **stable dark** was a waypoint or a door.

Passive sensor tone shifted once—soft, the kind Park said meant a band had stopped pretending to be empty.

Halden's voice, barely above a whisper: "They're done watching."

Calder did not look back at KR-14. He looked at the scope.

Held breath broke.
