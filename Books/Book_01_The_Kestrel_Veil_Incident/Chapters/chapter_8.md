# Chapter 8

## Boarding Action

❦



Fourteen hours had passed since Park closed the narrow-path verification loop. Relay contact to L-3 remained lost—no carrier, no acknowledgment, no institutional voice to settle arguments the ship had learned to settle without one. What remained was a single low-band handshake lane Park had proven trustworthy enough to act on: not restoration, not authority, but a verified pipe through the same weather that had eaten everything else.

The *Kestrel Veil* held at KR-14 approach coordinates with soft dock to Platform KR-14-Alpha released at 0812 and sublight drift adjusted to maintain two-kilometer separation from the installation spine. Buffer five held the reconstructed survey log at seventy-one percent recovery, segregated and sealed. Four KR-12 buffers remained isolated behind Koss's protocol. Master log reconciliation stood unchanged at eighteen percent uncorroborated entries. No deletions. Only absence.

Calder had slept twice since the reconstruction completed—two blocks Okwelu would have logged as insufficient and not counted as recovery. He returned to the command deck at 0647 during first-watch turnover. Walsh logged the handoff without commentary. Thessaly was already reviewing the compartmentalization packet beside his console—tabs ordered by chronology, edges aligned, priority sequence she had imposed without asking whether he preferred arrival order.

She did not mention the straightening. She never did.

Ghost Fleet was no longer a sensor ghost. It was a file category. Over six hours spanning two watches, Park and Cole had stitched fragmentary authentication traces, obsolete call signs, and partial transit logs into a coherent ugly shape: prior Fleet expeditions into Korrath had occurred under compartmentalized mandates, split across buffers Fleet Operations had never merged for Calder's mission brief. Unit designations. Transit windows. Names that appeared in buffer five's partial roster and again in Park's narrow-path captures with timestamps that should not have overlapped if official history were complete.

Who had ordered the compartmentalization remained unanswered. That it had happened was now operational fact—something the crew could act inside, the way they acted inside contradictory star fixes.

In the galley alcove forty minutes earlier, Park had refilled Halden's coffee without being asked. Halden had not joked. Park had not commented on the absence of joking. Cole had appeared with two mugs—one borrowed from Park's secondary station, one from her own collection, the Helion Prime souvenir with the chipped handle—and set them beside the relay panel before Calder finished his meal packet. She did not explain the mugs. Park did not ask for them back.

Okwelu moved through Corridor C once per watch without being summoned. Calder had tried environmental door 7-C without the kick on his way back from the galley. The panel bound at the lower-left seam. Brenner passed two steps behind him, heel strike on the seam, panel sliding open on the second try. Calder stepped aside. Some procedures lived in muscle memory he had not yet earned.

Reyes and Ortega occupied the nav/sensor stations with the restrained alertness Calder associated with mapping operations in unreliable space. Tanaka's color-coded checklist was open to the KR-14 hold page—the current page because Reyes had not yet turned to confirm the next segment. Ortega waited for Reyes's nod before reporting anything above threshold. Reyes gave the nod without looking up.

Halden maintained defensive watch. Hale had rotated onto pulse emitter calibration at 0530 and fallen asleep in the auxiliary chair angled seventeen degrees from vertical until Halden woke him with a single word Calder did not catch from the command deck. Hale returned to work without embarrassment. Scout crews treated sleep where it found you as logistics, not character.

Koss remained in the engineering ring monitoring auxiliary draw from the platform umbilical teardown—draw now at zero, ring two released, Brenner cleared for away-team duty if authorized. She reported to Calder at 0615: "Platform vault sealed. Buffer five path stable. No ingestion flags overnight."

Park ran passive relay listen on the forty-minute cycle. Each cycle returned flat line on fleet bands. The narrow path was different—not open, not continuous, but verified: a handshake stub that answered when Park sent the correct sequence and returned checksum integrity Park and Cole both trusted. Cole sat beside him at secondary relay, identifying silence and congestion by ear before the display confirmed either.

Calder had begun glancing at Park before addressing Cole by name. Not yet habit. Becoming one.

He reviewed the compartmentalization packet one more time—not as decision authority, but as context for whatever KR-14 still had left to show them. Survey Unit Seven on the platform. Obsolete auth codes on ghost contacts. A narrow comm path that proved institutional merge was not the only kind of truth the ship could use.

He did not request fleet confirmation. Request was impossible. Confirmation, he suspected, would deny what the narrow path had already confirmed in fragments.

---



The handshake arrived at 0714—not on fleet relay, not on the bands Park had watched die, but on the narrow lane he and Cole had spent six hours proving: low-band, compressed, checksum-stable when it answered at all.

Cole's head tilted a degree before the display lit.

"Incoming on verified path," she said. "Congestion profile's clean. That's real traffic."

Park was already decoding. "Distress stub. Scout-class signature. Partial hull identifier—SCV-219. Call sign *Watcher's Mark*. Authentication layer obsolete, pre-2210 issue range. Checksum passes narrow-path verification."

Calder moved to the comm station. Thessaly arrived from the XO console without being called—she did that when the ship's rhythm changed.

"Read it," Calder said.

Park: "Automated distress first, then manual overlay. Hull breach, midships. Atmosphere integrity failing in two compartments. Power bus on backup. Last manual addendum—" He paused, lips moving once without sound. "—'one alive aft, data vault sealed, do not merge buffers.' Signed Lieutenant Commander S. Okonkwo."

The name was not in Calder's mission brief. It appeared in Park's compartmentalization reconstruction—expedition lead, mandate class interior mapping, transit window 2223.

Thessaly: "Ghost Fleet contact becomes physical contact."

Halden: "Range and bearing."

Reyes: "Primary array places contact at eleven hundred meters relative, drifting slow on sublight residual. Bearing consistent with KR-14-Alpha approach corridor. Mass profile scout-class, consistent with SCV-219 registry if registry still means anything."

Ortega: "Thermal elevated midships. Aft segment—" He waited for Reyes's nod. "—thermal signature consistent with occupied compartment. Single point source."

Halden: "Redundant array?"

Reyes pulled the shadow log without merge authority. "Redundant array places contact at eleven hundred meters. Mass consistent. Life signs—zero across hull volume."

The command deck recalibrated attention the professional way.

"Define zero," Calder said.

Reyes: "Redundant sweep reports no metabolic signature in any compartment. Primary array reports one metabolic signature, aft vault-adjacent section, confidence medium."

Ortega: "Thermal agrees with primary on single aft source. Also reports three residual heat zones midships and forward—unoccupied, decay profile inconsistent with single catastrophic event."

Koss, from the engineering channel: "Hull telemetry on narrow path confirms breach midships. Atmosphere loss rate suggests breach occurred six to fourteen hours ago. Aft compartment pressure holding at zero-point-nine-four standard."

Calder studied the overlay—two arrays, one ship, incompatible answers.

"Park. Narrow path still verified?"

"Checksum stable on last exchange. *Watcher's Mark* responded to our handshake within tolerance."

Cole: "Lane's thin but it's honest. If they're lying, they're lying on a pipe we already trust."

Calder did not smile. He filed the sentence as crew competence.

"Thessaly. Recommendation."

She had already built it. "Board. One survivor claimed in manual traffic. Data vault sealed per Okonkwo's addendum. If we leave them, we leave compartmentalized history twice—platform and hull. If we board wrong because sensors disagree, we lose people. Split problem: find the survivor primary array trusts, recover vault data Okonkwo sealed, extract before atmosphere cascade completes aft isolation."

Halden: "Defensive posture—contact shows no weapons signature. No secondary contacts on passive sweep. Drift track doesn't suggest trap geometry. Trap geometry could hide in sensor disagreement. I don't like boarding on split life-sign reads."

Reyes: "I don't like navigating a hull where redundant array says empty and primary says occupied. But primary and thermal agree. Redundant has been divergent since KR-12 on interior mass reads. Shadow log only—but shadow log predicted platform thermal offset at KR-14-Alpha before merge would have."

Calder heard the argument structure: not which sensor was true, but which sensor had been useful when acting without merge authority.

"Koss?"

"Brenner can mate emergency boarding collar if hull collar isn't crushed. Sharma on suit atmo support. I need draw capped at six percent auxiliary for boarding umbilical and portable relay pack. Cole's lane, Park's certification."

Park: "Away-team comm routes through narrow path buffer on Cole's portable relay. Ship-side congestion stays with me. No fleet transmission. Recovered data to buffer six—new segregated lane, no merge with buffer five or KR-12 set."

Calder looked at the distress stub one more time. *One alive aft, data vault sealed, do not merge buffers.*

He had learned to act on enough confidence, not enough certainty.

"Authorize boarding. Calder leads away team. Thessaly holds command deck. Brenner, Sharma, Cole on away team. Brenner—collar and breach assessment. Sharma—atmo and evac. Cole—portable relay, narrow-path bridge to Park. Halden—defensive watch. Reyes and Ortega—cross-array reconciliation on ship. Koss—umbilical draw and hull integrity monitoring. Park—comm authority."

"Aye, Captain," Thessaly said.

Tanaka had already opened her checklist to the boarding support page before Reyes turned.

---


The *Kestrel Veil* closed on *Watcher's Mark* using maneuvering thrusters only—no active ping, Halden's passive posture held. The damaged scout resolved on forward display as a familiar silhouette gone wrong: scout spine, instrument boom stowed on one side and sheared on the other, midships hull plate peeled back in a ragged flap that caught starlight like a torn seal.

Calder watched from the boarding airlock while Brenner led the physical assessment on suit comm. Cole cycled the portable relay pack onto her back harness and ran handshake verification with Park before umbilical extension—three quick tones, the narrow-path answer, Cole's finger tapping Park's channel mute once when congestion built in the buffer write lane before the display flagged it.

Park throttled ship-side without being told. Calder heard the exchange through his suit receiver and understood it as the same competence he had watched during buffer five reconstruction—sound before screen, trust before protocol theater.

"Collar housing crushed on port side," Brenner reported. "Starboard collar functional. Manual series-seven again—same as KR-14-Alpha. I can mate soft collar in twelve minutes if Koss signs draw."

Koss: "Signed at six percent cap. Sharma, confirm suit seals before mate."

Sharma: "Suit seals nominal. Portable atmo scrubber online. Calder, your pressure read is clean."

"Copy."

Reyes in Calder's ear—two channels, Cole's relay keeping them separated from ship noise: "Primary array update. Life sign aft holding. Midships heat zones cooling—unoccupied decay. Redundant array still reports zero life company-wide. I'm logging divergence pattern against KR-14 platform offset. Same class of disagreement."

Ortega: "Particulate scatter midships consistent with abrupt decompression, not slow leak. Timeline suggests breach event, then secondary compartment failures staggered over four to nine hours."

Halden: "No movement on passive sweep. No new contacts."

Thessaly on command channel: "Boarding timeline forty minutes to atmosphere decision point. Okwelu standing by for medical intake if we extract."

Calder watched Brenner work the collar—hands visible on feed, no wasted motion, torque sequence on manual latches the way he had worked KR-14-Alpha's vault panel. Soft mate engaged at 0758. Atmosphere handshake from *Watcher's Mark* showed aft compartment stable, midships compartments at vacuum, forward compartment at partial pressure bleeding down.

"Starboard collar nominal," Brenner said. "Inner hatch route: forward to midships breach, then aft through emergency seal corridor if seal holds. I do not trust midships for atmosphere. We go over it, not through it."

Koss: "Agree. Emergency corridor route on Okonkwo's manual traffic matches aft vault adjacency. Vault seal status unknown until Brenner reads the panel."

Cole adjusted relay gain. Calder heard the buffer lane change—a slight pitch shift in the carrier whine Cole identified before Park logged it.

"Congestion building on away lane," she said. "I'm throttling local write. Park, copy?"

"Copy. Ship-side matched."

Calder: "Boarding team enters at 0804. Brenner lead. Sharma second. I third. Cole, relay pack stays at collar junction—you bridge from there, we do not carry the congestion into the vault."

"Aye, Captain."

---


The inner hatch opened onto a corridor Calder recognized from scout-class layouts—narrow, utilitarian, fleet gray paint scuffed at handhold height. Emergency strips glowed amber on backup power. The air tasted wrong—not contaminated, but thin, with the metallic edge of a compartment that had been losing pressure slowly enough for filters to compensate until they couldn't.

Brenner moved first with a seal scanner. Sharma followed with atmo readouts streaming to Koss and Okwelu on the ship. Calder kept his gloved hand off the near wall out of habit he had learned from Brenner's unspoken corrections.

"Midships breach visible through junction four," Brenner reported. "Vacuum on the other side. Emergency seal corridor aft—pressure door green on local panel. Someone closed it from the aft side."

Reyes: "Primary life sign beyond that door. Confidence rising to medium-high."

Halden: "Redundant still zero."

Calder: "We trust the door and the manual traffic."

"And the primary," Reyes said. Not argument. Clarification.

They reached the pressure door at 0819. Brenner read the local panel with both hands steady on the scanner.

"Seal intact. Atmo on far side zero-point-nine-one standard and climbing—active scrubber in aft compartment. Last manual cycle logged eighteen minutes ago on panel stub. Someone's running atmo from inside."

Sharma: "Far-side composition clean. Stress load on scrubber suggests one occupant, not two."

Calder keyed the manual override with Brenner's confirmation. The door cycled open.

The emergency corridor beyond was short—ten meters to a second door, vault-adjacent housing on the port side. Calder saw the evidence Ortega had predicted before anyone said it aloud: a ration wrapper in the corridor niche, fleet issue, expiration stamp 2224; a boot scuff through dust that did not match the wrapper's age layer; a maintenance tag on the conduit housing dated 2225.06, handwritten, fresh ink when it was written, now faded.

Different times. Same corridor. Not one event.

"Crew didn't vanish together," Sharma said quietly.

Calder: "Log it. Do not merge interpretations yet."

Brenner read the vault-adjacent panel. "Data vault sealed. Manual lock engaged from inside. Okonkwo's addendum wasn't poetry—someone locked this before midships went."

Calder keyed the comm on narrow path through Cole's relay at the collar.

"Park. We're at vault housing. Sealed from inside. Life sign beyond inner door?"

Cole: "Lane's thin—hold." A pause Calder felt in his suit spine. "Primary confirms one sign beyond inner door. Park's overlay says the same. Redundant still blind. Lane's honest."

Thessaly: "Medical prep standing by. Extract decision after contact and data assessment."

Calder keyed the inner door knock protocol—three strikes, fleet standard. Response came after four seconds: two strikes, weak, human rhythm.

Brenner opened the inner door.

---


Lieutenant Commander Sade Okonkwo sat against the vault housing in a pressure suit with the helmet off and the seal ring cracked open for air she clearly did not have energy to fight. Calder knew her face from Park's compartmentalization file—expedition lead, older than the portrait, younger than the exhaustion.

She was conscious. She was not stable.

Sharma was on her before Calder finished the threshold step—vitals scan, pressure cuff on suit fabric, oxygen supplement from portable pack. Okwelu on ship channel: "Heart rate elevated, not arrhythmia. Dehydration. Mild hypoxia. She boards to medical bay, not command deck."

Okonkwo's voice was dry as scraped metal. "You came on the narrow lane."

Calder: "SCV-219 Calder Venn, *Kestrel Veil*. We received your distress on verified path."

"Park," Okonkwo said. Almost a laugh. Almost not. "Still the only one who could make that pipe honest."

Calder glanced at Brenner. Brenner had already moved to the vault panel—not opening, reading.

"Vault's sealed with dual-key protocol," Brenner said. "Okonkwo's key is biometric on the housing. Second key is physical—slot empty. She locked it from inside and kept the manual override chip on her person."

Okonkwo lifted her left glove. A grounded case the size of a ration tin was clipped to her suit belt—the same form factor Brenner had carried the KR-14-Alpha survey chip in.

"Compartmentalized mandate data," she said. "Interior mapping runs KR-13 through KR-14 segment six. Transit logs. Names. Not merged. Not for fleet buffer. For whoever still reads pins instead of headers."

Calder: "We have buffer six segregated. Park's lane."

Okonkwo looked at him with the flat assessment of someone who had stopped trusting institutional merge and had not stopped trusting procedure entirely.

"You read buffer five at the platform."

"We reconstructed seventy-one percent."

"Then you know headers lie." She pressed the case into Calder's gloved hand. "Second key is in the case. Brenner opens the vault. You take the core store and the survivor. Not in that order."

Sharma: "Medical order says survivor first."

"Vault first," Okonkwo said. "Sixty seconds. Then I'll walk. I did not seal myself in to die with the only copy."

Calder looked at Brenner. Brenner looked at Koss on channel.

Koss: "Sixty seconds is ugly but viable if Sharma keeps her conscious and Brenner opens clean. Cole—relay congestion?"

Cole, from the collar junction: "Lane's building again. I can hold sixty if Park throttles ship-side and nobody on the command deck narrates into my buffer."

Park: "Throttled. Brenner, go."

Brenner took the case, keyed biometric acceptance from Okonkwo's pressed thumb on the housing, slotted the manual override chip, and opened the vault panel with the same torque sequence he had used on KR-14-Alpha external seven—lubricant shim from his kit, eleven minutes compressed into forty-one seconds because urgency was not the same as hurry.

The data core came out in a grounded cradle—one unit, fleet inventory code, label **KISU-7 MISSION STORE / DO NOT MERGE**.

"Got it," Brenner said.

Sharma: "Okonkwo on her feet in thirty. Calder, carry the cradle."

Calder secured the cradle to his suit harness. Okonkwo stood when Sharma told her to stand—not when Calder asked. Calder noted the distinction without comment.

They moved back through the emergency corridor. At the junction overlooking midships breach, Calder saw what redundant array had called empty and primary had called complicated: three crew restraints clipped to handholds near the breach edge—one still latched, one open, one torn free at the mount. A toolkit floated in the vacuum beyond the ragged hull flap, tumbling slow. A suit helmet, empty, drifting. Personal effects in the forward compartment visible through a shattered viewport—locker door open, contents arranged as if packed for departure, dust layer inconsistent with the corridor ration wrapper's age.

People had left this ship at different times by different means. Not all of them had left through the airlock.

Okwelu on channel: "Bring her clean. I don't want midships dust in the collar."

They brought her clean.

---


Extraction should have been the simple part. It was not.

At 0837, as Brenner cycled the starboard collar to transfer Okonkwo across the umbilical, Halden reported: "New contact bearing, long range. Passive only. No emission spike. Possible shadow track on drift corridor."

Thessaly: "Suspend collar transfer?"

Calder at the collar with Okonkwo on Sharma's arm and the data cradle on his harness: "Negative. Complete transfer. Halden—track. Reyes—bearing solution on primary. Do not active ping."

Reyes: "Primary places shadow contact at extreme range, confidence low. Redundant places same bearing with confidence medium-high and range closer by factor two."

Calder heard the pattern again—same disagreement, different stakes.

Halden: "If redundant range is right, we have less time than primary says. If primary range is right, we're alarmed for nothing."

Koss: "Collar transfer at eighty percent. Okonkwo across. Cradle next. Interrupt now and we risk collar seal."

Cole's voice tightened on the relay—congestion whine pitching up in Calder's ear.

"Lane congestion spiking," she said. "Park, I need ship-side throttle now or we lose away-team audio."

Park: "Throttled. Cole, hold."

Calder finished the cradle transfer handoff to Brenner on the *Veil* side. Okonkwo was already through. Sharma followed. Calder crossed last, backing through the collar as Brenner disengaged manual latches.

"Collar clear," Brenner said at 0841.

Koss: "Umbilical reel. Draw at zero in thirty seconds."

Halden: "Shadow contact holding bearing. No closure rate on primary. Redundant reports closure rate low but nonzero."

Reyes: "Recommend immediate separation burn minimal—thrusters only, Halden's passive posture preserved."

Thessaly looked at Calder on the feed from the command deck. Calder looked at Okonkwo on Sharma's med gurney, conscious, eyes tracking, the data cradle in Brenner's hands, Cole still at the collar junction reeling relay pack while congestion bled down in the carrier tone.

He did not have unified sensor truth. He had a survivor, a sealed mission store, and a shadow contact that might be real at one range and fiction at another.

"Minimal separation burn," he said. "Thessaly executes from command. Away team to medical and ingestion bay. Park—buffer six for the core store. Koss certifies path. No merge."

"Aye, Captain."

The *Veil* separated from *Watcher's Mark* on thrusters. The damaged scout receded on forward display—peeled midships, three heat zones cooling to nothing, one life sign now absent because the life had crossed the collar and redundant array had never believed in her anyway.

On the command deck, Reyes logged final divergence: primary range to shadow contact long, redundant range medium, neither merged, both preserved in shadow log for later use.

Halden: "Shadow contact not closing on primary solution. Holding passive."

Calder stripped helmet in the airlock anteroom and handed the data cradle to Koss at the ingestion bay threshold. Brenner was already mounting the core in bay three under Park's buffer six initialization. Cole came in still wearing the relay pack, mug from Park's station clipped to her harness because of course it was, and ran checksum verification with Park over the narrow lane one last time before switching to local ship comm.

"Lane held," she said. Mostly to Park. Partly to the room.

Park: "Copy."

Okwelu took Okonkwo to medical bay without asking Calder for permission he would have given. Thessaly met him in the passageway outside ingestion with Walsh's meal packet and the compartmentalization packet tabs reordered again—chronology plus one new name now confirmed alive.

"Human victory's messy today," she said.

"Today it counts," Calder said.

---


The core store read completed at 1147 after four hours of segregated ingestion—Koss on hardware path, Brenner on pin seating when ghost reads appeared, Ortega and Reyes running lineage cross-check against buffer five without merge authority, Park maintaining buffer six isolation with Cole identifying congestion by sound at hours two and three when ingestion rate threatened narrow-path echo on the local relay bus.

Tanaka caught a timestamp stagger error at sector boundary nineteen before it propagated into subpartition B. Reyes slowed Ortega with a look when Ortega tried to read personnel strings and navigation strings in the same pass. Walsh delivered meal packets. Halden maintained defensive watch—shadow contact never closed on primary solution, never resolved on redundant, eventually fell below threshold on both and was logged as inconclusive rather than false.

Okwelu cleared Okonkwo for limited debrief at 1312—twenty minutes maximum, sitting only, no command deck.

Calder met her in medical bay. Park attended because narrow-path verification was part of her story. Thessaly stood at the bulkhead without entering the conversation until Calder glanced at her for time.

Okonkwo's hands shook once when she set the water bulb down. She did not apologize for it.

"Compartmentalization order came from Fleet Operations division tagged **Interior Mandate**," she said. "Not my chain. Not your brief. We mapped KR-13 segment four and KR-14 interior baseline under KISU-7 while official charts said decommission. Four of us on *Watcher's Mark*. Then the region stopped agreeing with itself."

Calder: "Your crew."

Okonkwo's face closed a fraction. "Lieutenant Reyes—no relation to your navigator—forward compartment. Lost on breach timeline primary array says was single event. Ensign Matu midships—evacuated on staggered schedule per her own log entries dated months apart from mine. Chief Kellan—" She stopped.

"Chief Kellan," Calder said.

"Vault watch. He opened midships to recover Matu when redundant array said she was already gone and primary said she was still aboard. He did not come back from the breach edge. I sealed the vault and sent distress on the narrow lane because it was the only comm path that still answered honestly when headers didn't."

Park: "Authentication on your manual overlay matched compartmentalization capture. You were on our reconstruction before we knew you were alive."

Okonkwo looked at him. "I was on your reconstruction before I knew you existed. That's the region."

Calder did not ask her to resolve sensor disagreement. He asked what they had earned.

"Recovery status," he said.

Koss on comm from ingestion: "Core store seventy-eight percent intact read. Mission logs, transit windows, personnel names—including Kellan and Matu last-known compartment entries with timestamp divergence flagged. Buffer six sealed. No merge with buffer five or KR-12 set."

Brenner: "Vault hardware verified. Core mount clean. Okonkwo's manual override chip preserved in evidence storage."

Park: "Buffer six checksum stable. Narrow path logged for distress and extraction window. No outbound fleet transmission."

Calder nodded once. "Log methodology in ship record. Medical holds Okonkwo per Okwelu's authority."

Okonkwo: "Matu might still be in the forward compartment if your primary array was right and your redundant was wrong. Kellan is in the midships vacuum if neither array is lying. Reyes—your lieutenant—is forward debris. I did not see him go. I saw the times disagree."

Calder held the fragment without forcing it into a single timeline.

"Understood."

Thessaly at the bulkhead: "Two minutes."

Okonkwo: "You pulled the store and me. That's more than Interior Mandate expected anyone to do."

Calder did not ask what Interior Mandate expected. He filed the name **Interior Mandate** beside **who ordered compartmentalization**—related, unproven, waiting.

Okwelu ended the debrief at fourteen minutes. Calder left medical bay with Park and Thessaly, passed Cole in Corridor C reeling cable from the portable relay pack, mug still clipped to her harness—the Helion Prime chip on the handle catching corridor light.

"Lane's quiet now," she said.

"Good work," Calder said.

She looked surprised he had said it aloud. Then she nodded and kept reeling.

In the galley alcove an hour later, Park refilled Halden's coffee. Halden did not joke. Park did not comment. Calder ate half a ration bar standing and reviewed buffer six summary strings on his tablet—not merged, not complete, enough.

The *Kestrel Veil* held at KR-14 vicinity with relay mostly lost and one narrow path proven again under load. A survivor aboard. A compartmentalized mission store recovered. A damaged scout drifting aft with three restraint clips and one empty helmet and evidence that its crew had not vanished as a single story.

Human victory: boarding team extracted Lieutenant Commander Okonkwo and the KISU-7 core store under contradictory sensor conditions, with every senior officer contributing—Thessaly on command timeline, Reyes and Ortega on array reconciliation, Halden on passive threat posture, Koss on umbilical and ingestion path, Park on narrow-path authority, Calder on the collar and the vault threshold.

The unanswered question was not whether Okonkwo was alive—she was.

The unanswered question was what had happened to everyone else on *Watcher's Mark*—Matu, Kellan, Lieutenant Reyes, and the rest of a crew whose disappearance did not fit one timestamp, one sensor, or one orderly Fleet narrative.

Calder would carry the survivor, the data, and the question forward.
