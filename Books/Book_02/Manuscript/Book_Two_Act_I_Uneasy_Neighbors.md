# Prelude — The Founding Exodus {.prologue}

The harvest banners had been going up since midmorning. By the time Yeva reached the main promenade of Serath District, they were fully strung — gold thread on old cord, the same set she had helped carry down from storage four autumns running, the corners reinforced with neat stitching by hands she did not know. Two of the longest panels were older than the rest: denser weave, a border mark the storage inventory still filed as *pre-district pattern, retain*. No one in the hanging crew could say what the mark had meant. They hung those panels first anyway, because that was how it was done.

She had walked this promenade her entire adult life. She knew which section of the north end had been poorly repaved and caught your toe if you weren't paying attention. She knew which vendors kept the best fermented root stock, which stall's dried fruit ran sweet rather than sharp, which of the music groups played the old autumn sequence with the correct slowing in the third movement. She knew all of it. She was walking through it for the last time, and she couldn't decide whether to pay more attention or less.

Maren ran ahead and came back. She was eight, and eight-year-olds moved at a different pace from the world around them. She returned now holding a grain figure bought from a vendor, dried stalks twisted into a human shape.

"It looks like Halcen," she said.

"It looks like a grain figure," Yeva said.

"Halcen has the same arms."

Yeva considered the figure more carefully. Maren was probably right. "Don't tell him that," she said.

Maren pocketed the figure and ran toward the lantern stalls.

The promenade was full in the way it always was on the first night of harvest week. Families with grain bundles moving toward the district hearth for the evening ceremony. Old couples in autumn colors. Academy students who had been let out early. The percussion group warming up in the east corner, the metallic opening sequence that everyone recognized from childhood on.

On the wall above the main arch, the oldest plaque in the district — bronze script on gray stone, the names of the seven surveyors who had established Serath District three hundred years prior. Yeva had walked under it most of her life without reading it. She stopped and read it now.

The names were ordinary. Not heroes, or at least not recorded as heroes. Just names, and a date, and a notation: *Survey cohort, autumn passage, established Serath.*

Autumn passage. She thought about that. She thought about tomorrow.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


She found Petra at the grain-bundle stalls, where Andris was arguing pleasantly with a vendor about whether the cord color was traditional or just what they had on hand. Their two boys were eating something fried and watching the percussion group settle in. The taller boy was already as tall as his father, which Yeva found alarming — the sort of change that takes years and somehow still arrives overnight.

Petra saw her and came over. They had been friends since they were twelve, had lived two buildings apart for the past nine years, had helped each other through hard years she was not going to unpack on the promenade.

"You're done, then," Petra said.

"This afternoon," Yeva said. "Torin's at the dock running numbers with Halcen. I wanted one more evening."

"One more evening," Petra said, without editorializing, which Yeva appreciated.

"Maren wanted to see the lanterns."

"Yes." Petra was watching her boys. Andris had won the cord argument and was wrapping the grain bundle correctly now, the traditional crossed pattern, the way his father used to do it. "Andris keeps bringing up that Valdorn cohort — the three who came back. Every time. As if you haven't heard."

"He means well."

"He does. He also can't imagine going himself, so he talks about the ones who came back." She paused. "Three out of nine. He keeps forgetting the other six."

Yeva nodded. She understood Andris and everyone staying the way you understand a language you grew up speaking — without having to think about it. History still mattered. Seven surveyors' names sat on the plaque above the arch, and they would sit there long after Yeva was gone. That weight was real. She just wanted more room ahead of her than the district had left to give — and she wanted Maren farther from a harvest that had started to mean more than banners and grain.

Petra asked about the route. Yeva told her about the survey continuation bearing, the hundred-forty-two-year estimate at convoy pace, what Halcen had said about the conditions beyond the catalogued edge. She tried to make it sound ordinary, which it was to her by now, and she could tell it was not ordinary to Petra at all by the way Petra's eyes stayed very still while she listened.

"Will you send word?" Petra asked. "When you arrive."

"As soon as we can establish a relay."

"My mother used to say that news comes first to those who stop moving." Petra considered it. "She meant it as a criticism."

"I always thought it was an argument for going," Yeva said.

Petra smiled, small and real. "She might have meant it both ways."

The lanterns came on along the vendor rows, all at once, the district's timing making it look deliberate and beautiful, and the promenade shifted from an evening crowd to something warmer and older. Maren appeared out of the gathering with her grain figure and three other children behind her, all of them serious about something. The percussion group began the autumn sequence. Yeva recognized the third movement coming and felt the weight of it in her chest, and let it be there, and did not try to name it.

When families began moving toward the district hearth and others were peeling off toward home, Petra found Yeva in the thinning crowd and pressed a small grain bundle into her hands. Not the presentation kind, the formal hearth type. A small one, made with cord from Petra's own stall, gold thread worked into the binding.

"For the new place," Petra said. "So you have one."

Yeva held it. She thought about the distance between here and the survey continuation zone, and the grain figure in Maren's pocket, and the seven names above the arch.

"Preparation honors those who come after us," Petra said. The traditional harvest season close.

Yeva said it back. She meant it as the same thing and as something different, and both were true, and neither of them said so.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


Arvos Kastal's office was on the third level of the district records building, the oldest building still in administrative use in Serath District, its walls a patchwork of four construction eras. He had been a district registrar for seventeen years — long enough that the work no longer felt like a job so much as a habit.

He set his tea aside and worked through the departure certification forms.

Thirty-seven families. Four unaffiliated individuals. One collective agricultural unit with heritage grain stock they'd declined to identify by variety until after departure, which he'd negotiated down to *varieties to be documented in-transit* without breaking the form's requirements. A survey consortium incorporated six weeks ago specifically to meet the legal classification for departure registration. He had seen this maneuver before. It was technically correct.

The destination field gave him pause. The form expected a registered colony designation or an authorized survey corridor reference. These families had neither — only a bearing and a survey continuation zone notation, Dominion standard for the edge of the charts and what lay beyond them. He had seen that notation twice before in seventeen years, both times with much smaller groups.

He wrote: *Provisional Survey Continuation Zone, uncatalogued sectors, bearing as noted.*

Outside, through the wall, the harvest festival percussion was audible — the opening sequence, the one everyone knew. He considered going down to the promenade. He rarely did. The forms needed finishing before the cohort's dock time in the morning.

He had spoken with maybe a third of the departing families over the past few weeks and noticed they were not who he might have predicted. Some were young with children, as expected for a frontier cohort. But others were established in Serath — a woman who had run the agricultural coordination office here for eleven years, a man named Halcen who had surveyed three systems on Dominion commission and had now decided he wanted to survey somewhere the Dominion was not managing. Halcen had come in for a records consultation rather than a departure form, wanting to see the history of prior voluntary departures from the region. Arvos had let him read. Halcen made notes on a slate and thanked him without saying what he'd been looking for.

In the supplementary field — not required, used at the registrar's discretion — he wrote: *Voluntary departure during harvest festival period. Timing noted as intentional per cohort preference.*

He had first written *consistent with historical colonial practice* and changed *practice* to *tradition,* because practice sounded temporary. The departure had happened. It was tradition now.

Once, years earlier, he had pulled an older seasonal index while reconciling a damaged file and found harvest-remembrance entries dated before Serath's survey founding. The index did not explain them. He had closed it and gone back to work. The forms in front of him did not require that kind of curiosity.

He stamped the forms, set them in the completed tray, poured more tea, and went back to work.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


By dawn the promenade was being swept. The gold banners stayed up — harvest week still had four days to run — but the vendor stalls were closed and the percussion instruments packed away, the district holding the particular quiet of a place recently full.

Yeva reached the dock with Maren in a carry pod on her back and found Torin and Halcen already deep in the cargo allocation, moving between vessels with slates and the economy of movement that came from a very early start.

The dock was large and built to last rather than to impress. Old stone facing on the outer walls, newer composite panels on the loading bays, a century of modification making the surface textures inconsistent in the way of a structure continuously used rather than planned. Worn paint markings on the floor in six different generations of colors. Forty-three vessels in varying states of readiness, from fully loaded to still receiving final crates.

Halcen saw her and held up two fingers. Two issues. He'd get to them. She nodded.

He was an unhurried man, which she had taken initially for slowness and recalibrated to precision. He moved through problems at the speed of someone who had categorized them before acting, which meant he rarely acted twice. He had done this kind of thing before, not this exact convoy but this kind of departure, the crossing from catalogued space into something without a map key. He had surveyed beyond one edge, returned to write up his findings for the Dominion record, and then at some point decided he preferred the uncatalogued part.

She had asked him once why this cohort specifically.

"The agricultural unit is carrying heritage grain varieties they won't identify," he had said. "That means they're serious about a stable colony, not a survey pass. And you've got children. I prefer serious." He had paused, then added, quieter: "The catalogued edge has been inconsistent for a while — returns that won't settle. Something is out there. I would rather put years between my watch and that."

Maren woke up and demanded to be set down and immediately located another girl her age near a stack of cargo trunks. Within a few minutes they were sitting on the same trunk, discussing something with the seriousness of children who have not yet learned to be indirect about friendships.

A man with a scar along his jaw caught Yeva's eye from across the dock — he had gotten the scar in a survey tunnel and had a manner to him that said he understood physical risk clearly and had decided this risk was preferable to others. He nodded once, a greeting that needed nothing back.

Torin came over and stood beside her.

"Halcen says we're on schedule," he said.

"Good."

"First twenty vessels fully loaded. The last section needs about two more hours."

Yeva looked at the assembled convoy, the various hulls, the families around them — loading, checking, adjusting, talking, standing quietly. Parents with children who didn't fully understand what was happening and would remember the dock without knowing it as the dock. Old hands who moved like they had left before. Young couples who had never left anything.

Whatever came next, these were the people.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


At midday the district continuity officer arrived.

She came with two assistants and a transport cart and the brisk manner of someone with a full afternoon ahead. Her name was Dunara Vel. Yeva had not known this was coming until Halcen mentioned it that morning — he had said the district sent someone for departures over thirty families, that it had been done this way for a long time.

The cart carried forty-three grain bundles. Dried harvest stalks, gold cord, a district seal stamped on each binding.

Dunara Vel read the names from a list and the families came forward one by one and received their bundle and said the appropriate phrase and she marked them off. It took a while. The dock had arranged itself into an informal gathering, the way crowds assembled for a shared occasion without being directed. Children climbed on cargo trunks. Someone produced a blanket for an old man sitting down.

When Yeva's family was called she stepped forward with Maren beside her, Torin behind. Maren received the bundle with complete seriousness. Dunara Vel asked if Yeva wanted to say anything — apparently a tradition, offered but not required.

"We're grateful for the years here," Yeva said. "We hope the district continues well."

Dunara Vel nodded and marked the list.

When the last name was marked she faced the assembled cohort and read the departure acknowledgment. She read it clearly and without decoration, the voice of someone who had prepared for this and found it worth preparing for: "Serath District notes your voluntary departure and wishes your cohort success in your survey continuation. You carry with you the grain of the harvest in the year of your going. Whatever you build from here, you began here."

It was the harvest season language, different words, same movement — the recognition that preparation and departure were connected, that what you carried into the future you had first grown in the ground behind you. One line in the older response still used a verb no one conjugated in ordinary speech anymore. Children learned it by ear. Adults said it without asking when the wording had last been current.

Someone in the crowd began the harvest response and the others joined, the traditional completion, the words everyone knew from childhood. Yeva said them with the rest.

Behind the barrier rail where visitors stood, the people who had come to watch did so without speeches. Yeva found Petra and Andris. Petra's hand was in her husband's. She looked steady. They had said what they needed to say the night before, and Petra was a person who did not need to say things twice.

Yeva nodded once. Petra nodded back.

The loading resumed.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


They lifted two hours before dark.

Yeva found a viewpoint window on the second deck and watched. Serath District resolved into a planetary pattern below her, city lights and agricultural grids and the particular color of the sea basin to the south that she had always liked from orbit on the few times she had left and returned. Harvest week's lights were visible on the night side, the festival still running, gold thread banners still up over streets she had walked her whole life.

Maren came and stood beside her. She was carrying both grain figures now, her own and the bundle from Petra.

"When do we get there?" Maren asked.

"A long time," Yeva said. "Longer than I'll see the end of. Halcen says about a hundred and forty years. Your children's children, if we're careful."

Maren frowned at the number the way eight-year-olds frowned at anything that would not fit in a pocket. "What's there when we get there?"

"We don't know yet," Yeva said. "We'll figure it out. That's partly why we're going — so they have room to figure it out somewhere the harvest can't follow yet."

Maren seemed to find the second answer more usable than the first. She leaned against the viewport and watched the planet turn below them.

Yeva thought: *each year at harvest we will do something to mark it. Not this, but something.* A shared meal in a corridor. This bundle on a table that moved with them. Someone saying *this is the time of year we left*, and the children asking why. The first answers would be honest and incomplete: because we wanted something that wasn't behind us. Because the harvest had started to mean more than the district would put on a form. Children born after Serath would do the same marking without quite knowing why that time of year felt weighted — or what they had been carried away from. Eventually it would have a shape, and a name, and a story that got the facts approximately right and the feeling exactly right — and forgot the danger under the banners.

She did not know what it would look like. She thought it would probably start small, as everything does. She thought it would probably last.

The harvest lights disappeared around the planet's curve.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


The survey continuation zone read empty on every instrument they carried, which Halcen had predicted and which still took several days to fully believe.

Yeva contributed a notation to the shared log on day fourteen: *survey continuation zone, first agricultural assessment, heritage stock reports positive propagation conditions. Recommend formal record when stable landing site identified.* She was a teacher, not a surveyor, but she was beginning to understand that in this convoy the roles distributed differently, and that record-keeping was now one of hers.

Maren made marks in the margin of her notation. Yeva left them in.

On day seventeen, Torin and the convoy coordinator and two members of the survey consortium spent eight hours with their charts and produced a preliminary bearing adjustment. The edge of the Dominion's catalogued space was behind them. Ahead, the charts had a notation that translated roughly as *not yet surveyed, conditions unknown, proceed with caution* — standard edge-of-chart language that had lived in this region for longer than anyone in the convoy had been alive.

Halcen looked at the chart and said: "Someone drew a line here. It doesn't mean there's anything on it."

"It means the Dominion stopped cataloguing," Torin said.

"That's what I said," Halcen said.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


Three months later, Arvos Kastal received a routing notice that another cohort was forming in Serath District.

Seven families and a freelance pilot cooperative. Same destination notation — he recognized the format because he had written it.

He thought of the autumn cohort sometimes: the grain bundles, and the line from Dunara Vel's speech that he'd heard read the same way at every departure and never quite listened to before — *whatever you build from here, you began here.*

He added one more note to the autumn cohort's file, not required, not standard: *cohort carried harvest grain into survey continuation. timing appears intentional.* His supervisor would probably change a word or two.

He left it for her and started the new forms.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


In the one hundred forty-first year the survey consortium found a system.

None of the people at the common table had stood on Serath's dock. Halcen's name was a watch rotation and a margin habit. Torin's bearing notes lived in copies of copies. Yeva's log hand was taught in the children's archive hour as *founding teacher, autumn cohort*, which was accurate and not enough.

What happened was that the duty surveyor on first watch — a woman named Kell who had Halcen's patience without having known his face — said to the navigator quietly that the spectral return on the third body looked promising, and the navigator checked, and said yes, and sent the notation to the convoy coordinator, who woke the leads.

By the time the leads gathered there were seven people standing around the display arguing calmly about what the readings meant. Sava came in late from the agricultural bay — Maren's great-granddaughter, forty-one years old, carrying a grain figure that had outlived everyone who remembered Serath firsthand. She looked at the display with the patience of a convoy that had stopped pretending the wait was temporary three generations ago.

"Is that it?" Sava asked.

"Probably," Kell said.

"It's small."

"They all start small," someone said, and nobody could remember who had said it first in the family line.

Sava set the grain figure on the table beside the display — not a child's gesture, and still the same gesture. Kell looked at it and then at the display. "We'll need a name for the record," she said. She said it practically.

"We'll figure it out," the coordinator said.

"Now," Kell said. "Whatever we call it, the first notation sets it. You rename later if you need to, but the first notation stays in the record."

The people around the table were quiet for a moment. All of them had been born in corridors. Serath was a plaque story, a cedar smell nobody could verify, a third movement slowed on festival nights because the archive said that was how it went.

Sava opened the sealed instruction Yeva had filed in year twelve and that Maren had made her swear to open only at first candidate system. The slate was worn. The hand was steady.

It named the district the earliest surveyors had come from, three centuries before Serath, the old name carved on plaques most people walked past without reading. Yeva had written: *So the record can say we came from somewhere. So they know we were getting our children past a harvest that had started to mean more than we could put on a form.*

Sava read the name aloud.

She did not say it because she had smelled the cedar. She said it because the instruction said to, and because a hundred forty-one years past the survey edge the things the first generation had tried to memorize had not stayed, and the record was the only part that might — including the part about why the leaving had been timed to harvest at all.

Kell wrote it down.

She wrote: *Year 141 survey notation: candidate system, bearing as recorded. Designated provisional. Heritage grain stock intact. Founding cohort descent present. Recommend formal assessment.*

She did not write: *This is the beginning of something.* She did not need to. Outside the viewports, the new system hung in the dark — small, and already named.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


In Serath District, the autumn cohort's departure forms went into the seasonal records under the harvest festival notation, cross-referenced against three prior departures and the Valdorn corridor cohort. Accurate. Complete. And missing the one thing they couldn't record — that forty-three families had carried the harvest's grain into uncatalogued space to get their children past a harvest that was already becoming something else, and would remember this as the year they began. What they'd left behind would remember it as the year they went.

The gold banners came down at the end of harvest week, folded carefully, stored with cedar oil, ready for next autumn.

On the new world, a hundred forty-one years and one system away, Maren's grain figure sat on a shelf that did not exist yet, in a room that would one day be called the first permanent structure. Harvest would come again there, as it came everywhere. They would mark it, because people mark things — and because they had already been marking it for generations without a world under them, long enough for the leaving to become the story and the danger under the banners to thin into tradition.

They would not know the banners were already going up again on the other side of the survey edge.

Neither side saw where the other had gone. Both were certain they remembered it correctly.

**IRON HARROW — SECTOR V21, EASTERN REACH**
ENGAGEMENT WATCH

The engagement began without a challenge.

Vex was reading damage assessments from V14 when Klimov's board lit in the orange band and Klimov said, "Contact," in the same voice he used for maintenance warnings and vessel traffic. The voice did not convey what the plot was showing.

The return on the passive board would not resolve.

It had arrived on the eastern bearing with no identification structure, no navigation packet, no approach vector consistent with a ship making normal transit. The mass return was wrong. Not an instrument error — the shape itself was wrong, refusing to settle into a size. Vex had watched passive boards for twenty years. A return was supposed to confirm itself: settle into a class, hold a curve, declare its geometry so the watching ship could categorize it and move on.

This one did not.

"Identification?" Vex asked.

"Nothing in standard encoding," Klimov said, not looking up from the board. "No challenge response. No navigation state on any broadcast channel."

"Distance?"

"Closing. Slowly." A pause. "Commander, the mass return is changing profile. Not a ranging error — the shape is changing."

Vex stood from the command chair.

The command compartment aboard Iron Harrow was old enough that the rail along the secondary plot had been worn to a slightly different texture where officers over the years had rested their left hands during difficult watches. Vex had a habit of not using that rail, had always done his hard thinking standing rather than leaning, and the habit had become part of how the crew read him: Vex standing still at the plot meant the situation was unusual.

Major Petrov had moved to the secondary plot without being asked. His gloves were off, laid on the rail with the precision of a man preparing for serious arithmetic. Petrov before difficult numbers always removed his gloves. In four years Vex had not asked him about it, and Petrov had not explained it, and it was one of the small reliable signals that made a ship function.

"Drevin," Vex said. "Weapons live. Hold fire."

"Live, Commander. No lock." Drevin's tone was flat in the specific way that meant he was working the problem rather than reacting to it. "The return will not hold still enough for a targeting solution."

"Continue attempting. Log each attempt."

"Logging."

The contact came within thirty thousand kilometers before the first effect reached Iron Harrow's hull. It was not an impact in any sense the damage control dictionary used that word. Section Four reported a distributed pressure event across a forty-meter span of exterior surface — hull plating compressed inward over the full length of the section, uniformly, as if something very large had passed closely and pressed against the hull without targeting it. Not a beam. Not a kinetic round. Pressure, extended across an area too wide to be a weapon.

"Section Four," the damage circuit reported. "Exterior surface deformation. Amber status. No breach."

"Seal Section Four," Vex said. "Engineering on the loop. Full structural lock."

"Sealing."

The contact had not adjusted bearing to fire. Had not illuminated weapons in any spectrum Klimov could identify. Had continued on its original track — the same track it had been holding when Klimov first called contact — as if Iron Harrow were terrain. As if the cruiser were a ridge to be routed around, a feature of the landscape it was moving through.

"I cannot determine whether this is one contact or multiple in close proximity," Klimov said. He had the steady methodical cadence of an operator who had been trained to separate uncertainty from alarm, and it was holding now. "The return geometry is inconsistent. One pass reads it as a single mass. The next reads it as distributed mass. It is not behaving like a single hull."

"Log it as stated," Vex said. "Note both possibilities. Continue track."

Petrov had the archive comparison running. He turned toward Vex without speaking, which was how Petrov announced that he had found something he was still weighing.

"What do you have," Vex said.

"V14 contact, eighteen months ago. V18 contact, two years before that." Petrov aligned the archive entries beside the current passive return on the secondary plot. The mass curve shapes were wrong in the same specific way — ambiguous, shifting, occupying more bearing arc than a consistent hull should. "Similarity notation only. I cannot confirm a match. The return characteristics are consistent across all three events."

"Total archive entries with these characteristics?"

Petrov worked for a moment. "Twenty-one entries. The oldest is eleven years."

The second pressure event reached Section Seven. Same character: distributed, uniform, extended across a span that no precision weapon would occupy. Hull plating compressed along the outer surface, not reaching the structural layer.

"Section Seven, structural warning," the damage circuit said. "Margin at eighty-six percent nominal. Holding."

"Seal Seven. Structural lock full. Section Seven crew accountability — forward of frame thirty."

The damage circuit acknowledged.

The accountability report came back in under a minute: full crew forward, no personnel in the compromised section.

Vex watched the bearing trace on the main plot. The contact had not changed course. Not to engage, not to evade, not to orient toward Iron Harrow's weapons or command spaces or propulsion. In the time it had taken to absorb hull contact twice, ready weapons, begin damage control, and attempt to resolve a sensor return that refused to stabilize, the contact had been on one line.

It was going somewhere.

Iron Harrow was in the path.

He let that sit for a moment before he acted on it. The contact had not been looking for a fight. Had not, as far as his sensors could determine, looked at Iron Harrow at all. The hull contact had not been fire. It had been proximity.

He had fought Dominion ships and foreign registry vessels and once, early in his career, a piracy action off the fringe of V9 that had been brief and violent and thoroughly catalogued in his service record. He knew what it felt like when something was trying to destroy you. He knew the sensor signature of intent.

This was not that.

"Drevin," Vex said. "Prepare evasive maneuvering. Off this bearing. Minimum signature. No weapons discharge."

Drevin said, "Commander," in the tone that communicated disagreement without stating it, and then started the maneuvering calculation. He was good at that part — knowing the difference between an objection worth pressing and a decision already made.

"Petrov," Vex said. "Audio of the contact's emission signature. Pipe it low."

Petrov reached for the signals panel, then paused with his hand on the control. He looked at Vex.

"Low," Vex confirmed.

Petrov opened the audio render.

The bridge held through three seconds of it. Four. Five.

Drevin's hands stopped on the maneuvering board.

"Close it," Vex said.

Petrov closed the channel.

Silence. Real silence — the kind that follows something that has no easy category.

Drevin's hands resumed the maneuvering calculation after a moment. He did not ask about the sound. He looked at the plot and worked.

Iron Harrow came off the contact's bearing in a slow arc, minimum signature, no weapons discharge. The Peligar return did not adjust. Its bearing trace continued past the space the cruiser had vacated and kept going, still cycling through that ambiguous profile that refused to confirm a vessel count, still doing whatever it was doing on its original heading.

The mass return drifted past on the passive plot.

Not satisfied with the exchange. Not frustrated. Simply continuing.

"Passive track," Vex said. "No illumination. Full emissions profile recorded at maximum resolution. Audio intercept recorded from initial contact — confirm duration."

"Thirty-one seconds captured before we came off bearing," Petrov said. "Recording complete."

"Keep the track active until it passes below sensor threshold."

"Aye, Commander."

Vex stood at the main plot and watched the return drift away. In twenty-two minutes it fell below the passive sensor edge and the bearing was clean.

"Contact lost below passive threshold," Klimov said. "Bearing clear."

The damage circuit had Section Four at amber stable and Section Seven at amber holding. No breach. Full crew accountability confirmed. No casualties.

Vex looked at the twenty-one archive entries on Petrov's secondary plot and the two new ones they had just added.

Twenty-three contacts across eleven years across three sectors.

Eleven years of reports routed to V21 command and Strategic Security, and somewhere in the early entries someone had attributed the anomalous returns to instrument error or unknown vessel types and moved on, and somewhere around the seventh or eighth entry someone had started a pattern notation that grew slowly into a recognizable shape.

He had read the full archive once, when he took command of Iron Harrow and asked to be briefed on the sector's unresolved contacts. Then he had asked Petrov what the strategic guidance said. Petrov told him: observation and documentation, avoid provocation, technical data collection, do not share outside authorized channels.

He had accepted that guidance then.

He was looking at the twenty-third entry now.

"Log everything," Vex said. "Full detail. Return characteristics, emissions profile, audio intercept, hull damage report, bearing trace from initial contact to sensor loss. V21 command and Strategic Security, both copies urgent-flagged."

"Classification?" Klimov asked.

"No classification," Vex said. "Log observed. Nothing added."

Klimov logged observed.

<p align="center" class="scene-break" markdown="0">&#10086;</p>


**IRON HARROW — BRIEFING COMPARTMENT**
POST-CONTACT ASSESSMENT

Petrov had both hands flat on the table.

That was the tell that preceded arithmetic that would not produce a clean outcome. Vex had been reading it for four years and had learned to adjust his expectations: when Petrov's hands went flat, the numbers were going to require honest engagement with what they meant.

Lieutenant Sorova from Engineering gave the structural summary first — precise, without editorial. Sections Four and Seven: exterior deformation, hull plating, no penetration into the structural layer. Repair category three. Four days at transit speed with materials on hand. Park almost missed it.

The passive archive comparison ran as background during observation windows — routine, automated, the kind of process that filled the long middle hours of a watch with null results and occasionally surfaced something worth a closer look. Faint at first — faint enough that Park almost tagged it for the null archive and moved on. He stacked the returns. The second stack showed it stronger. The third showed it stronger still, and by the third stack there was no honest reading of the data that said sensor noise.

Something was out there.

"Captain," Park said.

Calder looked over from the corridor plot. In eighteen months Calder had developed the habit of checking the plot when the ship had nothing to report — a habit Park had come to recognize as a sign that the captain was watching the larger picture rather than waiting for someone to bring him problems. The spectral range ran from frequencies his instruments could resolve clearly down into bands where the rendering went approximate and then uncertain. This had been reduced. The original, at source, was larger.

Ortega had moved to the sensor station without being asked. Thessaly crossed the command deck in the deliberate way that meant she was already working the problem before anyone said it out loud.

Park looked at the signal. He had been working communications for eleven years. He knew distress signals the way he knew his own name — the formats, the frequencies, the patterns of people in trouble, the specific character of an emergency broadcast from a civilian vessel that had lost engines in deep space and was using everything it had to say: we are here, come now, please. He had responded to five actual distress calls in his career and trained for dozens.

The recording did not feel like any of those.

It felt like something talking.

"My honest read is that it is doing something," Park said. "It is not random. It is not noise. The pattern is too complex for noise and too structured for panic." He paused. "But I cannot tell you what it is doing. The instruments can only capture part of it. The part I can capture does not map to anything in the library."

The mass return cycled through its inconsistent profile states: large, very large, ambiguous, shifting back. Nothing in Ortega's comparison database produced a class-confident return.

The audio signal remained on the passive board.

Park kept the audio channel closed and watched the waveform instead.

He was thinking about the layers in it. Genuine layering — not one signal modulated across frequencies, but multiple things happening simultaneously at different spectral addresses, as if the source was doing several kinds of signaling at once and had not bothered to separate them. He had seen ships use multi-band transmission for different receivers in different locations. That was organized: one band for one destination, another for another. This was not organized that way.

This was as if every available channel carried the same source signal simultaneously. As if the source did not have the concept of directing a signal to a specific receiver.

He noted that observation in the technical supplemental without forming a conclusion from it. He did not have enough information for a conclusion.

Twenty-three minutes after Ortega logged the contact's bearing shift, the mass return turned away from the frontier edge and moved into the fringe on a heading that took it beyond the passive sensor threshold over the following half hour.

The signal fell off with the distance.

Then it was gone.

"Contact departed below passive threshold," Ortega said. "Passive board clear."

The deck released the particular quality of attention that a watch under observation required. Brenner checked his helm card. Thessaly made a note at the XO station. Park looked at the empty waveform panel and then closed it.

The contact had been present on the bearing for an hour and forty-nine minutes. It had moved parallel to the frontier edge the entire time. It had not approached. It had not altered bearing when — as far as Park could determine — it might have detected Kestrel Veil's position. It had not addressed any signal toward them.

It had simply continued.

"Nothing to relay yet?" Park asked.

"Nothing ready to relay," Calder said. "We have a provisional log entry and a mass return that never confirmed. We do not have a reportable contact."

Park closed the display. The archive had the intercept. The mass return data was saved. The provisional classification was in the local log.

He sat with the clean passive board and thought about what he had heard.

Park runs the language work. Thessaly owns procedure. Calder owns field judgment on the Fleet side. Delegate Senka, do you confirm Dominion authority for this session?"

"I confirm provisional authority," Senka said. "Strategic Security retains review on anything touching Peligar classification. Science Directorate retains review on linguistic method. Civil Government retains the right to end the venue."

Orlen's mouth tightened at the word *Peligar*. Not surprise. Caution. As if the name itself were something you did not hand across a table without counting the cost.

"Then we begin," Rowan said. "Header stack first. Who may speak. Who records. What may be said on a shared channel."

Park put the draft on the wall display.

It looked like a checklist written by people who did not trust each other and were trying to survive that fact.

Speaker turns were timed. Interruptions required a named request. Raw audio recording was restricted to language work sessions only, and only with dual consent. Operational status exchanges used text headers with controlled vocabulary. Either side could suspend a session without explanation beyond *protocol hold*. Neither side could claim the other's silence as agreement.

Senka read it twice. "We accept the structure. We reject Fleet's request for complete raw session audio. Summary logs only, except during authorized language work."

"Fleet Council will dislike that," Rowan said.

"Strategic Security will dislike everything else," Senka said. "Choose which dislike you can live with."

Rowan did not argue the point long. "Accepted for Session One. Revisit after language work proves useful or dangerous."

"Both are likely," Orlen said, without looking up from her notebook.

Thessaly almost smiled. She did not. Park saw the almost and logged it the way he logged every useful human signal in a room: Thessaly respected competence even when it arrived wearing the other side's coat.

They spent forty minutes on classification tags for shared traffic. Fleet wanted a clean ladder: routine, elevated, urgent, critical. Dominion wanted historical precedent tags and inheritance markers that Fleet's forms did not understand. Park translated between them until they had a usable compromise: urgency grades on top, provenance notes underneath, and a hard rule that no shared header could invent meaning the sending side had not certified.

"If we do not know," Calder said, "we say we do not know."

"My instructor called that the first honesty," Orlen said. "People forget it under pressure."

Vorin glanced at Senka before he spoke. "SSD will review every Peligar-adjacent header before it leaves Dominion custody."

"Fleet Intelligence will do the same on our side," Rowan said. "Suspicion is not an insult today. It is the working condition."

Senka poured more tea for her own side. Nobody asked Fleet to drink it. Park kept his coffee where it was.

Calder asked for a walk-out drill before they closed the first block. Thessaly ran it: Fleet suspends, Dominion acknowledges, both sides stop talking, no one treats silence as consent. Then they reversed it. The pedestals recorded the acknowledgments. The room practiced ending itself without drama and without pretending the ending was temporary friendship.

"Again," Calder said.

They ran it again. The second time was cleaner. Park noticed Vorin's shoulders drop a fraction after the second successful suspend. People revealed more than instruments. Even here.

By the end of the first hour they had a brittle protocol that existed on both systems and pleased no one completely.

Courtesy held the room together. Nobody called it trust.

"Language work begins after the break," Thessaly said. "Park and the Directorate team. Command presence reduced to on-call. We keep the veto open."

Senka stood. "We will not gift you a dictionary of what you do not yet understand. We will help you speak to us without lying by accident. That is the offer."

"That is enough for today," Calder said.

Rowan's channel closed on a short acknowledgment. The pedestals went dark. The room smelled like tea and Fleet coffee and the particular dust of a station that had not been designed for diplomacy.

Park closed his slate and looked at Orlen's notebook. She wrote in a dense hand, lines neat enough to be archival.

Calder stopped beside him on the way out. "You are carrying the distress tag harder than the packet needs."

"I put the wrong label on something people will keep quoting," Park said. "That is my job failing in public."

"Then fix the label when the room can hold a better one," Calder said. "Do not invent a better one to feel cleaner."

Park nodded. That was the instruction he needed, and the one he disliked.

"Ready?" he asked Orlen.

"My grandmother used to say readiness is what you bring when the room is already wrong," Orlen said. "So yes."

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — LANGUAGE SUITE**
HUMAN CHANNEL WORK

The language suite was smaller than the protocol room and better for the work. One shared table. Two wall boards. A dual-consent recorder that stayed dark until both sides keyed it live. A tea warmer on the Dominion end. A Fleet coffee urn that Park had insisted on after walking through the pantry inventory and deciding he would not negotiate grammar on Dominion hospitality alone.

Orlen began with nouns that should have been easy.

They were not.

"Ship," Park said, pointing to the schematic on the board.

Orlen wrote the Dominion term, then paused. "Your word covers hull, crew, and mission as one object. Ours separates the vessel from the obligation. If you say *ship* when you mean *duty*, we will hear the wrong thing."

Vorin added a second line. "Also: your *scout* is a mission class. Our nearest word is a watch designation. They do not map."

Park logged both notes. He felt the old pleasure of a clean problem and the newer shame under it. Back on the *Kestrel*, he had tagged a signal as provisional distress because he had lacked a better bin. That tag was still in the Fleet archive. He could feel it the way you feel a wrong label on a cable you have to keep using.

"All right," he said. "We build a shared list for human speech. No poetry. No cleverness. If a word lies, we cut it."

Orlen nodded once. "Accepted."

They worked for two hours without spectacle and with constant friction.

Military terms were the worst. Fleet *engage* meant open hostilities under rules. Dominion *engage* in inherited usage could mean take up a duty, confront a problem, or begin a watch. Fleet *stand down* was a clear order. Dominion had three related phrases depending on whether the stand-down was temporary, honorable, or forced by higher authority. Park's first draft of a joint alert phrase nearly authorized a Dominion ship to cease watch when Fleet meant cease fire.

"That one kills people," Vorin said, flat.

"Yes," Park said. He struck it. "We write it again."

They rebuilt the phrase until both sides could say *hold weapons* and mean the same physical action. Then they tested it by having Orlen speak the Dominion line and Park speak the Fleet line into the dual-consent recorder, then swapping and listening for drift. There was drift. They cut it again.

Coffee and tea stayed on the table as work tools, not conversation pieces. Orlen drank between entries. Park refilled once when his mug emptied and went back to the board. Thessaly looked in once, listened to three minutes of argument about the word *neighbor*, and left without comment.

"Neighbor," Orlen said, "implies shared boundary and mutual recognition. We have not granted that."

"Fleet used it in public statements," Park said. "I know. It was political language. For this room, we can use *adjacent traffic* or *contacting party*."

"Contacting party," Vorin said. "Less claim."

Park wrote it down. Small usable progress. Thin trust.

False friends kept appearing. Fleet *secure* meant lock down and protect. Dominion *secure* in one older register meant make certain through historical precedent. Fleet *clear* meant empty of threat. Dominion *clear* could mean accounted for in the record. Park started a separate column titled *looks shared, is not* and filled it faster than he liked.

"Your people invent words when the old ones fail," Orlen said during a pause. It was not quite accusation. "Ours keep the old ones and make the room fit them."

"Both habits break under pressure," Park said.

"Yes. That is why we are here."

Senka visited at the ninety-minute mark and stood in the doorway long enough to remind them the room had political owners. "No historical speculation on the open board," she said. "No Founders' Week comparisons. No shared-origin talk. If it arises, you end the line and note *out of scope*."

"Understood," Park said.

After she left, Orlen said, "She is not wrong to fear contamination. Words travel farther than people intend."

"Fleet has the same fear in the other direction," Park said. "We record you to gain advantage. You teach us to make us useful. Both can be true and still leave us needing the glossary."

Vorin almost smiled. It did not become one. "Old Commander Petran told my class that useful enemies are still enemies. He was not a cheerful man."

"Sounds accurate," Park said.

They broke for a short meal in the anteroom. Dominion rations were denser and more heavily seasoned than Fleet standard. Park ate what was offered and did not comment. Orlen watched him the way a professional watches a subject without admitting the watch. Vorin talked about academy speech drills: inherited quotation as discipline, not decoration. Park talked about relay poker and how he played badly on purpose to keep people talking. Orlen found that interesting enough to write down. Park pretended not to notice.

"Your people ask what works now," Vorin said. "Ours ask what held before. Both questions can be honest. Both can miss the thing in front of them."

"Today the thing in front of us is human speech," Park said. "I can live with missing the rest until the glossary stops lying."

"Can you?" Orlen asked.

Park thought about the fringe recording and the provisional distress flag and Calder refusing a false category. "I can try."

After the meal they ran a live turn-taking drill: Park speaking Fleet terms into the open channel, Orlen answering in Dominion, Vorin catching substitutions before they hardened. Three times they nearly accepted a phrase that sounded shared and meant opposite obligations. Three times they cut it. Progress felt like subtraction more than invention, which Park found oddly comforting. Communications work had always been about what the channel did to the message. Here the channel was culture itself.

By midday they had a working list of sixty-three shared terms for human operational speech: hold weapons, protocol hold, status request, status reply, unknown origin, unresolved mass, provisional category, walk out, resume, map later. Map later was Calder's phrase, added when he stopped by and refused to let them invent certainty about charts they had not yet agreed to show.

"Do not schedule the map conversation inside a language session," Calder said. "Language first. Geography when the words can carry it."

"Yes, Captain," Orlen said. Professional respect, not warmth.

Park noticed she used *Captain* the way Fleet used it: as a role, not a courtesy flourish. He logged that too, privately, because conversational structure was his other instrument.

When they paused, Orlen closed her notebook and looked at Park's board work.

"You hear hesitation," she said. "That is useful. My instructor said speech is half what is said and half what is delayed."

"I remember conversations," Park said. "Who changed order. Who stopped joking. That kind of thing."

"Then you will do well with us," Orlen said. "And poorly with what is not us."

He knew what she meant. He did not ask for the Peligar name to be explained. Not yet. The protocol room had already taught him that names were currency.

"Human channels first," he said.

"Human channels first," she agreed.

Vorin keyed the dual-consent recorder off and packed the morning slate copies into separate custody sleeves, Fleet and Dominion. "SSD will want to read every term," he said. "They will look for soft openings."

"Let them look," Park said. "If a term is soft, we already failed."

Orlen poured tea for the afternoon session and set Park's coffee urn back within his reach without comment. Civilization texture, not friendship. He took it as the respect it was.

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — ANALYSIS COMPARTMENT**
INTERCEPT REVIEW

They brought the *Kestrel* recording into the analysis compartment in the afternoon, under dual consent and dual custody.

Park had not wanted to be the one to open it. He opened it anyway. Shame did not get to choose his work.

The waveform had stayed in his head since the fringe intercept: overlapping registers, intensity that looked like group pain if you forced a human analogy, structure that refused the analogy once you stopped needing the analogy to feel better. Ortega's sensor package sat on one board. Petrov's Dominion analysis notes, sanitized by SSD for shared review, sat on the other. Vorin handled the Dominion side of the render. Park handled Fleet audio. Thessaly stood where she could see both faces. Calder sat at the end of the table with his arms folded, refusing to lean toward either conclusion before it earned itself.

A third relay line ran open beside Rowan's: Fleet Communications Command, validation desk, a voice Park would have known blind after ten years.

"Holt, you have the Ortega package?" Park asked.

"I have it," Dana Holt said. "I've had it since last night. I was hoping you'd call before the Dominion side told you what it meant."

"I'm calling now."

"Barely." A pause, drier than the line needed. "You look terrible. I can hear it."

"Long week."

"Eat something after this," Holt said, and then her voice went flat and professional in the way Park recognized from watching her work a board for a decade. "I ran the infrared and polarization overlays against three independent Fleet archive events. They're not noise. They repeat. I can't tell you what they mean. I can tell you Fleet instruments see the same structure the *Kestrel* saw, on our own equipment, without anyone across the table telling us what to look for."

Park felt something loosen that he hadn't known was tight. Not relief, exactly. Company.

"Good," he said. "Stay on."

"I'm not going anywhere," Holt said.

"Play the audible fraction," Orlen said. "Low."

Park opened it low.

The sound filled the compartment the way it had filled the *Kestrel* command deck: layered, dissonant, multi-vocal, too much happening at once for a single throat or a single panic. It was still ugly. It was still wrong against every human emergency format Park knew.

Orlen did not flinch. Vorin watched the spectral bands instead of the speakers. Calder's jaw set and did not move. Thessaly watched Park more than the display, which told him she was reading the people problem inside the science problem.

"Stop," Orlen said after twelve seconds.

Park stopped it.

"Your classification is wrong," she said.

Park felt the shame land cleanly. "I know it may be wrong. I did not have another category that fit."

"Distress assumes a sender asking for help," Vorin said. "This is not asking. The structure is wrong for petition. Wrong for pain broadcast as we understand pain broadcast. Wrong for your medical formats."

Thessaly said, "Then what is it?"

Orlen looked at Senka's empty chair, then at the SSD review stamp on the Dominion packet, then at Calder. She chose her words the way a person chooses a path through a mined corridor.

"It is known to us as a channel behavior associated with Peligar contact," she said. "Not fully explained in this room. Not fully explained to you. Enough to say: you have treated speech as screaming."

Park stared at the waveform.

Multi-channel. Separable bands. No destination-targeting. The instruments had captured a slice. Infrared and polarization markers sat in Ortega's supplemental as unresolved overlays Park had noted and not understood. Harmonic structure ran under the audible mess like a second conversation happening in a room with bad walls.

"Park." Holt's voice, low, over the relay. "The overlays I flagged. Look at the timing against the audible spikes."

He looked. The layers did not sit under the scream by accident. They moved with it, ahead of it in places, like a second voice keeping its own rhythm.

He heard his own earlier sentence in memory. *If I had to give it a bin.*

He had given it a bin. The bin had been a kindness to his own instruments.

"They're talking," he said.

The room held still.

The recognition did not arrive as triumph. It arrived as a correction that made the archive heavier. If the scream was speech, then every prior intercept Fleet had treated as noise or panic had been a conversation happening past human ears. Park felt the scale of that without needing anyone to lecture him about it.

"Confirmed independently on Fleet instruments," Holt said. "I'm not telling you what Orlen is about to tell you. I'm telling you our own boards already agreed with her before she said a word."

"Yes," Orlen said. "They are talking. You are hearing a fraction. Infrared, polarization, harmonic layers travel with it. Your ears take the part that sounds like screaming because that is what human hearing can hold."

Park's hands were flat on the table. "Can you translate it?"

"No," Vorin said. Immediate. Absolute. "Not in any working sense. Pattern recognition, latency maps, partial science. Not meaning."

"So we know it is language," Thessaly said, "and we still do not know what it means."

"Correct," Orlen said. "That is worse than distress. Distress asks for you. This continues without you. A civilization speaking on channels you barely hear, while your doctrine tries to tag them under rescue."

Park looked at Calder. "I want to put *probable language* instead of *consistent with language*. It is stronger."

"It is also more certain than we have earned," Calder said. "Keep *consistent with*. If someone later proves meaning, we can upgrade. If someone later proves we were wrong again, the record should show we did not pretend mastery."

Park accepted that. It cost him. Certainty would have felt like penance. Calder would not let him buy penance with a stronger adjective.

Calder said, "Update the classification. Not distress. Language without recoverable meaning. Origin unresolved. Threat undetermined. Provisional remains until we have more than a word."

"Peligar," Vorin said. "If you use the name, understand it is our word for them. Not theirs. An exonym. Like calling a people by the shape of the fear they leave."

Park wrote the correction into the Fleet supplemental with care. He did not try to make it elegant. He made it exact.

Prior provisional medical-distress flag withdrawn. Reclassified: multi-channel structured emission consistent with language; meaning unrecovered; Dominion designation Peligar available as exonym only; no translation capability demonstrated or claimed.

He read it aloud. Orlen listened. Vorin checked the Dominion equivalent text. Thessaly watched for political landmines. Calder watched for false certainty.

"Is there any chance we recover meaning with better instruments?" Park asked.

Orlen shook her head once. "Better instruments give you more of the channel. Not the mind behind it. We have chased that error for years. Do not let Fleet invent hope where Science Directorate has already paid for the absence of it."

Calder accepted that without liking it. "Log the correction. Keep provisional on threat. I will not upgrade unknown speech into known enemy just to make the forms happier."

"Log it," Thessaly said, quieter, to Park.

Park sent the corrected header into the Fleet local system and the dual-consent shared packet. The old distress flag remained in history as a prior error. That was right. Erasing the mistake would have been another kind of lie.

Orlen poured tea for herself and Vorin. She did not celebrate. Neither did Park. The joy of a corrected category sat beside the colder fact that the thing speaking was still out there, still moving, still opaque.

"I was ashamed of the distress tag," Park said. "I still am. But I would rather be ashamed of a wrong label we fixed than proud of a wrong label we kept."

Orlen considered him. "Instructor Vek used to say that a corrected record is worth more than a perfect first guess. He was talking about students. It applies."

Calder stood. "Keep the recording under dual custody. No wider distribution without Rowan and Senka. Tomorrow we talk about what may be shared beyond language."

"Maps," Thessaly said.

"Eventually," Calder said. "Not as a gift. As a next argument."

"Holt, still with me?" Park asked.

"Still here. I'll write up the Fleet-side confirmation tonight and route it to you before the morning session, not after, so you're not walking in blind again."

"You didn't have to stay on the whole time."

"I know," Holt said. "Eat something, Jun. That's not a request, it's a validation note." A beat, quieter. "Glad it wasn't distress. For what it's worth."

"It's worse than distress."

"I heard. Sleep anyway."

The relay closed on her end first, the way it usually did.

Park closed the waveform display. The thumbnail vanished. The corrected header remained.

Not distress. Speech. Meaning: none.

He sat with that until the compartment emptied around the work that still needed doing.

On the way back to the language suite, Park stopped at the anteroom viewport and looked at the corridor traffic without seeing ship classes. He was listening for the absence of a category. The distress flag had been a place to put fear. Language-without-meaning was a place to put ignorance. Ignorance was heavier, and more honest, and it would not let him sleep any better.

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — OPERATIONS RELAY**
JOINT STATUS EXCHANGE

The near-miss happened before dinner and almost became a shooting problem.

It started as a routine joint status exchange under the new headers: two Dominion border watch vessels and one Fleet survey craft sharing position and posture on the outer corridor so nobody treated ordinary traffic as a threat. Park and Vorin had built the controlled vocabulary that morning. The software on both sides was supposed to map shared terms into native command language without invention.

Supposed to.

Park was mid-sentence with Orlen about turn-taking markers when the operations relay lit amber and Thessaly's voice came through from the protocol room.

"Park. Now."

He went. Calder was already there. The wall display showed three tracks and a header cascade that had gone wrong in the middle.

Fleet survey craft *Meridian Gauge* had sent: `STATUS REPLY / HOLD WEAPONS / UNKNOWN ORIGIN BEARING CLEAR`.

Dominion border watch *K-17 Node Three* had received a native render that, after software substitution, read as an order to cease watch and open approach corridor for unidentified traffic.

"That is not what we wrote," Park said.

"I know," Thessaly said. "The substitution table mapped *hold weapons* against an older Dominion phrase for *stand down watch posture*. Someone loaded a legacy lexicon overnight."

Vorin's face went tight. "SSD review copy. Not Science Directorate. They pushed a security lexicon patch without telling us."

Senka arrived fast enough that she had clearly been monitoring. "Explain without theater."

Calder did not raise his voice. "Your patch told a border watch to drop posture while our survey craft reported a clean bearing. If someone on that watch had been more obedient than curious, we would have an open corridor and a confused Fleet ship in the same space."

"And if Fleet software had been the one inventing meaning," Senka said, "you would be blaming us for sabotage while your own table did the damage."

"Yes," Calder said. "That is also true. Which is why we contain this procedurally before anyone sells a story."

Thessaly was already writing the containment order. "Both sides freeze lexicon patches. Dual consent required for any substitution table change. *Meridian Gauge* holds position. *K-17 Node Three* restores prior watch posture manually, spoken confirmation only, no software render until we clear the table."

Park opened a live channel to both ships with Orlen beside him and Vorin checking Dominion phrasing line by line. His voice stayed level. His hands did not.

"Node Three, Contact Node Seven," Park said. "Disregard last software render. Confirm spoken instruction: restore standard watch posture. Hold weapons means hold weapons. It does not mean leave your post."

A Dominion officer answered after a lag. "Confirming. Watch posture restored. We nearly complied with the render. My duty officer questioned it because it contradicted standing orders."

"Good questioning," Orlen said into the channel. "Log the contradiction. Do not punish it."

On the Fleet side, *Meridian Gauge* confirmed they had never altered course and had not illuminated. Their captain sounded irritated in the specific way of a person who had done everything right and still almost become someone else's incident report. Calder thanked them in the flat voice of a man who understood how close bureaucracy had come to creating a crisis out of vocabulary.

Blame arrived on schedule.

Senka wanted SSD's patch logged as unauthorized interference with an approved language session. Rowan, when the relay caught him twelve minutes later, wanted Fleet's acceptance testing logged as incomplete. A Fleet Intelligence note appeared suggesting Dominion had tested whether Fleet would notice a dangerous substitution. A Dominion security note appeared suggesting Fleet had designed ambiguous shared terms to force Dominion into error.

Thessaly put both notes on the same board and did not let either one become policy.

"We have a process failure," she said. "Sabotage is a hypothesis. Incompetence is a hypothesis. Doctrine clash is a hypothesis. None of them get to skip the repair."

Calder nodded. "Repair first. Narrative later, if ever."

Park and Vorin rebuilt the substitution table by hand, term by term, with Orlen reading each line aloud and Senka forced to wait until the table could not kill anyone by accident. It took fifty-one minutes. It felt longer. Park's conversational recall helped more than pride: he could reconstruct which morning argument had produced which shared term, and which soft synonym had crept in while they were tired. Vorin caught two Dominion-side inheritances that still mapped wrong under stress. Orlen struck both without apology.

When they finished, both ships confirmed clean renders on a test exchange that said nothing more ambitious than *status reply / bearing clear / hold weapons*.

Park sat back. His hands were unsteady in a small way he hoped nobody measured.

"Language work is mission-critical," he said. "Not decorative."

"Yes," Vorin said. "That was the demonstration. I would have preferred a quieter one."

Orlen closed her notebook. "My mother used to say a shared word is a shared blade. Useful. Not safe."

Nobody argued with her.

Rowan's final instruction on the channel was brief. "Continue protocols. Do not widen distribution of the near-miss beyond command need. Park, you and the Directorate team own lexicon custody jointly from this point. If either side patches alone again, I will suspend the venue."

Senka matched him without warmth. "Agreed. SSD will be reminded that Civil Government authorized Science Directorate method for this channel."

After the channels cleared, Thessaly poured coffee for Calder and Park from the Fleet urn and left the Dominion tea service untouched. No one made a speech about civilization. The drinks were just there, doing the ordinary work of keeping people functional while suspicion remained rational on both sides.

Park walked the substitution table one more time with Vorin before they locked custody. "If SSD pushes another patch," Park said, "I need a hard stop on my side before it reaches a ship."

"You will have one," Vorin said. "I will have one too. If either of us is overridden, we suspend the venue and make the override visible."

"Visible is the only protection we have," Park said.

Orlen had appeared in the doorway, notebook still open. "Then we keep it visible," she said. "Whatever that costs us with SSD."

The room exhaled into work again. Suspicion did not leave. It simply put its hands back on the tools.

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — PROTOCOL ROOM**
DISCLOSURE LIMITS

Evening session. Same two tables. Fewer people pretending the day had been ordinary.

Rowan on relay. Senka in person. Calder, Thessaly, Park on Fleet side. Orlen and Vorin on Dominion side. A Historical Office observer had been added at the last minute by Helion: Soraya Ellis, present by audio only, authorized to listen for narrative hygiene and forbidden to speak unless Founders' Week language entered the record.

It entered once, sideways, when a Fleet staff note in the packet used *shared frontier history* as a soft synonym for contact context.

"Strike that phrase," Ellis said, voice dry over lag. "We do not have a certified shared history for operational headers. Speculative kinship language stays out of contact protocols. Founders' Week is commemorative, not evidence."

"Struck," Thessaly said, and removed it without debate.

Park watched Ellis's restraint the way he watched buffer discipline. Institutional fear had a frequency too. He did not ask what she was afraid the wrong phrase would unlock. Out of scope was out of scope.

Senka opened the disclosure agenda. "What may be shared next. Maps. Warnings. The name Peligar in wider Fleet use. Depth of explanation."

Rowan said, "Fleet will not accept unexplained fear as orders. If you want us to move ships, you need more than a word and a corrected scream."

"And we will not explain everything so you can inventory our border," Senka said. "Science Directorate may share signal science under review. Civil Government may authorize limited warning geometry. Strategic Security will not open depth maps for curiosity."

Orlen spoke carefully. "We can schedule a map conversation. Lines, missing returns, pressure on the fringe. Not a full strategic gift. Enough to show the border moves."

"Moves," Calder repeated.

"Yes," Vorin said. "That is the part your observation habits do not like. Observation assumes a stable object. This does not stay still for the observer's convenience."

Park kept his mouth on language, where he belonged. "For the map session we need shared terms for *expanding pressure*, *unresolved return*, *warning without order*, and *help request* if that is coming. If you say *help* and we hear *surrender*, or you say *buffer* and we hear *trap*, we will fail the conversation before the charts open."

Senka's eyes narrowed. "Who told you a help request is coming?"

"Nobody," Park said. "The shape of this week suggests someone will have to ask for something costly soon. I am preparing vocabulary so the ask does not arrive in a broken sentence."

Calder did not contradict him. Thessaly marked the terms on the board.

Orlen wrote the Dominion equivalents, then stopped at *help request*. "We may need a different phrase. Petition language carries shame and strategy both. Old Commander Petran said asking for aid is how empires admit weather they cannot command."

"Then we write *request for cooperative warning posture*," Thessaly said. "Ugly. Clear. Less theater."

"Acceptable as a working label," Senka said. "Not a promise that the request exists."

Rowan accepted that with a silence that functioned as permission to continue planning without pretending agreement.

They spent the next hour negotiating the next disclosure layer like people disarming a device.

Peligar could be used in classified Fleet headers as an exonym with a note that meaning and self-name remained unknown. No public release. No anatomy briefings. No claim of translation. Signal science could include partial infrared and harmonic mapping as observed phenomena, not as a dictionary. Map conversation authorized for the following day's session under dual veto, limited to fringe pressure geometry relevant to both sides' current observations. No Founders' Week language. No alliance language. No demand that Dominion explain every eleven-year archive entry as the price of one chart.

Calder pushed once, carefully. "If the map shows a moving pressure and you will not say what it is processing, Fleet will still have to decide whether to treat the warning as theater."

"Then decide with incomplete information," Senka said. "That is what command is. We will not become your encyclopedia in exchange for temporary politeness."

"Fair," Calder said. He did not sound soothed. He sounded accurate.

Park added one more language constraint before Thessaly closed the agenda. "On the shared channel tomorrow, no one says *invasion* unless they can certify observed intent to conquer. No one says *ally* unless both Civil Government and Fleet Council authorize the word. If we need a placeholder, we use *contacting party under pressure*."

Senka studied him. "You are fencing us already."

"I am fencing both of us," Park said. "Wrong nouns start wars faster than wrong charts."

Orlen wrote the constraint into her notebook without looking at Senka for permission. Vorin glanced at Senka, received a short nod, and mirrored the note on the Dominion slate. That small chain of allowance was the closest the evening came to cooperation that felt almost warm. It still was not trust. It was procedure holding.

Thessaly converted the political mess into an executable next-day agenda: lexicon lock at start of watch, dual custody of any chart image, spoken confirmation behind every critical header, walk-out drill before the first overlay opened, Park and Orlen as language owners, Calder and Senka as veto holders, Rowan and Dominion Civil on lag relay for escalation only.

"Enough shared words to schedule the map conversation," Thessaly summarized. "Not enough to call this trust."

"Correct," Senka said.

**SEGMENT 6 — PATROL CRAFT HARROW LINE**
PASSIVE OBSERVATION / FOUNDERS' REACH BEARING

Lieutenant Sera Kade had wanted a quiet divert.

*Harrow Line* was a small patrol craft with a crew that knew each other's sleep debts and coffee preferences and did not need speeches. When New Meridian ordered them onto the Founders' Reach bearing for observation only, Kade took the order as annoyance first and dread second. Founders' Reach was the kind of colony you checked because the schedule said so, not because anyone expected trouble.

The passive board disagreed.

Mass returns on the approach would not settle. Orbital fixtures that should have answered with navigation beacons answered with thinning geometry. The atmospheric envelope around the primary landmass showed gradients that weather did not write. Something was taking the system apart along useful lines: volatiles, metals, orbital stock, energy gradients. Not in a single dramatic strike. Over tempo. Continuous. Methodical.

"If this is a battle," her sensor officer said, "it is the worst-managed battle I have ever watched."

"It is not a battle," Kade said, and hated how sure she sounded before doctrine allowed surety. "Keep passive. No challenge. Log every return class that refuses to class."

They held far enough out that *Harrow Line* was a witness, not a participant. That was the order. It was also the only honest posture available. The craft could not stop what the board was showing. It could only refuse to lie about it.

On the audible fraction, the multi-vocal signal rose and fell in layered registers. The crew flinched the first time. The second time they listened harder, which was worse. It sounded like screaming if you needed it to be pain. It sounded like coordinated labor if you had been warned that human ears took the wrong slice of a wider channel.

"People," the helm officer said. "There are people down there."

"Yes," Kade said.

"Then what is this."

Kade watched infrastructure diminish on the plot: a relay mast signature failing, an orbital shed return collapsing into feedstock geometry, atmospheric scrape increasing along a curve that made her stomach go cold. The system was being processed. The word arrived from the joint warning headers already circulating through New Meridian and Contact Node Seven. She did not like it. She could not find a better one that matched the sensors.

"They are not hunting the colony," the sensor officer said slowly. "They are taking the system. The colony is in the way."

Nobody answered that for a long time.

A secondary landmass return that had matched agricultural settlement density an hour earlier began to lose structure in the same gradient pattern. Not fire. Not cratering. Removal. The board made people into a category problem: present, then obstacle-shaped, then absent from the useful graph.

"Do not put that in poetry," Kade said when the sensor officer started drafting the packet. "Put the gradients. Put the missing beacons. Put the speech structure without claiming meaning."

"Aye."

The multi-vocal signal shifted register. Not louder in a human sense. Denser. As if more speakers had entered a work channel humans could only hear as overlapping strain. The sensor officer marked latency clusters and refused to call them casualties. Kade approved the refusal. The moment they named pain they would stop seeing process, and process was what the board was insisting on.

"Any weapons signature," the helm officer asked.

"None that Fleet tables recognize," the sensor officer said. "If this is war, it is war that skipped the part where anyone announces a war."

Kade thought of First Doctrine briefs she had sat through at Academy: confirmed intent, correlated returns, certified hostility. The plot in front of her had correlated returns. It did not have intent in any language a human court would accept. It had resource behavior. Atmospheres scraped. Orbitals dismantled. Infrastructure sorted into feedstock. A G-type system being read as inventory.

She sent the observation package up the chain under the new shared headers because the order said to use them, and because the old Fleet headers wanted *attack* the way a hand wants a familiar tool. She spoke the confirmation aloud into the packet so no software could invent kindness or war.

"Founders' Reach bearing. Processing signature elevated. No Fleet challenge response from colony relay. No battle illumination. Orbital and atmospheric dismantling over tempo. Multi-vocal signal present. Category: speech structure, meaning unresolved. People remain obstacles in the observed behavior. End."

She stopped recording and sat with her hands still on the console.

On the board, Founders' Reach continued ceasing to be a place in the ordinary sense. Not all at once. Not as spectacle. As industrial ecology written across a G-type system that had believed, for twenty years, that self-sufficiency was a kind of safety.

Kade thought of the school petition joke she had heard once from a New Meridian clerk: *containers forever, marble never*. She did not put the joke in the packet. She put the absence there instead.

The helm officer asked, quietly, whether they should illuminate one active scan for a clearer count of what remained.

"No," Kade said. "Standing orders hold. We are not lighting a corridor for something that is already working. Watch and report. If anyone asks what we saw, we say exactly what the board says. No more."

*Harrow Line* held.

The signal continued.

The colony did not answer.

Somewhere behind them, New Meridian's ops channel filled with clerks who had queued Founders' Reach paperwork that morning and did not yet know the queue had become archaeology. Kade did not listen long. She kept her eyes on the plot and her craft on the observation line.

The work of witnessing continued.

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — PROTOCOL ROOM / HELION RELAY**
INSTITUTIONAL RECEIPT

The proof arrived in layers. That was how institutions preferred catastrophe to arrive when they could not stop it.

First the joint board. Then New Meridian's failed contact attempts. Then *Harrow Line*'s observation package under the headers Session Three had made usable. Then the Segment status board in the Fleet operations annex, where Founders' Reach changed from quiet label to missing return while a clerk who had complained about paperwork batches that morning stood very still and stopped complaining.

Calder stayed in the protocol room because leaving would have been theater. Thessaly kept procedure standing when categories fell over. Park kept the shared strip honest while people around him reached for nouns that would make Council feel less helpless. Vex stood with his hands behind his back and took the confirmation the way a professional takes a forecast that has come true on schedule and still feels like failure.

Rowan's lag channel filled with other voices. Council panic set in, in the bureaucratic sense: competing briefings, classification fights, demands for public language and demands for silence, First Doctrine officers insisting on confirmation standards that the colony no longer existed to satisfy.

"Is the colony gone," a Council staffer asked, wanting a number, a map color, a sentence that could be read aloud.

Proof had landed.

Trust had not.

**CONTACT NODE SEVEN — PROTOCOL ROOM**
AFTER

The productive exchange room went quiet the way rooms go quiet when the work has outrun the words available for it.

Someone had left tea on the Dominion table and coffee on the Fleet side. Nobody finished either. Nobody used the cups to measure anything. The dual-consent recorder was still live because closing it required dual consent, and for a stretch of minutes neither side could find a sentence worth sealing.

"Contact Node Seven joint status. Founders' Reach missing return remains open. Processing signature corroborated. Shared warning instruments remain active under provisional limits. No alliance language. No trust declared. Necessity noted. Awaiting survey authorization for residual assessment."

Inside, the first exchanges had become real.

The venue had its answer.

Trust had not begun.

Necessity had.
