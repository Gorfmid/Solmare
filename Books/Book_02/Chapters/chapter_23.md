***HELION — ACCORD DRAFT ROOMS / UNDER ASH***
*TERMS HARDENED*

The draft rooms smelled like paper that had already failed once, and printer heat that had not learned manners from funerals.

Holdfast Three's incomplete windows still glowed on the dual board. Tag Twelve's amber densification still climbed in fractions humans could measure and not understand. The rooms that had drafted limited instruments before Act V had ash on the table now, and poison pills that no longer pretended to be jewelry.

Rowan opened the session without parade language.

"Helion Accord negotiation resumes," he said. "Not friendship. Not cousins. Cooperation, re-chosen under loss. Lock limited military treaty language, rescue rights, a shared warning network, joint observation in amended wartime form. Explicitly not alliance. Sunsets. Vetoes. Parallel sovereignty. No permanent combined command. Begin."

Calder sat as interface, because capitals still needed a body to blame when incomplete maps refused to finish. Vex sat with gloves on, Holdfast Three's dry marks under his slate. Senka held Civil Government's signing authority under closing conditions.

Breck struck first. "Holdfast Three proved cooperation costs Dominion funerals and Fleet medical tags. Give us alliance vocabulary we can hang, or pull the framework before Accords become receipts."

Mirek answered from the opposite hunger. "This is Counterstroke receipt theater with better stationery. Close the framework before galleries invent cousins mid-signature."

A soft-gloves voice tried the third hunger. "Sign alliance. Call it temporary if you must. Populations need a shared noun after dual-holiday fracture."

"Neither," Rowan said. "Alliance belongs to a later room, if anyone earns it. Divorce spends the fringe while Tag Twelve still answers. Four instruments. Non-alliance, spoken aloud. That is what Helion can keep."

Calder looked at the poison pills the negotiation had hardened instead of removing since Chapter Nineteen. Fleet's transparency demand still threatened Dominion depth. Dominion's constraints still threatened Fleet's improvisation. None of it had softened under ash. Some of it had grown teeth.

He looked at Vex across the draft glass. Professional mirrors. No affection. Micro-trust available; civilizational trust denied.

"Operational first," Calder said. "Politics later. If the warning-net falsely confirms clear, abort chase. If people cost exceeds the scoped window, Thessaly aborts and I honor it late. What do you abort?"

"If Fleet spends fuel into a ghost, I abort elevation and re-route," Vex said. "If kinship rumor tries to convert Holdfast Three's dead into cousins mid-clause, I refuse the noun and keep drafting. Your checklist held under ash. I will test whether it holds when ink becomes schedule. Don't ask me to like you for that. Ask me to predict you."

"I can predict you," Calder said. "You will accept a loss Fleet reads as coldness before you invent hatred to make it cleaner."

"Correct," Vex said. "And you will hesitate where Thessaly locks. Draft that."

Thessaly put the human cost in front of the slogans. "Named medical tags travel with every permission. Holdfast Three's names travel under controlled dual messaging only. Schedule mourning and duty in the same watch bill. Ask crews for the next watch. Not for hope."

Near the side aisle, a Fleet ops tech who had offered a fortune roll across the commons before the dual-holiday fracture stood with his jaw locked, like a man who had decided cousins and still had to report for Accord duty. A Long Watch runner on the twin feed did not look at him. Thessaly wrote both faces into the quiet channel beside the people product. Not as accusation. As schedule.

Ellis raised her stylus once. "Draft alliance grammar and Historical Office dissents on record. Draft divorce grammar while the fringe still answers, same dissent. You get provisional cooperation-instrument language. Historians can rename it later, if anyone survives to enjoy renaming."

Rowan kept the corridor narrow, because narrow was what bitter competence could still defend. "Fleet wants transparency that threatens Dominion depth. Dominion wants constraints that threaten Fleet's improvisation. The public wants alliance or divorce. We're offering neither. That is the available weather. Work continues."

Outside the draft glass, Helion's civic promenade kept hanging copper cloth for a season that had not paused for ash. Ordinary civilization kept practicing joy and mourning on the same calendar. The map had not paused for signatures either.

***HELION — ACCORD INSTRUMENT ANNEX***
*TEXT / SUNSETS*

Thessaly put implementation annexes on both walls before the doctrine clerks could finish arguing elegance — schedules, crew billets, abort trees, named medical-tag custody, warning-net node lists with the false-confirmation hazard spelled out in plain text.

"Runnable or useless," she said. "If a clause can't schedule humans, cut it. If it invents permanent combined command, cut it. If it converts rescue into pursuit, cut it."

Hale fought the wartime observation amendments the way doctrine officers fought every amendment that threatened Edition comfort. "Peacetime observation taught delay theater. Wartime form keeps contradictory states visible and refuses to crown one package the truth. Academy dissent remains on the sheet."

An Academy instructor on the observation rail looked offended by honesty and still did not leave. "Amend First Doctrine into wartime form, and you teach cadets that grief writes doctrine."

"Grief already wrote Holdfast Three," Hale said. "I hate that sentence. I still need one ships can sail under."

Kell fought map-share from the Dominion twin with an inherited fear that sounded like preparation, because sometimes it was. "Depth maps remain withheld. Missing-return correlation windows can widen. My grandmother said a watch that needs a villain to explain lateness will invent one while the contour keeps its own schedule. Partial share under sunset is what's on offer."

A Fleet captain on the wall strip tried the transparency argument once more. "If Dominion won't gift depth, the warning network is a prosthesis with a lie built in."

"You need headers that admit they can lie," Calder said. "That's uglier and more useful. Don't baptize the net as trust. Baptize it as infrastructure that can save a named window and still fail honestly."

The header language going into the annex had a signature nobody in the room would think to credit. Park had built it field-side against real lag, argued over for weeks with Dana Holt at Helion's validation desk until the corridor tags said only what the sensors actually allowed and nothing a hopeful officer might read into them: *confirmed. probable. degraded. contradictory.* No comfort words. No finish-line words. It went into the Accord annex now as boilerplate, scrubbed clean of the two people who had fought for exactly that plainness. Park did not raise a hand to claim it when Hale read the format into the record. Some kinds of credit belonged in a drawer, not on a signature page, and he had already told Holt as much on the last relay before the draft rooms reconvened.

Hadd checked fuel margins on the implementation strip twice and still looked offended by the honesty of the numbers. "Warning-net nodes need relief rotations or they invent false confirmation loops out of fatigue. If anyone in Helion thinks ink alone invents readiness, tell them logistics already refused."

Vex put the geometry on the wall the way a watch officer put a plot on a board. "Rescue rights trigger under Peligar threat conditions only. People-cost abort spoken. No chase into unknown depth dressed as mercy."

Breck struck at symbolism after the dual-holiday fracture. "You are signing with cousins under ash. If you refuse alliance vocabulary after kinship arithmetic, you look like cowards inventing procedure."

"Kinship intensifies blame," Ellis said, before Calder could soften it. "That is why you do not climb dual-holiday recognition into Accord text. You get instruments. You do not get a reunion fairy tale."

Senka did not soften her side either. "Civil Government will not gift Fleet a leaving story. We will also not pretend the fringe pauses for elegant blame. We sign limited instruments under protest. We do not kneel."

Rowan decided what Helion could keep without lying more than the last communiqué already had.

"Accord text stabilized enough to sign," he said. "Limited military treaty under sunset and dual veto. Rescue rights under Peligar threat conditions. A shared warning network that admits it can lie. Joint observation in amended wartime form. Explicit non-alliance. Parallel sovereignty. Mediator role named imperfectly, as a provisional institutional necessity. Poison pills intact. Seeds for later strain, planted without resolving them. Begin signing prep without parade."

Outside the annex glass, a Founders' Week committee clerk argued whether children's pageant props could keep the old first-departure mark. A Dominion twin board listed harvest-festival obligations that would land inside Helion's civic window whether protocol liked the weather or not.

The work continued.

***HELION — RATIFICATION CHAMBER***
*SIGNING WITHOUT PARADE*

The ratification chamber smelled like polish applied for ceremonies Helion preferred, and ash Helion could not remove.

No banners of triumph. No blessing procession routed through the doors. Coffee sat on the Fleet table as work presence. Tea sat on the Dominion table as continuity habit. Rowan stood where he could see both tables without giving either the comfort of believing they owned him. Senka stood opposite as Civil Government's signing authority. Calder sat as interface and provisional mediator. Thessaly sat where she could watch people instead of slogans, already counting who would try to turn the hour into triumph and who would try to turn it into apology. Vex stood with gloves on for the operational countersignature.

A junior clerk posted the Accord summary on the wall without looking at anyone's face: limited military treaty, rescue rights, shared warning network, joint observation in amended wartime form, explicit non-alliance, sunsets, dual veto, parallel sovereignty, no permanent combined command.

A civic aide had hung a Founders' Week sample fold near the chamber door, as if copper cloth could baptize ash into courage. Ellis saw it and did not climb the wall. She noted the lie the way historians noted lies: dry, exact, without turning the note into a sermon.

"Remove the fold from the chamber door," Rowan said. "Ceremony without parade. Duty, not love."

The aide removed the cloth. The room did not become prettier. It became honest enough to sign.

Breck tried triumph one last time. "At least say the yards earned this. Say Holdfast Three bought partnership."

"No," Rowan said. "Holdfast Three bought unfinished names and the political permission to keep limited instruments alive when politics wanted a divorce. That is not triumph. That is austere hope, and it's only possible if we refuse to invent parade language over it."

Mirek tried apology from the opposite hunger. "Then say we're sorry we signed with opacity."

"No," Senka said. "Civil Government does not apologize for depth philosophy. We sign a buffer strategy without trust. Descendants inherit preparation, not applause."

Rowan spoke duty without dressing it up as love.

"Fleet Authority signs the Helion Accord as limited instruments Helion can keep," he said. "Rescue rights under Peligar threat conditions. A shared warning network that admits it can lie. Joint observation under wartime amendment. Explicitly not a formal alliance. If this room converts ink into friendship vows, I will veto my own signature in the next honest minute."

Senka answered without petitioner grammar. "Civil Government signs under closing conditions. Depth philosophy intact. We do not kneel because Fleet galleries need courage cloth."

They signed. Ink first. Then countersignatures. Then operational annex seals.

The chamber did not cheer. A clerk stamped custody marks with the same dry motion he used for funeral notices. No one pretended the hour had become kinship. Austere hope sat in the room only because Holdfast Three's unfinished names still glowed on the side board and refused to become decorative cloth.

"Provisional institutional necessity," the clerk read aloud, because capitals preferred bodies when they invented titles. "Captain Calder Venn named mediator for joint Accord instruments under dual consent. Incomplete. Contested. Reviewable under sunset."

Calder did not perform confidence for the gallery rail. "If we don't hold limited instruments, the map keeps moving anyway. I will mediate without finished certainty."

Vex signed operational reality without sentimentalizing it. "Civilizational trust remains denied. They have no hatred. That remains field assessment, not gallery cloth. My old commander said a watch shared with a neighbor is still a watch, not a wedding. Keep that sentence. Keep the gloves."

Thessaly put the first runnable watch bill on both tables while the ink dried. "Implementation begins now. Warning-net rotations under shared feeds. Rescue rights checklist posted to both bridges. If anyone needs a parade, invent it after the first honest header returns."

Ellis logged the Accord into custody without converting it into comfort. "Instruments exist. Myths remain contested. Founders' Week wall text still lies in public adjacent spaces. I will record both. I will not bury either for stability theater."

Rowan looked once at Holdfast Three's unfinished list, once at Tag Twelve's amber densification still climbing, once at Thessaly's people schedule already moving humans through ash.

"Helion Accord exists," he said. "No formal alliance declared. Said aloud. The first Accord-authorized order will not be a parade flight. Work continues."

Outside the chamber glass, Helion kept hanging copper cloth. A memorial cart rolled past with Founders' Reach kits stacked too high. Ordinary civilization continued. Not hypocrisy — just how empires kept teaching children while adults argued funerals and signed prostheses.

Not optimism. Not alliance. Not finished certainty. Trust remained harder than war.

***HELION — HISTORICAL OFFICE / ELLIS CODA***
*MEMORY UNDER SEAL*

Ellis walked the Founders' Week exhibit corridor the way she walked every room that wanted beginnings more than evidence: carefully, custody tags visible, without believing the season would wait for signatures.

Ilen Maras hovered near the commemorative drawers. "Handlers want stability," he said. "They want dual-holiday recognition buried for the season. They want Historical Office to treat Accord ink as permission to stop asking rude questions about beginnings. Capitals just signed. Galleries will call that enough courage for one calendar."

"Accord instruments are not departure stories," Ellis said. "Rescue rights don't answer edition mismatch. A warning-net node doesn't sand a commemorative liturgy. I will not stay silent archive because ink smelled like austere hope."

A colleague from the civic wing tried loyalty dressed as caution. "Soraya. Capitals just signed. If you keep the dual-holiday packet live, someone will say you explained away Founders' Reach while Helion tried to practice hope."

"Then galleries can argue with the evidence," Ellis said. "I will not complete a romance about enemies becoming family because empty berths made arithmetic rude. I also will not pretend commemorations never rhyme. Partial archive survives. History stays unfinished. Austere hope is only possible because grief preceded it. That's not comfort. That's custody."

She secured the object set under seal, logging the Accord's meaning beside the sealed extraction frame without converting either into theater: cooperation instruments beside an unresolved founding asymmetry. Instruments real. Beginnings contested. Neither buried. Neither completed into reunion.

"If you climb that into open gallery," Ilen said, "committee cloth will try to outrun you before breakfast."

"Then I keep it sealed and live," Ellis said. "Historical Office will not stay silent archive. Capitals may weaponize it or bury it. The duty remains."

Outside the exhibit glass, Helion looked like a civilization celebrating beginnings while the frontier still moved. A junior runner carried sample folds past a memorial cart and did not look at either long enough to choose a sermon.

Rowan's reply came back dry and half-blind. *Hold under seal. Accord exists as instruments, not kinship cloth. The first Accord-authorized order follows: joint warning-corridor tasking toward the moving frontier. Work continues.*

Ellis closed the drawer without pretending the answer was comfort. Instruments existed beside unfinished history. That was enough for custody. It was not enough for belief.

Outside the exhibit glass, Tag Twelve's amber densification still climbed. The map had not paused for signatures. Cooperation instruments existed. Not optimism. Not alliance. Not finished certainty. Trust remained harder than war. The next corridor waited toward the moving frontier.
