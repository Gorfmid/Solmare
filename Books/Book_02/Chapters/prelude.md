# Prelude
## The Founding Exodus

The harvest banners had been going up since midmorning, and by the time Yeva reached the main promenade of Serath District, they were fully strung — gold thread on old cord, the same set she had helped carry down from storage four autumns running, the corners reinforced with neat stitching by hands she did not know, old work from someone careful about the details.

She had walked this promenade her entire adult life. She knew which section of the north end had been poorly repaved and caught your toe if you weren't paying attention. She knew which vendors kept the best fermented root stock, which stall's dried fruit ran sweet rather than sharp, which of the music groups played the old autumn sequence with the correct slowing in the third movement. She knew all of it, and she was walking through it for the last time, and she could not decide whether to pay more attention or less.

Maren ran ahead and came back. She was eight, and eight-year-olds moved at a different pace from the world around them. She returned now holding a grain figure bought from a vendor, dried stalks twisted into a human shape.

"It looks like Halcen," she said.

"It looks like a grain figure," Yeva said.

"Halcen has the same arms."

Yeva considered the figure more carefully. Maren was probably right. "Don't tell him that," she said.

Maren pocketed the figure and ran toward the lantern stalls.

The promenade was full in the way it always was on the first night of harvest week. Families with grain bundles moving toward the district hearth for the evening ceremony. Old couples in autumn colors. Academy students who had been let out early. The percussion group warming up in the east corner, the metallic opening sequence that everyone recognized from childhood on.

On the wall above the main arch, the oldest plaque in the district — bronze script on gray stone, the names of the seven surveyors who had established Serath District three hundred years prior. Yeva had walked under it most of her life without reading it. She stopped and read it now.

The names were ordinary. Not heroes, or at least not recorded as heroes. Just names, and a date, and a notation: *Survey cohort, autumn passage, established Serath.*

Autumn passage. She thought about that. She thought about tomorrow.

---

She found Petra at the grain-bundle stalls, where Andris was arguing pleasantly with a vendor about whether the cord color was traditional or just what they had on hand. Their two boys were eating something fried and watching the percussion group settle in. The taller boy was already as tall as his father, which Yeva found alarming in the way of things that happened gradually until they didn't.

Petra saw her and came over. They had been friends since they were twelve, had lived two buildings apart for the past nine years, had helped each other through things Yeva wasn't thinking about now because this was not the time.

"You finished packing," Petra said.

"This afternoon," Yeva said. "Torin's at the dock running numbers with Halcen. I wanted one more evening."

"One more evening," Petra said, without editorializing, which Yeva appreciated.

"Maren wanted to see the lanterns."

"Yes." Petra was watching her boys. Andris had won the cord argument and was wrapping the grain bundle correctly now, the traditional crossed pattern, the kind his father had done it. "Andris keeps saying the three who came back from the Valdorn corridor. Every time. As if you haven't heard."

"He means well."

"He does. He also can't imagine going himself, so he talks about the ones who came back." She paused. "Three out of nine. He keeps forgetting the other six."

Yeva nodded. She understood Andris. She understood all of the people who were staying, in the same way she understood a language she had grown up in, without having to think about it. The Dominion held depth, and the district held history, and history was not nothing. The plaque above the arch held the names of seven surveyors and would hold them for three hundred more years. There was something real in that. She just needed the space ahead of it.

Petra asked about the route. Yeva told her about the survey continuation bearing, the forty-two-day estimate at convoy pace, what Halcen had said about the conditions beyond the catalogued edge. She tried to make it sound ordinary, which it was to her by now, and she could tell it was not ordinary to Petra at all by the way Petra's eyes stayed very still while she listened.

"Will you send word?" Petra asked. "When you arrive."

"As soon as we can establish a relay."

"My mother used to say that news comes first to those who stop moving." Petra considered it. "She meant it as a criticism."

"I always thought it was an argument for going," Yeva said.

Petra smiled, small and real. "She might have meant it both ways."

The lanterns came on along the vendor rows, all at once, the district's timing making it look deliberate and beautiful, and the promenade shifted from an evening crowd to something warmer and older. Maren appeared out of the gathering with her grain figure and three other children behind her, all of them serious about something. The percussion group began the autumn sequence. Yeva recognized the third movement coming and felt the weight of it in her chest, and let it be there, and did not try to name it.

When families began moving toward the district hearth and others were peeling off toward home, Petra found Yeva in the thinning crowd and pressed a small grain bundle into her hands. Not the presentation kind, the formal hearth type. A small one, made with cord from Petra's own stall, gold thread worked into the binding.

"For the new place," Petra said. "So you have one."

Yeva held it. She thought about the distance between here and the survey continuation zone, and the grain figure in Maren's pocket, and the seven names above the arch.

"Preparation honors those who come after us," Petra said. The traditional harvest season close.

Yeva said it back. She meant it as the same thing and as something different, and both were true, and neither of them said so.

---

Arvos Kastal's office was on the third level of the district records building, which was also the oldest building in continuous administrative use in Serath District — a point the building made with some persistence, being composed of four identifiable construction eras visible in the different stone colors and wall textures, the oldest section dating to the original survey period and the newest addition about seventy years old and already beginning to look like the rest. The worn handrails in the stairwell were the same design as the ones in the seventy-year section and the three-hundred-year section, standardized at some point, generations of elbows finding the same position.

Arvos had been in this office for seventeen years. He had started as a junior records clerk and become a district registrar through competence, patience, and the kind of institutional loyalty that produced competence and patience as natural side effects. He was forty-three. He was, by any reasonable measure, someone who would continue to be a district registrar for the foreseeable future. He did not find this troubling. He found it comprehensible.

He set his tea aside and worked through the departure certification forms.

Thirty-seven families. Four unaffiliated individuals. One collective agricultural unit with heritage grain stock they had declined to identify by variety until after departure, which he had negotiated down to *varieties to be documented in-transit* without breaking the form's requirements. A survey consortium incorporated six weeks ago specifically to meet the legal classification for departure registration. He had seen this maneuver before. It was technically correct.

He worked through the certifications methodically. Property transfers. Census updates. School enrollment changes. Medical record forwarding requests. The kind of administrative housekeeping that a family performed when it ceased to be a district resident and became, in the formal language of the record, *a voluntary colonial departure, status: active transit.*

The destination field gave him pause. The form expected a registered colony designation or an authorized survey corridor reference. These families had neither. They had a bearing and a survey continuation zone notation, Dominion standard for the survey edge and what lay beyond it. He had seen that notation twice before in his seventeen years, both times with much smaller groups.

He wrote: *Provisional Survey Continuation Zone, uncatalogued sectors, bearing as noted.*

It was accurate. It was also the form's way of saying there was no there there yet, which was precisely the point.

Outside, through the wall, the harvest festival percussion was audible — distant, the opening sequence, the one everyone knew. He had considered going down to the promenade himself but decided against it. He always considered going. He rarely went. The forms needed finishing before the cohort's dock time in the morning.

He had spoken with several of the departing families over the past few weeks. Not all — there were forty-odd families and he had spoken with perhaps a third. He had noticed they were not the people he might have predicted. Some were young with children, as expected for frontier cohorts. But some were older than he was, established in Serath, with service records and trade connections and years of investment in this district. One woman had run the agricultural coordination office here for eleven years. A man who had surveyed three other systems for the Dominion, on Dominion commission, had now decided he wanted to survey somewhere the Dominion was not managing.

That man, Halcen, had come in for a records consultation rather than a departure form. He had wanted to look at the historical record of prior voluntary departures from the region. Arvos had let him. He read carefully, made notes on a slate, thanked Arvos without saying what he was looking for.

What Arvos had observed in that consultation was not impatience or resentment or any of the motivations he had associated in theory with people who left. What he had observed was something closer to the planning instinct of a person who had identified a task and was working through it methodically. He respected that, even when he didn't share it.

He would not be going. He was not inclined toward frontier living, and he was not inclined toward the specific mixture of hope and irritability he had observed in these families over the past several months. They were not rebels. They were people who had decided that Serath District and the district beyond it and the administrative continuity extending from here to the heart of Dominion space was a fine arrangement for some people and not, in the end, for them. He couldn't call that wrong. He had grown up in a town where three families left every generation for somewhere new and came back to report it strange or never came back at all, and the ones who didn't come back were not mourned as failures.

In the supplementary field — not required, used at the registrar's discretion — he wrote: *Voluntary departure during harvest festival period. Timing noted as intentional per cohort preference.*

He had first written *consistent with historical colonial practice* and changed *practice* to *tradition,* because practice sounded temporary, like a thing the district was still deciding whether to approve. The departure had happened. It was tradition now.

He stamped the forms and set them in the completed tray.

He thought about the three families who had come back from the Valdorn corridor reporting empty space and signal silence. He thought about how emptiness had not been a deterrent. He thought about Halcen's careful notes on the slate and the agricultural coordinator's deliberate property transfer and a woman named Yeva Soran who had come in twice for forms assistance and spoken both times with the attention of someone who intended to remember everything correctly.

He poured more tea and went back to work.

---

By dawn the promenade was being swept. The gold banners stayed up — harvest week still had four days to run — but the vendor stalls were closed and the percussion instruments packed away, the district holding the particular quiet of a place recently full.

Yeva reached the dock with Maren in a carry pod on her back and found Torin and Halcen already deep in the cargo allocation, moving between vessels with slates and the economy of movement that came from a very early start.

The dock was large and built to last rather than to impress. Old stone facing on the outer walls, newer composite panels on the loading bays, a century of modification making the surface textures inconsistent in the way of a structure continuously used rather than planned. Worn paint markings on the floor in six different generations of colors. Forty-three vessels in varying states of readiness, from fully loaded to still receiving final crates.

Halcen saw her and held up two fingers. Two issues. He'd get to them. She nodded.

He was an unhurried man, which she had taken initially for slowness and recalibrated to precision. He moved through problems at the speed of someone who had categorized them before acting, which meant he rarely acted twice. He had done this kind of thing before, not this exact convoy but this kind of departure, the crossing from catalogued space into something without a map key. He had surveyed beyond one edge, returned to write up his findings for the Dominion record, and then at some point decided he preferred the uncatalogued part.

She had asked him once why this cohort specifically.

"The agricultural unit is carrying heritage grain varieties they won't identify," he had said. "That means they're serious about a stable colony, not a survey pass. And you've got children. I prefer serious."

Maren woke up and demanded to be set down and immediately located another girl her age near a stack of cargo trunks. Within a few minutes they were sitting on the same trunk, discussing something with the seriousness of children who have not yet learned to be indirect about friendships.

A man with a scar along his jaw caught Yeva's eye from across the dock — he had gotten the scar in a survey tunnel and had a manner to him that said he understood physical risk clearly and had decided this risk was preferable to others. He nodded once, a greeting that needed nothing back.

Torin came over and stood beside her.

"Halcen says we're on schedule," he said.

"Good."

"First twenty vessels fully loaded. The last section needs about two more hours."

Yeva looked at the assembled convoy, the various hulls, the families around them — loading, checking, adjusting, talking, standing quietly. Parents with children who didn't fully understand what was happening and would remember the dock without knowing it as the dock. Old hands who moved like they had left before. Young couples who had never left anything.

Whatever came next, these were the people.

---

At midday the district continuity officer arrived.

She came with two assistants and a transport cart and the brisk manner of someone with a full afternoon ahead. Her name was Dunara Vel. Yeva had not known this was coming until Halcen mentioned it that morning — he had said the district sent someone for departures over thirty families, that it had been done this way for a long time.

The cart carried forty-three grain bundles. Dried harvest stalks, gold cord, a district seal stamped on each binding.

Dunara Vel read the names from a list and the families came forward one by one and received their bundle and said the appropriate phrase and she marked them off. It took a while. The dock had arranged itself into an informal gathering, the way crowds assembled for a shared occasion without being directed. Children climbed on cargo trunks. Someone produced a blanket for an old man sitting down.

When Yeva's family was called she stepped forward with Maren beside her, Torin behind. Maren received the bundle with complete seriousness. Dunara Vel asked if Yeva wanted to say anything — apparently a tradition, offered but not required.

"We're grateful for the years here," Yeva said. "We hope the district continues well."

Dunara Vel nodded and marked the list.

When the last name was marked she faced the assembled cohort and read the departure acknowledgment. She read it clearly and without decoration, the voice of someone who had prepared for this and found it worth preparing for: "Serath District notes your voluntary departure and wishes your cohort success in your survey continuation. You carry with you the grain of the harvest in the year of your going. Whatever you build from here, you began here."

It was the harvest season language, different words, same movement — the recognition that preparation and departure were connected, that what you carried into the future you had first grown in the ground behind you.

Someone in the crowd began the harvest response and the others joined, the traditional completion, the words everyone knew from childhood. Yeva said them with the rest.

Behind the barrier rail where visitors stood, the people who had come to watch did so without speeches. Yeva found Petra and Andris. Petra's hand was in her husband's. She looked steady. They had said what they needed to say the night before, and Petra was a person who did not need to say things twice.

Yeva nodded once. Petra nodded back.

The loading resumed.

---

They lifted two hours before dark.

Yeva found a viewpoint window on the second deck and watched. Serath District resolved into a planetary pattern below her, city lights and agricultural grids and the particular color of the sea basin to the south that she had always liked from orbit on the few times she had left and returned. Harvest week's lights were visible on the night side, the festival still running, gold thread banners still up over streets she had walked her whole life.

Maren came and stood beside her. She was carrying both grain figures now, her own and the bundle from Petra.

"When do we get there?" Maren asked.

"About forty days."

"What's there when we get there?"

"We don't know yet," Yeva said. "We'll figure it out."

Maren seemed to find this acceptable. She leaned against the viewport and watched the planet turn below them.

Yeva thought: *next year at this season we will do something to mark it. Not this, but something.* A shared meal. This bundle on a table somewhere that didn't exist yet, in a place without a name yet. Someone saying *this is the time of year we left* and the children asking why and the answer being honest: because we wanted something that wasn't behind us. And then the children's children, who would not have left, would do the same thing without quite knowing why the season felt weighted, and eventually it would have a shape and a name and a story that got the facts approximately right and the feeling exactly right.

She did not know what it would look like. She thought it would probably start small, as everything does. She thought it would probably last.

The harvest lights disappeared around the planet's curve.

---

The survey continuation zone read empty on every instrument they carried, which Halcen had predicted and which still took several days to fully believe.

Yeva contributed a notation to the shared log on day fourteen: *survey continuation zone, first agricultural assessment, heritage stock reports positive propagation conditions. Recommend formal record when stable landing site identified.* She was a teacher, not a surveyor, but she was beginning to understand that in this convoy the roles distributed differently, and that record-keeping was now one of hers.

Maren made marks in the margin of her notation. Yeva left them in.

On day seventeen, Torin and the convoy coordinator and two members of the survey consortium spent eight hours with their charts and produced a preliminary bearing adjustment. The edge of the Dominion's catalogued space was behind them. Ahead, the charts had a notation that translated roughly as *not yet surveyed, conditions unknown, proceed with caution* — standard edge-of-chart language that had lived in this region for longer than anyone in the convoy had been alive.

Halcen looked at the chart and said: "Someone drew a line here. It doesn't mean there's anything on it."

"It means the Dominion stopped cataloguing," Torin said.

"That's what I said," Halcen said.

---

Three months later, Arvos Kastal received a routing notice that another cohort was forming in Serath District.

Seven families and a freelance pilot cooperative. Same destination notation: *Provisional Survey Continuation Zone, uncatalogued sectors, bearing as noted.* He recognized the format because he had written it.

He started the forms.

He did not think much about the autumn cohort anymore. Occasionally the grain bundles crossed his mind. Occasionally the phrase from Dunara Vel's speech: *whatever you build from here, you began here.* He had heard it at departures before, the same words each time, read from the same card, and it had never struck him particularly until this autumn. He thought it was the most accurate thing said during the departure, even so.

In his seventeen years he had processed seven voluntary departures. They had never amounted to much in the district's institutional memory. A note in the seasonal records. A cross-reference entry. People who had left, and the district had kept going, and eventually the people who remembered them were replaced by people who had not known them, and the forms outlasted the familiarity.

He thought: in thirty years, no one in this district will remember most of their names.

He thought: in thirty years they will have names of their own, for the place they're building, and those names will not appear in any record he would ever read.

Neither thought was actionable. He did not write either one down.

He did write one final note in the margin of the autumn cohort supplementary field, not required, not standard, the kind of thing that might not survive a formal archive consolidation: *cohort carried harvest grain into survey continuation. timing appears intentional.*

His supervisor would probably change one word somewhere.

He left it for her and started the new forms.

---

On day forty-one the survey consortium found a system.

It was not dramatic in the way Yeva had half-expected. What happened was that Halcen, on first watch, said to the navigator quietly that the spectral return on the third body looked promising, and the navigator checked, and said yes, and sent the notation to the convoy coordinator, who woke Torin, who read it and said to call the leads.

By the time Yeva came to the common table there were seven people standing around the display arguing calmly about what the readings meant. Maren appeared behind her and looked at the display with patient interest.

"Is that it?" Maren asked.

"Probably," Yeva said.

"It's small."

"They all start small."

Maren considered this. She set her grain figure on the table beside the display — not a meaningful gesture, except that she was eight years old and it was what she had.

Halcen looked at it and then at the display. "We'll need a name for the record," he said. He said it practically.

"We'll figure it out," Torin said.

"Now," Halcen said. "Whatever we call it, the first notation sets it. You rename later if you need to, but the first notation stays in the record."

The people around the table were quiet for a moment.

Yeva thought about the autumn cohort entry that Arvos Kastal had written in Serath District, the harvest festival timing noted in the supplementary field, *consistent with established colonial tradition.* She thought about Petra's face behind the barrier rail and the grain bundle and *whatever you build from here, you began here,* which she had not been able to stop remembering.

She said the name of the district the earliest surveyors had come from, three centuries back, before Serath was the name. The old name, the one still carved on the oldest plaques in the district corridors, that most people walked past without reading.

She did not say it because it was sentimental. She said it because it would mean something in the record, a line that could be traced back if anyone ever wanted to, a way of saying *we came from somewhere.* And because she was forty-one days past the survey edge and beginning to understand that the things she had tried to memorize — the cedar smell, the lanterns, the third movement slowing — would not stay the way she had held them, and the record was the only part that might.

Halcen wrote it down.

He wrote: *Day 41 survey notation: candidate system, bearing as recorded. Designated provisional. Heritage grain stock intact. Full cohort present. Recommend formal assessment.*

He did not write: *This is the beginning of something.* He did not need to. Outside the viewports the new system hung in the dark, small and unnamed except for the name they had just given it, which had already become what it would always have been.

---

In Serath District, the autumn cohort departure forms sat in the seasonal records under the harvest festival period notation, cross-referenced against three prior voluntary departures and one Valdorn corridor cohort. Accurate, complete, and already missing what they could not contain — which was that forty-three families had carried the season's grain into uncatalogued space, and that everything those families would build would remember this season as its beginning, and everything they had left behind would remember this season as the year they went.

Both sets of records would be right about what they contained.

Both would be missing the other one.

The gold banners came down at the end of harvest week, folded carefully, stored with cedar oil, ready for next autumn.

On the new world, forty-one days and one system away, Maren's grain figure sat on a shelf that did not exist yet and would not exist for two more years, in a room that would eventually be called the first permanent structure, in a settlement that would be named and renamed twice before the name held.

The season would come again there as it came everywhere. They would mark it, because people mark things. They would teach the children what it meant.

They would not know that on the other side of the survey edge, in a district they were already starting to misremember, the harvest banners were going back up for another year.

They would not know because no one told them, and because they were not, any longer, people who would think to ask.

That was how it started. Neither side saw it happen. Both sides were already certain they remembered correctly.
