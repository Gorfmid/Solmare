## 1. OVERVIEW & MISSION

### 1.1 Function

Fleet Logistics Command (FLC) is the operational and administrative authority responsible for the sustained readiness, supply, and material health of all Unified Fleet Authority naval assets.

FLC does not decide where the fleet goes. FLC determines **how long it can stay there**.

The Logistics Commander reports directly to Fleet Command. FLC sits parallel to Fleet Intelligence, Fleet Command Operations, and the Science Directorate — and interacts formally with all three.

### 1.2 Core Mandate

1. **Fuel** — ensure all Fleet vessels can reach their assigned operational areas and return
2. **Materiel** — maintain spare parts, ordnance, and consumable resupply across the network
3. **Maintenance** — schedule, track, and certify hull readiness across vessel classes
4. **Deployment Sustainability** — calculate and report how long operational postures can be maintained without resupply
5. **Industrial Monitoring** — assess Fleet and foreign production capacity; translate observations into readiness timelines

### 1.3 Institutional Motto

> *"What the fleet needs, when it needs it, where it is."*

Not a philosophy. A specification. FLC personnel are expected to treat this as a throughput problem, not an inspiration.

### 1.4 Cultural Personality

FLC is the most numerically dense command in Fleet Authority. Its officers count things — lanes, depots, duty cycles, tonnage, slip schedules, readiness curves. They are **impatient with strategic philosophy** when numbers are available. They are impatient with numbers when timelines are available.

The working environment at Logistics Command headquarters (Helion Prime, Annex Seven) is:
- Coffee. Always.
- Printer output, readiness boards, tonnage ledgers.
- No artwork on the walls; charts and schedules everywhere.
- Officers who interrupt briefings to ask for the number, not the narrative.

FLC does not distrust Fleet Intelligence. It mistrusts Intelligence estimates that arrive without **rate, duration, and tonnage** attached.

> *"Tell me the number. Tell me the trend. Then you can have a conclusion."*
> — Standard FLC briefing culture, origin unknown, universally repeated

## 3. THE LOGISTICS NETWORK

### 3.1 Network Architecture

The Fleet logistics network is not a line. It is a **web with a spine**.

The **fuel spine** is the primary structural feature: a chain of high-capacity fixed depots connecting Helion Prime to the mid-rim frontier edge, following primary corridor geometry. Everything else — relay depots, forward caches, mobile tenders — branches off the spine or supports its margins.

```
HELION PRIME (Primary Yards / Full-Grade Depot)
    │
    ▼
RELAY DEPOT ALTHEN (Core Tier)
    │
    ▼
RELAY DEPOT CARANTH (Mid-Rim Tier)
    │
    ▼
FUEL STATION MERIC-7 (Frontier Tier)
    │
    ▼  ← Sparse below this point
FORWARD CACHE ECHOES (Provisional/Pre-positioned)
```

The frontier edge of the spine — beyond Meric-7 — is where logistics confidence degrades sharply. Forward caches at the Neutral Zone approach corridors exist on paper and in supply reports. Whether they are stocked, staffed, or physically accessible at any given time is a separate question. FLC Frontier Adjacency knows this. It is documented in Section 9 of every quarterly readiness review. The section continues to exist.

### 3.2 Node Classifications

| Node Type | Staff | Fuel Capacity | Maintenance | Location |
|-----------|-------|---------------|-------------|----------|
| **Yard Facility (Full)** | 500–3,000 | Maximum | Full overhaul | Helion Prime, 2 regional |
| **Relay Depot (Major)** | 80–200 | High | Field-plus maintenance | Core and Mid-Rim |
| **Fuel Station (Fixed)** | 20–60 | Moderate | Diagnostic only | Mid-Rim and Frontier |
| **Mobile Tender (Deployed)** | 40–80 | Low–Moderate | Field-level only | Mobile assignment |
| **Forward Cache (Pre-positioned)** | 0–5 (automated) | Low | None | Frontier / NZ approaches |
| **Emergency Reserve (Sealed)** | 0 (sealed) | Variable | None | Classified locations |

### 3.3 Redundancy Doctrine

FLC doctrine requires that any vessel operating within resupply range maintain access to at least **two independent supply paths** — primary and fallback.

Within the core sector: this is reliably achieved.
Within the mid-rim: primary path reliable; fallback path varies by corridor congestion.
Within the frontier zone: single-path operation is routine for extended operations. FLC doctrine **permits** this under Extended Deployment Protocol (EDP, Form LC-7). FLC does **not** recommend it.

The *Kestrel Veil* class of scout deployments — months of operations in frontier and Neutral Zone corridor approaches — operates under EDP by default. See Section 5 and Section 9.

## 4. FUEL SYSTEMS DOCTRINE

### 4.1 Fleet Fuel: Nature and Requirements

Fleet propulsion across all vessel classes relies on processed drive plasma — hereafter referred to as **fuel** for logistics classification purposes, regardless of specific propulsion architecture. Resupply is categorized by **fuel mass equivalent (FME)** to standardize across class differences.

Fuel is the logistical constraint that shapes Fleet deployments more than any other single factor. Everything else can be improvised; fuel cannot.

### 4.2 Fuel Depot Types

| Depot Type | Description | Refuel Capacity | Accessibility |
|------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Fixed Grade-A Depot** | Permanent installation, full-spectrum refueling | Any class including Leviathan | Planned approach required |
| **Fixed Grade-B Depot** | Mid-size installation | Up to Battleship class | Standard docking |
| **Mobile Tender (FT-series)** | Fleet tender vessel deployed with task groups | Up to Heavy Cruiser | Task group only |
| **Forward Cache** | Pre-positioned automated or lightly staffed | Frigate and below; Scouts primary | Navigation required; not always accessible |
| **Emergency Reserve** | Sealed strategic reserve | Classified | Emergency authorization only |

### 4.3 The Fuel Spine

The fuel spine is not a single route. It is the combined capacity of Grade-A and Grade-B fixed depots along Fleet's primary operational corridor, maintained at **minimum 90% capacity** per FLC Fuel Systems Directorate standing directive.

**Spine integrity is a strategic readiness indicator.** If the spine drops below 70% aggregate capacity — for any reason — FLC Readiness Assessment Division initiates Tier-2 review and notifies Fleet Command within 24 hours.

Drops below 50% trigger automatic escalation to the Logistics Commander and Fleet Command authority.

This has occurred twice in the past forty years. Both times were maintenance-driven, not combat-related.

### 4.4 Refueling by Vessel Class

| Class | Fuel Mass Equivalent | Depot Type Required | Estimated Refuel Time | Operational Range (full tank) |
|-------|---------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|------------------------------|
| **Scout** | Low (FME-1) | Forward Cache or better | 4–8 hours | Extended (months, full EDP) |
| **Fighter / Ion Fighter** | Minimal | Carrier or Tender | 30 min | Short sortie only |
| **Corvette** | Low (FME-2) | Grade-B or Mobile Tender | 6–12 hours | Medium patrol |
| **Frigate** | Moderate (FME-4) | Grade-B or better | 12–24 hours | Extended patrol |
| **Cruiser** | Moderate-High (FME-7) | Grade-B or better | 18–36 hours | Standard deployment |
| **Heavy Cruiser** | High (FME-12) | Grade-A preferred | 24–48 hours | Standard deployment |
| **Battleship** | Very High (FME-22) | Grade-A only | 48–72 hours | Limited (requires planned resupply) |
| **Fleet Carrier** | Very High (FME-24) | Grade-A only | 48–72 hours | Limited (requires planned resupply) |
| **Dreadnaught** | Extreme (FME-45) | Grade-A only | 72–96 hours | Very limited (fixed operations preferred) |
| **Titan** | Extreme (FME-90) | Grade-A full facility | 4–6 days | Strategic asset — planned corridors only |
| **Leviathan** | Massive (FME-200+) | Primary Yard or Grade-A Depot | 7–14 days | Strategic asset — corridor-dependent |

**Note on Scouts:** Scouts consume the least fuel per transit but operate the farthest from Grade-A infrastructure. This creates the characteristic Kestrel-class operational profile: small fuel loads, pre-positioned forward caches, extended self-sufficiency with periodic cold-cache refueling. Scouts running low on fuel in Neutral Zone corridor approaches cannot simply divert to the nearest major depot. There may not be one within operational range.

### 4.5 What Running Low Means

**For a Scout:** Running low on fuel in frontier operations means one thing — abort the mission and navigate to the nearest pre-positioned forward cache, hoping it is stocked and accessible. If it is not, the scout is in emergency protocol. There is no "slightly less fuel" situation for a scout at extended range; there is "adequate" and there is "abort now." Captains who allow fuel states to drop below 20% FME in a single-path corridor without a confirmed cache at the waypoint are violating EDP minimums.

**For a Frigate or Cruiser:** Running low means reduced operational flexibility, likely diversion from patrol lane, loss of mission time. Not existential, but operationally costly. Requires Form LC-7 Amendment filing.

**For a Capital Ship (Battleship and above):** Running low is a command failure requiring investigation. Capital ships do not deviate into unsupported corridors. If they do, and they run low, someone authorized the deployment without reading the sustainment appendix. This has happened. The sustainment appendix remains required reading.

### 4.6 Emergency Fueling Doctrine

**Emergency Fuel Protocol (EFP)** applies when a vessel cannot reach its planned resupply point under current fuel state.

Procedure:
1. Transmit fuel emergency signal on Fleet relay bands immediately
2. Calculate minimum divert options based on actual FME remaining
3. Request cross-class transfer from nearest available tender or depot-capable vessel if within range
4. FLC Frontier Adjacency activates nearest emergency reserve authorization (requires Logistics Commander acknowledgment for sealed reserves)
5. Report filed: Form EFP-1 within 6 hours of fuel emergency declaration

**Cross-class fueling:** Possible between compatible classes. A Mobile Tender (FT-series) can partially refuel a scout from a cruiser's fuel surplus in a pinch. Efficiency degrades; it works. Protocol covered in FME Transfer Addendum 12.

## 5. RESUPPLY CHAINS BY VESSEL CLASS

### 5.1 Light Classes — Fighters and Ion Fighters

Fighters and ion fighters do not operate independently of a carrier or base facility. Their logistics chain is internal to the carrier/base system. FLC tracks fighter resupply at the **platform level**, not the individual craft.

**Logistics footprint:** Minimal when carrier-based. The entire fighter resupply problem is a carrier resupply problem.

### 5.2 Corvettes and Frigates

Fast patrol classes. Designed for medium-duration independent operation with mobile tender support.

| Class | Resupply Interval (standard patrol) | Primary consumables | Dependency |
|-------|-------------------------------------|---------------------|------------|
| Corvette | 30–45 days | Fuel, laser capacitors, life support consumables | Grade-B depot or Mobile Tender |
| Frigate | 45–60 days | Fuel, missile ordnance, shields, life support | Grade-B depot minimum |

Frigates are the backbone of mid-rim patrol logistics. Their missile systems are high-consumption. An active frigate in patrol posture goes through ordnance faster than fuel in contested space. FLC Mid-Rim RLC tracks frigate ordnance consumption rates as a proxy for patrol intensity.

### 5.3 Cruisers and Heavy Cruisers

The logistical middle tier. Cruisers are the most commonly deployed independent capital asset; their supply profile defines what a "standard deployment" looks like for FLC planning.

| Class | Resupply Interval | Primary consumables | Dependency |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|------------|
| Cruiser | 60–90 days | Fuel, plasma system components, shield matrix, fighters (4) | Grade-B minimum; Grade-A preferred |
| Heavy Cruiser | 60–75 days | Fuel, enhanced plasma systems, shields, fighters (8) | Grade-A preferred |

**Shield matrix resupply** is a cruiser-specific logistical challenge. Shields degrade under combat; partial resupply from mobile tender is possible; full matrix reset requires yard facility time. A cruiser running with a partially degraded shield matrix is operationally categorized as **AMBER-LIMITED** (see Section 8).

### 5.4 Battleships and Fleet Carriers

High-consumption platforms requiring planned supply corridors.

| Class | Resupply Interval | Primary consumables | Notes |
|-------|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| Battleship | 30–45 days (active operations) | Fuel, ion system cells, fighter complement (40), crew stores | Cannot improvise forward supply; Grade-A required |
| Fleet Carrier | 30–45 days | Fuel, fighter complement (400), ordnance, crew stores | Carrier's logistics is mostly fighter logistics at scale |

Battleships and carriers are fuel-hungry and fighter-hungry. A battleship operating without a confirmed resupply schedule within its sustainment window is violating deployment doctrine. **This is non-negotiable.** The tonnage is too great and the fuel spine dependency too direct.

### 5.5 Capital Ships — Dreadnaughts, Titans, Leviathans

Strategic assets. Their logistics requirements are campaign-level considerations, not individual deployment decisions.

| Class | Crew | Operational cycle | Yard requirement |
|-------|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Dreadnaught | 8,000 | 60–90 days maximum sustained (crew rotation required) | Major Regional Yard, 4–8 week cycle |
| Titan | 25,000 | Campaign duration only; requires forward support fleet | Helion Prime-level facility or equivalent |
| Leviathan | 60,000 | Strategic operation only; self-sustaining elements | Helion Prime primary yards; no alternative currently exists |

**Leviathan logistics** is a planning domain unto itself. A Leviathan deployment requires: dedicated fuel spine corridor reservation, advance depot prestock (FME-200+ at each planned stop), forward tender assignment (minimum three FT-series), crew rotation schedule, medical logistics integration, and material pre-positioning for emergency repair. Planning begins 18 months before deployment. FLC maintains one standing Leviathan deployment plan per strategic axis. The plan is updated annually.

### 5.6 The Scout Class — Extended Deployment Profile

The scout class is a **mission designation**, not a combat class. Scouts are designed to operate far beyond the primary logistics network — sometimes by months — in environments where resupply is improvised, degraded, or simply absent.

**The Kestrel-class profile:**
- FME-1 consumption; lowest of any Fleet class
- Standard patrol duration: 60–90 days to planned resupply
- Extended Deployment Protocol (EDP): 120–180 days with forward cache dependency
- Beyond EDP: mission abort protocol mandatory

What this looks like in practice:

| Resupply method | Frequency | Reliability | Notes |
|-----------------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Planned Grade-B depot contact | Every 60 days | High (core/mid-rim) | Nominal |
| Forward cache pre-positioned | As available | Variable (frontier edge) | Cache may be low, outdated, or inaccessible |
| Mobile Tender rendezvous | Mission-specific | Variable | Requires advance coordination |
| Emergency cross-class transfer | Emergency only | Low | Protocol exists; execution under field conditions is difficult |
| Local improvisation | Last resort | Low | Mission abort preferred; improvisation is not a logistics plan |

**FLC assessment of scout extended operations:** Scouts beyond EDP parameters are operating on institutional optimism, not logistics planning. Forward caches in Neutral Zone corridor approaches are pre-positioned on best-effort schedules. FLC Frontier Adjacency reports annually on cache stock currency. The report is not comfortable reading.

## 6. SHIPYARD & MAINTENANCE SYSTEM

### 6.1 Three-Tier Maintenance Structure

Fleet maintenance is divided into three tiers by depth of work, facility requirement, and authorization level:

**Tier 1 — Forward Maintenance (Field Level)**
- Location: Anywhere the ship is operating; any forward depot with maintenance bay
- Scope: Emergency repairs, patch-level hull work, system resets, consumable replacement
- Authorization: Commanding Officer + Chief Engineer sign-off
- Classification: **Operational Workaround (OW)**
- Key limitation: An OW flag does not mean the problem is solved. It means the problem is deferred.

**Tier 2 — Regional Maintenance (Mid-Level)**
- Location: Major relay depots, regional yards (Caranth and equivalents)
- Scope: Component replacement, drive system maintenance, shield matrix resupply, structural repair within defined limits
- Authorization: Regional Logistics Command certification required
- Duration: 5–21 days
- Classification: **Maintenance Certified (MC)**

**Tier 3 — Full Overhaul (Primary Yards)**
- Location: Helion Prime yards, two regional full-grade facilities
- Scope: Hull resurfacing, drive replacement, full structural inspection, full weapons system overhaul, certification renewal
- Authorization: Yard Certification Office (Form RC-12, signed by Commander Vaskar's office or designated authority)
- Duration: Class-dependent; Scout: 2–4 weeks; Frigate: 6–8 weeks; Battleship: 4–6 months; Leviathan: 12–18 months
- Classification: **Yard Certified (YC)**

### 6.2 Certification Pass Culture

Fleet vessels carry a maintenance certification board visible to boarding logistics officers at any port of call. The board shows:

- Last yard-certified date and facility
- Outstanding OW flags (deferred repairs)
- MC-level work completed since last YC certification
- System-specific certification status (propulsion, shields, weapons, life support, sensors)

**The Koss Standard:** Named informally after Senior Engineer Elara Koss (Kestrel Veil), who codified the institutional distinction between a hull that reads certified and a hull that is actually ready:

> *"Certification tells you what was signed. The vibration in Reing 3 tells you what's true."*

OW flags that accumulate without Tier 2 or Tier 3 resolution produce a condition FLC Readiness calls **"patched, not repaired"** — the ship looks operational on the board, may even pass field certification, but is operating with deferred structural or systems debt. The longer the debt accumulates, the higher the probability of cascading failure under operational stress.

FLC Readiness Assessment Division tracks the aggregate OW flag count across the fleet as a **systemic health indicator**. High OW flag counts correlate directly with emergency repair incidents at frontier nodes.

### 6.3 Yard Scheduling Doctrine

Helion Prime yards and the two regional full-grade facilities operate on a rolling **slip schedule** — the number of active maintenance berths (slips) available at any time. Slip count is the scarcest readiness resource in the Fleet.

**Slip count** is a primary metric tracked by the Logistics Commander daily.

**Scheduling priority (descending):**
1. Emergency repair — vessel unable to operate safely
2. YC certification expiry — overdue for full overhaul
3. Pre-deployment readiness certification — vessels within 60 days of planned deployment
4. Scheduled overhaul — vessels in normal cycle
5. Discretionary upgrade — vessels with performance improvement requests

When slip count is constrained (which is most of the time), vessels at lower priority wait. Vessels that wait accumulate OW flags. The cycle perpetuates.

**Helion Prime slip capacity:** classified at full-count level; FLC planning documents reference "sufficient for steady-state peacetime operations with limited surge capacity." Translation: adequate until it isn't.

### 6.4 Readiness Curves

FLC Readiness Assessment Division tracks each vessel class on a **readiness curve** — a model that predicts increasing maintenance probability as time passes since last Tier 3 overhaul.

All vessel classes experience increasing maintenance probability after:
- Scout: 18 months post-YC
- Frigate: 24 months post-YC
- Cruiser: 30 months post-YC
- Battleship: 36 months post-YC
- Capital assets: class-specific modeling

Vessels past their curve inflection point are scheduled for priority yard time. Vessels past their hard deadline are flagged for AMBER or RED classification and may not be deployed without Logistics Commander waiver.

## 7. DEPLOYMENT SUSTAINABILITY MODELS

### 7.1 Operational Posture Classifications

FLC models Fleet deployments against four standard posture types:

| Posture | Description | Sustainment Duration | Supply Requirement |
|---------|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **ROUTINE PATROL** | Standard lane patrol, planned resupply | Indefinite with scheduled resupply | Core/Mid-Rim support only |
| **EXTENDED PATROL** | Beyond standard resupply window, EDP active | 120–180 days max (scout) / 90–120 days (frigate) | Forward cache dependency |
| **SURGE OPERATION** | Concentrated task group, elevated consumption | 30–60 days before resupply demand | Pre-positioned depot stock required |
| **CRISIS OPERATION** | Maximum sustained effort, cross-class dependency | 14–30 days before critical resupply | Emergency reserve draw authorized |

### 7.2 The Scout Sustainability Edge

The scout class — specifically the Kestrel profile — represents Fleet's longest self-sustaining deployment type by mission design, even though scouts are individually the smallest vessels.

A fully loaded scout departing a Grade-B depot can sustain operations for:
- **60 days** on fuel alone (nominal EDP entry point)
- **120 days** with confirmed forward cache sequence
- **150+ days** with improvised cache access and careful fuel management
- **Beyond 150 days:** mission abort mandatory; crew safety becomes primary concern

The institutional assumption embedded in Fleet doctrine is that scouts will find usable caches within frontier corridors because FLC Frontier Adjacency places them there. The institutional reality is that cache stock currency in Neutral Zone approach corridors is variable. FLC quarterly reports quantify the gap. The gap exists.

### 7.3 When Ships Exceed Their Supply Profile

**For scouts:** Deteriorating fuel state; degraded sensor performance as systems are powered down to conserve; crew fatigue compounding decision quality. The vessel is a data-delivery risk as much as a life safety risk — a scout that cannot return is a scout whose observations die with it.

**For frigates and cruisers:** Reduced patrol coverage, missed lane assignments, cascading schedule impacts on relay depots expecting resupply handoffs. Operationally costly, rarely life-threatening unless in contested space.

**For capital ships:** This represents a command failure requiring after-action review. Capital ships that exceed their supply profile were either sent beyond their sustainment window (planning failure) or consumed more than projected (operational failure). Both are investigated.

### 7.4 The Institutional Tension

FLC's job is to tell Fleet Command what is sustainable. Fleet Command's job is to use the fleet.

These two mandates are not compatible under surge conditions. FLC publishes sustainment windows. Fleet Command treats them as advisory when timelines are pressing. FLC publishes them again, with annotations. Fleet Command acknowledges them and proceeds.

This is how it works. Both sides understand it. FLC documents it. Fleet Command accepts the documentation as a form of institutional liability transfer.

## 8. READINESS REPORTING & METRICS

### 8.1 Fleet Readiness Index (FRI)

The Fleet Readiness Index is the primary aggregate metric reported by FLC to Fleet Command and civilian political leadership.

FRI is calculated across four components:

| Component | Weight | Measures |
|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Fuel State (FS)** | 30% | Aggregate FME stock across depots vs. operational demand |
| **Hull Certification (HC)** | 30% | Percentage of deployed fleet within YC certification and below OW flag threshold |
| **Supply Chain Health (SCH)** | 20% | Throughput per month (TPM) across the logistics network; ordnance and materiel stock levels |
| **Deployment Sustainability (DS)** | 20% | Available sustainment window for current deployment posture |

FRI is reported on a 0–100 scale. FLC reports to Fleet Command weekly.

### 8.2 Vessel Readiness Classifications

| Classification | Code | Meaning | Deployment Status |
|---------------|------|---------|------------------|
| **GREEN-FULL** | GF | Fully certified, no outstanding OW flags, supply state adequate | Deploy without restriction |
| **GREEN-LIMITED** | GL | Certified, minor OW flags below threshold, supply state adequate | Deploy with flag notation |
| **AMBER-LIMITED** | AL | OW flags above threshold, or approaching certification deadline, or supply state reduced | Deploy only with Logistics Commander waiver |
| **AMBER-DEGRADED** | AD | Multiple OW flags in critical systems, or post-certification deadline, or fuel state below 40% | Deploy only under Fleet Command authorization |
| **RED-DEGRADED** | RD | Critical system OW flags unresolved, fuel state below 20%, or hull cert expired | Do not deploy pending Tier 2 or Tier 3 maintenance |
| **BLACK-EMERGENCY** | BE | Vessel in emergency; operation has deviated beyond all sustainment parameters | Emergency protocol; all resources diverted |

### 8.3 What a Logistics Officer Tracks Daily

The standard FLC watch officer monitors:

- **TPM (Throughput Per Month):** Total materiel mass moving through each depot tier; deviation from baseline triggers review
- **TSC (Tonnage Slip Count):** Active yard berths vs. vessels awaiting maintenance; slip count pressure drives prioritization
- **FRI-Component Deltas:** Any single component dropping more than 5 points in 72 hours triggers mandatory notification
- **OW Flag Accumulation Rate:** Fleet-wide; daily count; trend is as important as absolute number
- **Cache Stock Currency Reports:** Frontier edge; weekly; accuracy is often approximate beyond Meric-7
- **Forward Deployment Fuel States:** Scout and extended patrol vessels; tracked individually past EDP entry

### 8.4 What Gets Hidden or Softened

Fleet Logistics Command is required to report accurately. It does.

However, the following are known institutional softening patterns:

- **OW flag backlog:** Reported as aggregate. Individual vessel OW severity is in appendices. Appendices are frequently not read by Fleet Command briefers.
- **Cache stock currency:** Reported as "estimated stock at last confirmation date." The confirmation date may be 90 days old for frontier caches.
- **Readiness curves past inflection:** Vessels are technically still AMBER-LIMITED, not RED-DEGRADED. Technically.
- **Sustainment windows:** Reported as maximum. "Maximum" assumes no operational deviation, no unexpected consumption, and fully stocked forward caches. These assumptions are noted in footnotes.

> *"If it's in the footnote, it's real. Read the footnotes."*
> — Standard Logistics Command new-officer orientation, credited to Deputy Commander Readiness

## 9. THE FRONTIER LOGISTICS CHALLENGE

### 9.1 Core vs. Frontier Network Contrast

| Factor | Core Sector | Frontier Edge |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Depot density | High (Grade-A within 20 lane-days) | Low to none |
| Cache currency | Updated quarterly | Updated annually, best effort |
| Maintenance access | Tier 2 within 10 days | None within operational range |
| Communication relay | Real-time or near-real-time | Latency 2–7 days; gaps possible |
| Redundant supply paths | Reliable | Single-path routine |
| FLC situational awareness | High | Low to approximate |

FLC's operational picture of the frontier is, institutionally speaking, **a projection** — a model based on what was true when last surveyed, adjusted for estimated drift. It is better than nothing. It is not ground truth.

### 9.2 The Neutral Zone as a Logistics Problem

The Neutral Zone appears in Fleet logistics documentation as a volume of space with:
- Minimal fixed Fleet infrastructure
- Pre-positioned caches at corridor approach waypoints (currency variable)
- No confirmed Grade-B or Grade-A depot presence
- No reliable relay coverage for real-time status updates

From a logistics officer's perspective, the Neutral Zone is not a strategic buffer — it is a **supply black box**. Vessels operating beyond the approach corridors are beyond confirmed logistical support. They are relying on pre-positioned caches that may or may not be adequate, and on communication infrastructure that may or may not be current.

FLC Frontier Adjacency has requested an upgrade to Neutral Zone corridor approach infrastructure in every budget cycle for twelve years. The requests have been acknowledged. The budget has been allocated to core-sector readiness.

### 9.3 What FLC Sees vs. What's There

**FLC picture of the NZ approach corridors:**
- Forward caches at six waypoints (as filed)
- Stock currency: "estimated adequate" at last survey
- Patrol support: Scout-class EDP operations, intermittent contact, expected

**What is actually there:**
- Caches at four of six waypoints confirmed functional at last survey
- One cache below minimum stock threshold at last survey (notation in Frontier Adjacency appendix, Quarterly Review 7.4)
- One cache location potentially compromised by an unreported navigation hazard (notation in supplemental, same review)
- Extended lane traffic — origin and classification uncertain — noted in several scout contact logs but not aggregated into formal FLC intelligence picture

The gap between FLC's forward-facing picture and the frontier reality is not unique to the Neutral Zone. It is the standard condition at the edge of the network. FLC manages it through documentation, conservative sustainment windows, and mandatory EDP abort thresholds.

### 9.4 The Kestrel Operational Reality

A scout operating on extended EDP in Neutral Zone corridor approaches:
- Has a fuel state FLC cannot confirm in real time
- Is approaching caches that may or may not be stocked as expected
- Cannot call for assistance and expect a rapid response
- Is making maintenance decisions without Tier 2 access
- Is operating on the accumulated OW flag debt of whatever it left port with

The Kestrel Veil departed on its last recorded mission with two outstanding OW flags below threshold (Tier 1 notation). These were deferred pending return to Grade-B availability. Standard practice for extended deployment. Normal. The flags are in the file.

## 10. INDUSTRIAL INTELLIGENCE & THREAT ASSESSMENT

### 10.1 What FLC Processes from Foreign Observation

When Fleet Intelligence or operational scouts return observations of foreign facilities, FLC translates them through a specific analytical lens:

| Intelligence observation | FLC translation | What FLC wants to know |
|--------------------------|-----------------|------------------------|
| Yard throughput rate | Ships per year; comparable to Fleet reference baseline | Are they building faster than us? |
| Fuel depot concentration | Fuel spine geometry; sustained operation radius | How far can they project without resupply? |
| Maintenance layering | Operational depth; how long before their hulls degrade | Are they surging or sustaining? |
| Reserve baseline | Stockpile depth; how long they can operate without new production | Is this mobilization or preparation? |
| Traffic patterns | Lane utilization; civilian vs. military throughput differential | Are they moving for war or commerce? |

### 10.2 Segment Seven — Logistics Officer Interpretation

The observations from segment seven (Neutral Zone corridor approach, classified) are, to a logistics officer, not a question of whether something is there.

They are a question of **what kind of there it is**.

The FLC Industrial Intelligence interpretation of segment seven-equivalent data:

**If fuel spine depth is comparable to Fleet reference baseline:** The observed entity has strategic range equivalent to Fleet. It is not a frontier security force.

**If maintenance layering shows active yard cycling at depth:** The entity is not a surge force — it is a sustained operational posture. This takes years to build and sustain.

**If reserve baselines are above Fleet equivalent:** The entity is preparing to operate independently of resupply for extended periods. This is a war-planning indicator, not a peacetime efficiency metric.

**If the lane traffic pattern shows civilian/military throughput integration:** The entity has a full logistics economy behind it. What Fleet is seeing is not the capability. It is the surface.

FLC's institutional instinct when confronted with this data: **crash procurement, accelerated yard scheduling, immediate resupply chain surge.** The request is ready before the briefing is complete. The request will be approved or not approved by Fleet Command. FLC will document both outcomes.

### 10.3 The Institutional Conflict

**FLC position:** The observations imply peer-scale sustained operational capability. The response window to match peer-scale readiness is measured in years. Begin now.

**Fleet Command position (General Rowan framing):** The data is genuine. The interpretation may be premature. Act without evidence of intent and you announce awareness before understanding.

**FLC response:** Readiness does not require confirmed intent. Readiness requires confirmed capability. The capability is confirmed. The timeline is the problem.

This conflict is not new. FLC has been this version of alarmed before. Fleet Command has been this version of cautious before. Both positions have institutional merit. The documentation record is extensive on both sides.

The difference this time is the data quality. FLC knows the difference between estimated foreign capacity and observed yard throughput. Segment seven is observed throughput.

## 11. EMERGENCY & CRISIS LOGISTICS

### 11.1 Scout Emergency Return Protocol

When a damaged or fuel-critical scout arrives at any FLC facility — planned or unplanned — the receiving depot activates **Emergency Return Protocol (ERP)**:

**Immediate actions (0–4 hours):**
1. Fuel state assessment and emergency refuel authorization (no approval required; automatic for any vessel below 15% FME)
2. Medical evaluation of crew; life support critical assessment
3. Commanding Officer debrief intake — logistics officer assigns ERP-1 case number
4. Data recovery priority assessment — if vessel is in emergency, what did it observe?

**Within 24 hours:**
1. Hull assessment for Tier 1/2 triage
2. OW flag audit — document what the vessel arrived with vs. what occurred during deployment
3. Priority slot request to nearest Tier 2 facility if hull status is RED-DEGRADED
4. Incident summary to Regional Logistics Command and FLC operations watch

**The data courier problem:** FLC doctrine is clear that damaged scout data is a priority payload. If the vessel cannot transmit, the data travels with the crew. If the crew is incapacitated, the data stays with the vessel until a cleared FLC data officer retrieves it. Information logistics is logistics too. Form ERP-3 governs data custody chain.

### 11.2 Triage at Regional Yards

When a damaged vessel arrives at a regional yard, the triage sequence is:
1. **Life safety** — crew, medical
2. **Strategic data** — if scout or intelligence vessel, data is extracted before hull work begins
3. **Mission continuation assessment** — can the vessel be returned to operations within the sustainment window?
4. **Hull triage** — Tier 1 patch or Tier 2 repair decision within 48 hours
5. **Fleet Command notification** — if vessel is AMBER-DEGRADED or worse on arrival, Fleet Command is notified within 12 hours

### 11.3 How Crisis Conditions Expose Logistics Gaps

Crisis conditions — particularly in frontier or Neutral Zone corridor operations — consistently expose the same FLC vulnerabilities:

- **Cache stock assumptions:** Vessels planned resupply around cache availability that was not confirmed at departure. The assumption was close enough in normal operations. In crisis, close enough is not close enough.
- **Single-path dependency:** Extended operations that accepted single-path supply exposure produce no fallback when the primary path is cut, blocked, or exhausted.
- **OW flag inheritance:** Vessels that departed with OW flags under threshold hit stress conditions and discover that the threshold was optimistic.
- **Communication lag:** FLC learns about crisis conditions when vessels arrive — or when they don't arrive. The frontier information lag means FLC is reactive, not proactive.

FLC Frontier Adjacency has a phrase for this: **"We find out when they dock or when they don't."**

## 12. THE LOGISTICS OFFICER CAREER

### 12.1 Academy Track

Fleet Academy logistics curriculum is handled under the Applied Operations division. Logistics track cadets:
- Take three terms of supply chain theory and fleet logistics modeling
- Complete simulation exercises in depot management, readiness tracking, and sustainment window calculation
- Graduate with Logistics Branch designation (gold-and-grey insignia — less ceremony than combat track; more math)

FLC's assessment of Academy logistics preparation: **technically adequate, practically soft.** The simulations use clean data. The real network does not.

New logistics officers are assigned 18-month rotations at Tier 1 or Tier 2 depot facilities before any FLC assignment. The purpose is cultural as much as technical: they need to understand what "approximately stocked" means in practice at 0300 when a frigate needs refueling.

### 12.2 The Culture of Quantitative Competence

FLC officers are evaluated on one primary quality before any other: **accuracy of projection**.

If you told Logistics Command that a depot could refuel four frigates simultaneously and it turned out to be three, that is noted. If it was two, that is reviewed. If you did not know and said you knew, that ends careers faster than failure.

FLC does not reward optimism. It rewards accurate pessimism followed by plans that work anyway.

## 13. SUPPLY CHAIN VULNERABILITIES

### 13.1 Known Weak Points

FLC documents its own vulnerabilities annually in the **Network Exposure Assessment (NEA)**, a classified internal report distributed to Fleet Command and the Logistics Commander's senior staff. Key findings from the current NEA:

**1. Frontier Adjacency RLC Understaffing**
Frontier Adjacency RLC operates at 68% authorized staffing. Cache stock currency reporting is degraded. Real-time situational awareness of extended deployment vessels in NZ corridor approaches is sporadic.

**2. Single-Spine Dependency**
The fuel spine connecting Helion Prime to Meric-7 has no parallel redundant path at Grade-A level. A sustained disruption to the spine at any point between Caranth and Meric-7 degrades mid-rim operational capacity immediately.

**3. Predictable Resupply Windows**
Patrol routes create predictable resupply timing. Vessels that visit Grade-B depots on regular schedules create observable patterns. FLC knows this. The answer — randomized resupply scheduling — creates operational inefficiency. Fleet Command has consistently chosen efficiency.

**4. Helion Prime Yard Concentration**
Full overhaul capability for capital ships above Battleship is concentrated at Helion Prime. Two regional Grade-A facilities exist, but neither has full Titan-class maintenance capacity. Leviathan overhaul exists at Helion Prime only. This concentration is known, documented, and regarded as an acceptable risk in peacetime.

**5. Neutral Zone Cache Exposure**
If an adversarial force with knowledge of cache locations acted against them in advance of a conflict, extended deployment vessels in NZ corridor approaches would be operating on empty. FLC has no current model for the adversary knowing where the caches are. This assumption has not been formally reviewed in seven years.

### 13.2 Dominion Logistics as Analytical Reference

FLC Industrial Intelligence uses **Fleet logistics as the reference class** for what a major power's logistics infrastructure looks like. When comparing observed foreign infrastructure to this reference:

The Dominion (from observed indicators, NZ approach vicinity) shows:
- Fuel depot concentration pattern consistent with sustained corridor operations, not frontier patrol
- Maintenance layering at depth (multiple tier equivalents active simultaneously)
- Reserve baselines above Fleet equivalent at observed nodes

**FLC assessment:** This is not a mirror of Fleet logistics. It is a developed logistics model with different optimization — deeper reserves, longer corridors, more pre-positioned maintenance. The Dominion does not appear to have optimized for rapid expansion. It has optimized for **sustained operations at distance**.

This is a qualitatively different logistics philosophy from Fleet's. Fleet optimizes for range and flexibility. The observed pattern optimizes for duration and depth.

A civilization that builds logistics for duration over flexibility is not preparing for a quick war.

Fleet Admiral Rowan's restraint is not logistical. It is interpretive: the data shows capability, not intent. Acting on capability alone risks a spiral.

FLC does not have an opinion on spirals. FLC has an opinion on timelines. The timeline does not improve with delay.

### 15.3 What Changes If the Neutral Zone Is Not Empty

From a logistics officer's perspective, this is not primarily a strategic crisis. It is an **infrastructure exposure crisis**. FLC's immediate operational question: what can be moved, resupplied, and hardened before the exposure matters?

The crash procurement debate follows from this. Not from fear. From math.

## D. KEY TERMINOLOGY

**Fuel Spine** — The primary chain of Grade-A and Grade-B fixed fuel depots along Fleet's main operational corridor, maintained at 90% capacity minimum.

**Extended Deployment Protocol (EDP)** — Operational authorization for scout and frigate operations beyond standard resupply window; requires confirmed forward cache sequence.

**Patched, Not Repaired** — Institutional FLC term for a vessel carrying unresolved OW flags that make it technically certified but operationally fragile. Associated with Engineer Koss; now general FLC vocabulary.

**Slip Count** — Number of active maintenance berths available at yard facilities; the primary scarcest readiness resource.

**Readiness Curve** — FLC model predicting increasing maintenance probability as function of time since last Tier 3 overhaul.

**Throughput** — Total materiel mass moving through a depot node per time period; primary logistics network health indicator.

**Sustainment Window** — Maximum duration Fleet can maintain a given operational posture before mandatory resupply; published by FLC; treated as advisory by Fleet Command; becomes binding in crisis.

**Cache Stock Currency** — Measure of how recently a forward cache's stock levels were confirmed by direct survey vs. estimated from last contact date.

**Industrial Baseline** — FLC's reference model of what a major power's production and logistics infrastructure looks like; currently assumes Fleet standard as reference class.

**Mobilization vs. Preparation** — FLC Industrial Intelligence's key analytical distinction: a mobilized force is surging from baseline; a prepared force has built its baseline to operational levels already. Preparation is harder to detect because there is no spike.
