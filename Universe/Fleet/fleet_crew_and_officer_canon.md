# 1. New Design Goal

Fleet ships must feel like:

- military ecosystems
- not full civilian cities

Even the largest vessels must remain narratively trackable and character-relevant.

# 3. Design Principles (Updated)

- Ships must remain narratively legible environments
- Crew sizes must support character-driven storytelling
- Even largest vessels must allow:
  - recognizable internal departments
  - meaningful interpersonal dynamics
  - believable command structure

---

# 4. Officer Scaling (Canon)

Officer scaling reflects reduced total crew. Narrative focus stays on command teams and department heads — not every crew member at scale.

---

## Scout–Frigate

**Total crew:** 20–150

**Command model:** Small centralized command teams

- Captain + Executive Officer as primary decision loop
- Department heads often dual-hat or cross-trained
- Officers know most crew by name or face
- Narrative can track the majority of ship personnel when needed

---

## Cruiser–Heavy Cruiser

**Total crew:** 300–600

**Command model:** Moderate multi-team coordination

- Distinct departments (engineering, tactical, navigation, medical, communications)
- Watch officers and department heads form the narrative surface
- Crew beyond department heads exists but remains background unless scene requires them

---

## Battleship

**Total crew:** 2,000

**Command model:** Large structured command hierarchy

- Full flag-ready command structure with dedicated staff sections
- Narrative focuses on bridge, CIC, and key department heads — not all 2,000
- Boarding and internal action remain plausible at human scale

---

## Fleet Carrier

**Total crew:** 3,000 (+ 400 fighters)

**Command model:** Carrier air-group coordination layered on battleship-scale hull discipline

- Flight operations, deck crews, and strike coordination are distinct narrative zones
- Command scenes emphasize air boss, strike lead, and flag coordination — not entire complement

---

## Dreadnaught–Leviathan

**Total crew:** 8,000–60,000

**Command model:** Multi-department command systems — still comprehensible by narrative focus

- Strategic command decks, fleet coordination centers, and major departments are identifiable
- Narrative follows flag staff, department heads, and mission-critical teams
- **Not** population-scale cities — large military complements with defined zones
- Boarding actions target sections, not entire vessels simultaneously

---

# 5. Leviathan Redesign Rule (Important)

Leviathans are no longer "mobile cities."

They are:

> strategic super-capital warships with large but controlled crew complements

They remain:

- rare
- terrifying
- fleet-defining

But they are **NOT** population-scale habitats.

---

# 6. Narrative Benefit Requirement

This revision must improve:

- character visibility in large ships
- boarding action plausibility
- command clarity in battle scenes
- emotional readability of fleet engagements

No ship should feel too large to meaningfully depict human-scale stories.

---

# 7. Consistency Lock

All future references must use this revised scale.

This overrides all previous crew size values — including any draft assumptions of Leviathan-scale (200,000+) or Titan-scale city populations.

Any deviation must be explicitly justified in-story (understaffed deployment, emergency evacuation, temporary augmentation, or classified configuration).

# 8. Book One — Kestrel Veil Instance Note

Fleet standard Scout complement: **20**.

Book One narrative foregrounds **fourteen principal personnel** (six senior officers + eight named support crew). Remaining complement slots may be watch-standers, rotating specialists, or unfilled billets — not required in every scene.

See: `Characters/Support_Crew/support_crew.md` · `Books/Book_01_The_Kestrel_Veil_Incident/Notes/kestrel_veil.md`

Chapter prose references to "fourteen people" describe the **dramatis crew family**, not the Fleet maximum complement.
