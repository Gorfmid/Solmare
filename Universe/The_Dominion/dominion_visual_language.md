## Core Principle

Readers should **recognize Dominion scenes before reading the chapter heading**.

Visual language works alongside proverbs, holidays, and professional ritual — not instead of them. The reader should *see* whose world they are in before the POV header confirms it.

**Do not force motifs.**

Allow them to **recur naturally**.

Over time, readers should immediately feel they have entered **Dominion space**.

---

## Writing Rules

1. **One or two visual motifs per scene** — texture, not checklist.
2. **Never explain motifs on the page** — characters do not notice their own civilization's aesthetics as tourism.
3. **Earned repetition** — the same images may recur across books; meaning accumulates through context.
4. **Ordinary settings first** — offices, transit, yards, border nodes, schools, homes. Not only directorates and command chambers.
5. **Age is visible** — Dominion spaces feel **inherited**, not newly built. Fleet spaces feel comparatively recent; Dominion spaces feel **layered**.
6. **Recognition before naming** — especially in Act III: Dominion Thread scenes should read Dominion even if the faction name is withheld from Fleet POV.

## Recurring Visual Motifs (Series Canon)

| Motif | Usage notes |
|-------|-------------|
| **Gloves removed** before important work | Serious talk, packets, numbers, decisions — courtesy to the task |
| **Tea** | Planning rooms, directorates, commons — too strong is correct |
| **Old stone and old metal** | Basalt-and-steel complexes, border installations, yards — new composite over limestone, weld scars under paint |
| **Gold thread** | Harvest festival banners — anticipatory, not yet garish; holiday texture |
| **Corridor maps** | Planning tables, wall displays, traffic geometry — civilization as scheduling |
| **Plaques** | Border stations, anterooms, campaign names in old script — history on walls |
| **Rain** | Rain screens, transit walks, cold corridor air — weather as engineered atmosphere |
| **Layered architecture** | Centuries of revision visible — older fabric under newer installs |
| **Inherited tools** | Coffee pots, slates, cranes, chairs with worn armrest creases — used by generations |
| **Worn handrails** | Gantries, transit, watch stations — elbows agree on the same spacing |
| **Family photographs** | Desks, lockers, safety boards — private life inside institutional duty |
| **Historical inscriptions** | Plaque text, old script, school lessons on walls — events everyone already knows |

Not every scene needs every motif. **Two well-chosen details** often do more than six.

## Contrast with Fleet Visual Language

Fleet Authority scenes tend toward: industrial aurora (**Second Sunrise**), **copper and blue** bunting, glass terraces, foundry light, newer installs, expansion optimism in architecture.

Dominion scenes tend toward: **depth, patina, inheritance, geometry on walls**, celebration through **gold thread** and sacrifice-narrative ritual (**harvest festival** — not Founders' Week).

Show contrast; never explain it.

Fleet visual texture: `Universe/cultural_touchstones.md` → § Fleet Authority

---

## Do Not

- Checklist every motif in a single scene
- Describe Dominion spaces as generic sci-fi military sets
- Make every room identical — member-world and station variation exists within the visual language
- Explain gold thread, plaques, or glove ritual to the reader
- Use Dominion visual motifs in Fleet POV scenes (unless observing from outside — rare)
