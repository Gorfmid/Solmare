## 1. Overview

The Dominion is first a civilization, then a military power. Its institutions — civil government, industry, education, science, transportation — have existed for centuries, and civilian life carries that age in everything: in the architecture, the schoolroom walls, the proverbs grandmothers teach grandchildren, the market stalls that open every morning regardless of what the Directorates are deliberating. Civilian life is not separate from institutional health; it is evidence of it.

Dominion citizens do not relate to the state as subjects to a ruler, nor as free individuals asserting rights against authority. The relationship is closer to what a family member feels toward a household they were raised in: the household shaped them, they carry it forward, and they feel responsible for its continuation. Citizens genuinely believe the Dominion has survived this long because previous generations sacrificed to preserve it — and that their own contributions (long factory shifts, frontier duty, heavy taxation during buildup periods, sons and daughters posted far from home) are the same form of contribution made in different circumstances.

This is not propaganda successfully believed. It is a cultural framework inherited across generations, continuously reinforced by education, ritual, and the visible age of institutions. When a shipyard welder in Korrast operates machinery that his grandfather helped install, he is not told to feel continuity. He simply feels it.

**The state is experienced most directly through:**
- The educational system (intensely present throughout childhood)
- The Directorates (as employers, regulators, and seasonal logistical authorities)
- The military (through deployed family members, garrison units, and public funerals)
- The harvest festival (the one annual moment when civilian and institutional life explicitly converge)
- Local civil administration (district councils, permit offices, food and transport coordination)

Most ordinary citizens have little direct experience of the Strategic Security Directorate or the Intelligence Directorate. They experience the Dominion through its local faces: the teacher who recites old history, the district administrator who processes harvest festival leave requests, the transit official who posts freight window changes.

**The Dominion must feel lived in.** Every scene set in civilian space should carry at least one element — a pot of tea, a worn railing, a family photograph, a half-finished chess game — that suggests ordinary life has been continuing here for a very long time.

## 2. Social Structure

### The Civic-Military Spectrum

The Dominion does not have a sharp civilian/military divide. It has a spectrum. Every adult participates in civic life, and military service is one recognized form of civic contribution among several. Factory workers, teachers, scientists, freight operators, and district administrators are all understood, at some institutional level, to be performing functions within the civilization's preparedness framework — whether or not individual workers think of it in those terms.

Social standing is determined primarily by:

- **Profession**: the Directorates provide structured career paths; advancement within a Directorate confers recognized status without requiring military rank
- **Service record (family and personal)**: generational military service families carry specific social weight — not superiority over others, but a recognized form of contribution that communities respect and reference
- **Education level**: academy graduates, university-trained scientists, and senior technical specialists occupy a recognized professional class with real social mobility attached
- **Geography**: proximity to capital and major administrative centers carries status; frontier postings and agricultural worlds are respected but not prestigious in the way that core-world careers are

### Professional Classes

**The Administrative Class**: civil servants, district officers, Directorate functionaries. Educated, often from families with multi-generational service records. They move between postings across worlds; their identity is institutional rather than local.

**The Technical-Professional Class**: scientists, engineers, medical staff, senior logistics specialists. Highly educated; the Science Directorate commands significant prestige. A research lead at a Science Directorate institute outranks most civilian administrators in social terms, and this is broadly accepted.

**The Service Class**: factory workers, freight operators, market vendors, teachers at ordinary district schools, transit workers, agricultural specialists. The largest class. Educated but not academically credentialed. Local in identity — less likely to relocate between worlds; more likely to live in the same district their parents did, in housing that has been in the family for two or three generations.

**The Military Family Class**: overlaps all others. Families with long service records exist across every economic level. A shipyard welder from a five-generation service family and a district administrator whose grandfather commanded a frontier garrison both carry military heritage as a recognized form of civic identity. This is not a caste; it does not come with formal privileges. It comes with a kind of communal acknowledgment that is expressed in small, everyday ways: a neighbor who pauses to ask after a deployed sibling; a teacher who mentions a family's service history during a Review Assembly.

**The Frontier Class**: civilians at border stations and frontier worlds occupy a distinct social position — independent, pragmatic, geographically aware of the strategic situation in ways that core-world civilians are not. They are respected in Dominion culture as evidence of preparedness doctrine in practice, and often slightly resented by the same core-world civilians for implying, simply by existing at the border, that the comfortable distance between the administrative centers and the frontier is a luxury, not a neutral fact.

### Does the Divide Exist?

The honest answer is: the divide exists on paper and dissolves in practice. A factory worker producing components for shipyard cranes understands, at some level, that the cranes are necessary because the shipyards are necessary because the fleet must be maintained. They may not think about this explicitly while on shift. But when a supervisor says "we run this plant the way it needs to be run," the remark carries an implicit weight that workers recognize without needing it named.

## 3. Daily Rhythms

### The Working Day

Dominion cities run on shift culture. Industrial worlds operate continuous cycles; administrative centers and research stations use a standard six-shift rotation across the local planetary day-length, adjusted per member world. Most civilian workers identify with their shift group rather than with an abstract clock hour. A "third-shift person" is a social identity, not just a schedule description.

A standard working week runs eight shifts across eleven days, with three rest days. Shift pairs develop their own social character — a third-shift crew at a Korrast factory will have their own café table, their own running arguments, their own small commemorative habits at shift transitions.

**Morning routines** are built around the shift change:
- Tea brewed before departure (rarely skipped; the pot is a household object with specific identity)
- Brief review of district notice boards (freight announcements, public memorial notices, school calendar updates)
- Commute by transit; Dominion cities developed transit infrastructure early, before private transport was widely available, and the habit is deep

**Midday** varies by profession. Factory workers eat in canteens; scientists in institute cafeterias; administrators in department rooms or adjacent cafés. Meals are functional — quick, shared where possible, rarely elaborate outside of festival season.

**Shift end** is the social transition: brief gathering at commons or café before transit home. This is when the chess board comes out. This is when veterans talk. This is when news circulates person-to-person rather than through official boards.

### Ordinary Professions: Daily Texture

**Factory worker (industrial world, e.g. Korrast shipyard district)**: Shift starts at machinery still warm from the previous crew. Personal tools are marked with initials or small carved notches — a welder may have a notch for every year of service. Midday canteen; conversation runs to sports results, material delivery complaints, and news of deployed relatives. Shift-end: sign the board, rinse, tea before transit. Monthly safety reviews; annual component inspection days that carry the weight of ritual even when they are not ceremonial.

**Scientist (Science Directorate research institute)**: Later start, irregular hours. Heavy documentation culture — Dominion science is bureaucratically meticulous, and the records go back far enough that institute researchers sometimes cite findings from three generations ago. Significant intellectual pride; colleagues reference previous institute directors the way military officers reference old commanders. Tea consumed in large quantities; pots go cold and are reheated without ceremony or comment.

**Administrator (civil service, district office)**: Among the first to arrive; among the last to leave during filing seasons and harvest festival surge. Heavy correspondence load. Handles permit applications, leave requests, freight coordination, public memorial logistics, school calendar approvals. Known throughout the district by name; the connective tissue between civilian life and institutional function.

**Market vendor**: Opens before transit begins. Knows regular customers across generations — a grandmother's vegetable stall may have been her mother's first; the measuring weights are inherited. Keeps a small board of district notices near the front display: harvest festival dates, memorial announcements, school pageant schedules. Closes after the afternoon transit wave; occasionally stays late during festival season.

**Teacher (district school)**: The most socially trusted profession in ordinary Dominion civilian life. Teachers carry the historical narrative in practical terms — they are who children first hear the proverbs from, before the Academy, before grandparents explain what the campaigns actually cost. School days run early; teachers stay late during harvest festival season for pageant rehearsals. Family-known; recognized at market; asked after at the café.

**Engineer (Transportation Directorate, freight logistics)**: Coordinate freight window scheduling around harvest festival pressure and military transport requirements. Map-and-schedule culture is native; corridor geometry is their professional language. Often deployed between worlds; maintain a district apartment but travel regularly. Strong esprit de corps within the Directorate — they are aware their work underpins everything else.

## 4. Education System

### Structure

Dominion education follows a recognized three-phase progression:

**Primary School** (ages 5–13): Universal across all member worlds. Funded and curriculum-standardized by the Civil Government, with regional application variance. Core subjects: Dominion history (framed through sacrifice narrative), mathematics, language and rhetoric, physical training, introductory logistics (presented as "how goods and people move between worlds, and why that matters"), and civic studies. The phrase *strategic depth* appears in civic studies no later than age ten — not as military terminology, but as a concept about why buffer space between expanding civilizations matters.

**Preparatory School** (ages 13–18): Tracks diverge here. The preparatory track feeds academies and universities; the civic track feeds Directorate apprenticeships and technical colleges. Both tracks include mandatory history — second year: frontier campaigns; fourth year: institutional history of the Directorates — and logistics continuation. Science Directorate academies draw from preparatory track graduates with strong applied sciences and mathematics.

**Academy and University** (ages 18–24, variable by field): Military academies weight logistics, history, and strategic theory alongside tactical training. Science academies weight applied research, documentation, and cross-discipline collaboration. Administrative colleges weight law, civil management, and Directorate procedure. All share one teaching characteristic: assessment relies heavily on historical case study. Students are evaluated not only on what they would do but on what the historical record shows others chose — and why those choices had the consequences they did.

### Children Learning Through Stories of Sacrifice

The Dominion does not teach its history as conquest. Expansion, campaigns, and border maintenance are presented through what previous generations endured to make the present possible. By age eight, most children can name at least two historical campaigns — not by their tactical outcomes but by the sacrifices that defined them.

Standard primary texts do not say "we won the Vereth Corridor campaigns." They say "the Vereth Corridor families gave three generations before the border held." The distinction is cultural, not legal. Dominion history is framed as a sequence of preserved things rather than taken things. This framing extends into all school ceremonial life.

### Review Assembly

Held twice yearly at every primary school, the Review Assembly requires each student to recite a passage from historical record. Not a battle summary — a personal account: a letter, a memorial inscription, a recorded testimony from someone who served and came home or did not. Teachers guide selection with attention to local history. The explicit goal is that students feel connected to the sacrifice, not merely proud of a result. Families are not required to attend; many do.

### Passage Day

The end of primary school is marked by Passage Day — a school ceremony in which each student's academic record is read aloud before their family, and the student formally selects their preparatory track in the presence of teachers and parents. It is the first civic choice a Dominion child makes publicly. The school provides tea and simple food; speeches are short. In harvest festival years, Passage Day is scheduled to avoid peak festival weeks.

### Military History in Curriculum

Military history is mandatory from preparatory school onward. It is not propaganda; it is analytic. Students are expected to identify logistics failures in historical campaigns, not only tactical outcomes. The standard orienting question in military history classes is: *"What did they not have enough of, and when did they know?"* Academy graduates consistently report that this question remains the organizing principle of their professional thinking for decades afterward.

### Regional vs. Central Education Variance

Central worlds follow curriculum closely; frontier and agricultural worlds have more teacher autonomy. A border-world primary school in Dreva Station may spend twice the recommended time on frontier campaign history because the teacher's family lived it. An agricultural world school may integrate harvest logistics directly into mathematics lessons in ways the central curriculum does not specify. This variation is tolerated without conflict because the core narrative — sacrifice, preparation, continuity — is not being contested, only emphasized differently.

## 5. Family Life

### Structure

Dominion families tend toward multi-generational cohesion, particularly in service families. A household may include grandparents, parents, and children under one roof or in close geographic proximity. This is partly cultural tradition and partly practical: grandparents are the primary transmitters of proverbs, family service history, and the emotional texture of what the campaigns actually cost. A family that loses its grandparent generation loses something specific — a living connection to the historical voice that the schoolbooks carry in abstract.

Family identity is substantially organized around service records. A family's service history is known, discussed, and displayed: photographs in service uniform alongside civilian photographs; medals in small cases; memorial cards for family members lost in frontier duty kept in the household. This is not a cult of militarism. It is a form of family memory — the same household that displays a grandfather's service record will also display his wedding photograph, his tools, and a letter he wrote home.

### Generational Service Records as Pride

The pride is not in rank achieved or engagements fought. It is in having served — in having been someone the civilization called upon, and having answered. A family whose three generations served on frontier duty does not necessarily produce people who want to command capital fleets. It produces people who believe that when the state requires something, you provide it. The pride is in the willingness, not the glory.

### Children and Absent Parents

Children in service families learn to relate to absent parents through two primary channels:

1. **Regular letters and short-burst communications** — frontier communications are slow; a letter may take weeks, arrive out of order, carry news already superseded. Children learn early that the date at the top of a letter matters, and that sequence is not always delivered in sequence.

2. **Stories from the parent who remained** — the grandmother or resident parent maintains the absent person's voice in the household. Their proverbs are repeated; their chair is not moved; their habits are referenced. A child grows up knowing, intimately, a person who is not physically present.

Children from these families often feel closer to history in a practical way. The campaigns in schoolroom textbooks are not abstractions; they are the places where relatives served, the geography behind the proverbs. History teachers in frontier-family communities know this and use it without explicit comment.

### Funerals and Promotions

**Public funerals** are the canonical Dominion form of military loss acknowledgment. When a Dominion service member dies, the community is informed and invited. Funeral processions move through the district. The public presence is understood as the civilization honoring the sacrifice — not as a performance of grief for institutional purposes, but as a genuine act of communal witness. Neighbors attend who did not know the deceased personally, because the loss belongs to everyone who benefits from the defense.

**Private promotions** are the inverse. Advancement is an internal, professional matter. Officers receive promotion orders in small institutional ceremonies. Families may receive brief communication. There is no parade, no district announcement. The accomplishment belongs to the institution; the sacrifice belongs to everyone.

### Household Rituals

- Tea brewed at consistent times; the household pot is associated with specific family members in specific ways (a grandfather's pot, a mother's pot, a pot kept from a former posting)
- Family meals are functionally rather than ceremonially structured; sitting together is the ritual itself
- Reading aloud in evenings is common in households with younger children — historical accounts, practical manuals, old letters
- Tools passed between generations — a carpenter's instruments, a mechanic's calibration kit, a scientist's annotated reference texts — are used, not retired to display

## 6. Home & Community

### Housing Across Economic Levels

Dominion housing reflects the civilization's age. Most structures are not new, and even recently built districts layer new construction over older foundations — good building sites are sites where buildings already stood. Dominion architecture does not pursue novelty for its own sake; additions are made as needed, visible as later layers on older fabric.

**Core-world urban housing**: multi-story residential blocks in clustered districts; stone-and-composite construction; worn stairwells with handrails smoothed by decades of use; windows that look into courtyards rather than outward. Districts are small enough that residents know their local market vendor and district administrator by face.

**Scientific world housing**: smaller scale; institute staff often live in campus-adjacent housing blocks; common areas are heavily used and show it. Research communities develop strong internal social cohesion partly because professional and domestic life are geographically inseparable.

**Agricultural world housing**: more dispersed; some structures older than anything on the capital worlds; family estates in continuous occupation across generations. The harvest festival is experienced most intensely here — it marks actual agricultural labor completed, not only a symbolic season.

**Frontier and border-station housing**: functional, often cramped; garrison-adjacent. Structures are utilitarian. The most visible feature is usually the memorial board: a public surface listing every previous occupant of the station — civilian staff and military alike — updated after each departure and each loss. The memorial board is the most consulted public surface in any frontier community.

### Neighborhood Structure

Dominion cities are organized in districts with named histories. A district name often carries historical weight: Verek District in Korrast takes its name from a commander who organized its first industrial settlement. Residents know this, reference it without emphasis, and feel the name as landscape rather than tribute.

Each district typically includes:
- A primary school, or shared school if the district is small
- At least one café functioning as the unofficial community center
- A memorial square with old stone listing names from the Long Campaigns and more recent service losses
- A district administrator's office handling permits, community notices, festival logistics
- A veterans' hall, or a section of the café serving the same purpose in smaller communities

### Community Meeting Spaces

**Cafés** are the central civilian gathering space. Not luxurious — usually small, stone-floored, with worn tables and a tea service running from before first shift to after last. A café will typically have one chess set that belongs to the café rather than any customer. Veterans gather here; teachers plan lessons here; engineers argue over schematics here; grandparents bring grandchildren on rest days. The café crosses professions and age groups. It is where information travels person-to-person.

**Veterans' halls** serve formally for pension administration and memorial coordination, and informally as evening gathering places. A veterans' hall in continuous use for more than a century will have accumulated wall plaques from every generation of service in that community. The Verek District hall in Korrast has a chess tournament that has been running for forty years without interruption; the scores are kept in a bound ledger that functions, in its way, as a memorial document.

**Public squares** in Dominion cities are dominated by the district memorial — old stone, often carved in an earlier script style, with names added in successive waves. The square is not primarily a market space or performance space. It is civic ground, and its function is memorial. Markets may operate near it; festivals may fill it; but the stone is the square's organizing presence, and everyone orients to it without being told to.

## 7. Food & Drink Culture

### Daily Meals

**Tea** is the default institutional and civilian drink, served strong enough that most outside visitors describe it as too strong. Dominion civilians drink tea before shift, at institutional breaks, with meals, and in the evening. The teapot is a household object with specific domestic identity — a family's everyday pot is often older than any living family member, and its particular flavor (the mineral accumulation of years of use) is what tea is supposed to taste like.

**Everyday staples**:

- **Korvash flatbread** — a standard baked grain product, dense and filling, available at every market and district canteen. Regional variants exist across the Dominion; the basic form is universal.
- **Bramsha** — a meat-and-root-vegetable stew that varies enormously by region, season, and available ingredients. The name is consistent; the dish is not. A frontier bramsha and a capital-city bramsha share a name and a general character; details differ by world. Associated with homecoming meals — when a service member returns from deployment, bramsha is what the household makes.
- **Stered** — pressed grain cakes, common as school lunch staple and shift-end snack. Not considered festive food; considered reliable food.
- **Pickled amber root** — a preserved vegetable present in almost every Dominion kitchen; a working-class pantry staple with a shelf life measured in years. Associated with frontier and agricultural worlds but used everywhere.

**Tea varieties**:
- *Black koral* is the dominant variety — dark, intensely brewed, consumed without sweetener in institutional settings; with sweetener in homes. The standard professional and domestic tea.
- *Pale koral* is a lighter variety associated with mornings, illness, and mourning. Military tradition holds that pale koral is appropriate only for the sick and the grieving; serving it to guests in a professional context would read as an unintentional comment on their state.

### Celebration Foods and the Harvest Festival

- **Goldcrust bread** — a sweetened, braided bread made specifically during harvest season; sold at market stalls and made in homes. The braid form is consistent across the Dominion; sweetness level varies by family and region. Children at district festivals receive individual goldcrust portions as a standard treat.
- **Harvest grain soup** — a celebratory variant of everyday soup, made with the first grain of the season; served at district festival gatherings and institutional cafeterias during festival days. The significance is in the timing, not the recipe.
- **Festival cider** — a pressed-fruit drink available during harvest season only; served hot in cold climates, cold in warm ones. Shared across age groups at district gatherings.

### Food as Community

Dominion food culture does not separate eating from gathering. When a family member returns from service, the household prepares bramsha not through formal tradition but through accumulated household habit — it is what return tastes like. When a district loses someone publicly at funeral, neighbors bring food to the family without being organized to do so. The food is present before any coordination requires it.

Institutional canteens and cafés serve food that is hot, consistent, and shared in common. The social function of eating in a canteen — seeing the same faces, hearing the same shift conversations — is not incidental to work culture. It is part of it.

## 8. Leisure & Culture

### Chess

Chess is the professional pastime of the Dominion. It appears in officers' commons, veterans' halls, Directorate waiting rooms, café tables, and research institute break rooms. It is not a sport; it is a conversational medium. Most games are accompanied by conversation; games are rarely completed in a single sitting, and resuming a game the following day is normal.

The standard Dominion chess set uses slightly elongated pieces with a matte finish — standardized enough that any Dominion player knows the set by feel. Frontier station chess sets are often noticeably worn, with pieces repaired or replaced with hand-carved substitutes over the years. This is considered evidence of a functioning community rather than evidence of neglect.

Chess is not explained or celebrated on the page. It appears as furniture.

### Music

Dominion music tradition distinguishes between **work songs** (associated with industrial and agricultural communities, call-and-response in form, rhythmic for labor coordination) and **memorial songs** (slower, harmonic, associated with public funerals and harvest festival ceremonies). The two forms do not mix. A memorial song played at a casual gathering would feel profoundly wrong to any Dominion civilian, in a way that they would feel before they could explain.

**District orchestral ensembles** exist in most larger communities — semi-professional, funded by district administration, performing at harvest festival events and memorial services. They are not prestige cultural institutions; they are civic infrastructure. Musicians hold day jobs.

Children learn simple traditional songs in primary school — not complex music education, but enough communal participation that the singing at harvest festival feels natural rather than performed.

### Storytelling Culture

Oral storytelling is a primary Dominion cultural form. Family members tell history stories; grandparents are expected to tell their grandchildren about the family's service history and the campaigns they or their parents knew. Teachers tell historical accounts. Veterans tell frontier stories to younger civilians in cafés.

The distinction between official history (textbooks) and oral history (what grandparents tell) is understood without being stated. Neither is considered more authoritative; they are different transmissions of the same tradition. An official account tells you what happened at the Vereth Corridor. A grandmother tells you what the family lost there, and which proverb they still repeat because of it. Both forms are necessary; neither replaces the other.

### Rest Days and Leave

Rest days for most civilians are genuinely unstructured. Common activities:
- Extended family gatherings (the meal is the gathering; there is usually no organized activity)
- District café visits — for chess, conversation, or simply presence
- Maintenance and repair work around the home using inherited tools; projects passed down across generations
- Walking in district public spaces; market browsing
- Reading — practical texts more than fiction; historical accounts; technical manuals with personal annotation

**Military leave** is structured around return rather than relaxation. A service member returning from frontier duty spends leave at home, in the district, present to family. Leave culture is not about travel or adventure; it is about reintegration. Long-service officers sometimes report that the hardest part of frontier duty is not the isolation but the awkwardness of relearning how to be home.

## 9. The Harvest Festival

### Origin and Meaning

The harvest festival is the Dominion's central annual civilian celebration. Its origin is agricultural — a seasonal marker tied to the first harvest of the dominant crop cycle on the Dominion's founding worlds — but its meaning has expanded over centuries into something broader: a celebration of continuity, of things having lasted.

The festival's emotional core is not abundance (though abundance is celebrated) but *preservation*. The harvest was gathered; the civilization endured another year; the grain came in as it always does. This framing — earned continuation rather than triumphant arrival — distinguishes it sharply from Fleet Authority's Founders' Week, which celebrates a founding moment and looks forward. The harvest festival looks sideways: at the season, at the community, at what has been maintained.

### Gold-Thread Banners

Gold-thread banners are the festival's most recognizable visual element. Hung from windows, strung across district streets, mounted on institutional buildings, they appear approximately three weeks before the festival's main observance and come down within a week after. The gold thread is not precious; it is a consistent material associated specifically with the festival. Newer banners are nearly identical to older ones — the design does not change by year or district.

Seeing gold-thread banners means harvest season has begun. In civilian consciousness, the banners register before the institutional calendar announces it. A child who has grown up in the Dominion knows that specific shade of gold before they can explain what it means.

### The District Festival

The district festival is the neighborhood-scale expression of harvest season. Each district organizes its own events separately from the institutional calendar, though the dates align. A typical district festival includes:

**Memorial reading**: An administrator or community elder reads the district's memorial list of service losses aloud at the central square. This is brief — the list is read, not commented on — but treated as the opening act of the day's festivities. Grief and celebration are not separated in Dominion culture; one precedes the other as a matter of appropriate sequence.

**School pageant**: Primary school students perform a historical account — usually a short dramatization of a sacrifice narrative drawn from local or regional history. Teachers select material specific to the district; a frontier district performs different material from a capital-city district. Families attend. It is one of the most broadly attended civic events of the year.

**Market extension**: District markets extend hours, add seasonal stalls (goldcrust bread, festival cider, harvest-season crafts), and transform the streets from transit space to social space.

**Evening community gathering**: Informal, after dark, in the public square or the largest available open space. Communal eating, district orchestra performance where available, old work songs. Not organized toward any specific ceremony — self-organizing around the community structure that already exists.

### Freight Windows and Transportation Pressure

The harvest festival creates significant logistical pressure on the Transportation Directorate. Freight windows — scheduled periods when civilian agricultural cargo has priority on shared transport corridors — are announced weeks in advance, triggering complex route adjustments across the Dominion's interstellar freight network.

Workers with Transportation Directorate expertise understand the harvest festival primarily as a logistics event: the annual surge in agricultural cargo that must move before winter months on agricultural worlds creates congestion that propagates through corridors used for other freight. Transit schedules are adjusted during harvest season; leave is temporarily restricted for Transportation Directorate staff; corridor geometry maps in planning offices show festival pressure in route congestion models.

This overlap between civilian celebration and logistical infrastructure is not hidden from ordinary civilians. The transit schedule adjustment is public. Most people understand, at some level, that the routes they use for personal travel are the same routes that carry the harvest.

### Pageant Structure: Recurring Detail

Pageants share a consistent structure across the Dominion:
- A narrator (usually an older student) provides historical context
- Younger students play civilians or service members in the sacrifice account
- The account ends at the moment of preservation, never the moment of loss — the pageant always concludes with something saved, not something destroyed

Teachers typically use the same story for three or four years, allowing it to become part of district identity, then rotate to a new story. Parents who attended the same school as children sometimes recognize the account their children perform.

### How It Differs from Founders' Week

| Element | Harvest Festival | Founders' Week |
|---------|-----------------|----------------|
| Orientation | Continuation, seasonal rhythm | Origin, founding celebration |
| Visual marker | Gold-thread banners | Copper and blue bunting |
| Central act | Memorial reading + community gathering | Blessing procession, parade |
| Emotional register | Gratitude and obligation | Optimism and shared pride |
| School tradition | Historical sacrifice pageant | Graduation cohort naming |
| Food | Goldcrust, harvest grain soup, festival cider | Fortune rolls, prosperity sharing |
| Transport impact | Freight window congestion | Coordinated Founders' Week leave |

Do not bleed holiday names across civilizations. Fleet characters call the border celebration "a local holiday" in transit traffic analysis, never "harvest festival." Dominion characters do not use Fleet holiday names.

## 10. Civilian Relationship to the Military

### How Civilians View Service

The average Dominion civilian views military service as necessary, costly, and honorable — roughly in that order. The necessity is not in question; the cost is acknowledged and managed; the honor is real but not romanticized. A family with three generations of frontier service does not describe military life as glorious. They describe it as what was asked, and what was provided.

This produces a civilian relationship to the military that is supportive without being martial. Dominion civilians are not aggressive about national military capacity; they are sober about it. When a factory worker in Korrast learns that a new wing of the shipyard will produce heavy-cruiser components, the response is not excitement or pride. It is a quiet note of recognition that the state is doing what it always does — preparing.

### Pride and Burden, Together

Service-family households carry both simultaneously. The pride is in having contributed to something larger than any individual career. The burden is in the missing chair, the slow letter, the child who grew up while the parent was posted to Dreva Station.

Civilians without service-family backgrounds relate to the military primarily through:
- Public funerals, which they attend as community members
- Harvest festival memorial readings, which name everyone
- The visible presence of garrison units in larger districts
- The occasional neighbor who returns from frontier duty changed in ways that are acknowledged and never directly discussed

### Veterans in Civilian Life

Veterans return to civilian life without formal ceremony. They re-enter the workforce, the district cafés, the schools where their children attend. Their service is known; it is not constantly referenced. The veteran at a café table playing chess is not performing his service for the other customers. He is simply there, and they know what he did, and that fact is part of the table's texture.

Veterans' halls handle the administrative and social functions of veteran civilian life. They also carry decades of institutional memory: attendance ledgers, annual memorial coordination records, chess tournament scores. A long-running veterans' hall is a kind of community archive, and its members know it.

### What Civilians Know and Accept

Most ordinary Dominion citizens know, without being formally told:
- The Dominion has sustained strategic concerns about expansion pressure in the direction of the frontier
- Military exercises and shipyard activity periodically increase, which means preparation is ongoing
- The harvest festival freight congestion overlaps with military transport windows in ways that suggest shared infrastructure

What they accept without specific knowledge:
- The exact nature of the strategic threat
- The contents of classified planning conferences
- The status of advanced military programs
- The Intelligence Directorate's current assessment of risk timelines

The gap between what is known and what is accepted is not felt as censorship. It is felt as appropriate division of labor. The Directorates handle what civilians cannot be asked to handle; civilians trust this because the institutions have, within the living memory of most families, continued to function. The tea is hot, the school is open, and the harvest festival arrives each year.

## 11. Economy & Labor

### Major Civilian Economic Sectors

**Industrial sector**: Dominion industry is concentrated in dedicated industrial worlds and orbital platforms. Factory work is the largest single category of civilian employment. Industrial workers in shipyard districts work alongside military construction timelines without formally being military employees — the distinction matters legally but dissolves in practice.

**Agricultural sector**: Agricultural worlds provide food supply for the broader Dominion. The harvest festival's cultural prominence reflects the historical importance of agricultural stability. Agricultural workers have the most locally-rooted identity in the Dominion — longer family tenures on the same land than anywhere else; slower to relocate than any other professional class.

**Transportation sector**: The Transportation Directorate manages interstellar freight, passenger transit, and the logistics infrastructure that underlies the entire economy. Transportation workers are highly mobile (between worlds), practically-oriented, and acutely aware of corridor geometry. They are not glamorous; they are understood to be essential.

**Scientific sector**: The Science Directorate supports a large community of researchers, analysts, and technical specialists. Scientific positions carry genuine prestige. Research communities are insular and intellectually intense — they argue internally about everything from resource allocation to methodology to the proper ratio of basic to applied research, and these arguments are as old as the institutes themselves.

**Administrative sector**: Civil service at every level, from district permit offices to Directorate policy staff. The backbone of institutional life; not glamorous; steady.

### The Directorates and Civilian Experience

**Industrial Directorate**: sets production schedules, manages safety regulation, coordinates with military procurement. The boundary between civilian industrial and military procurement is not always legible to workers on the floor.

**Science Directorate**: funds research, operates academies, recruits aggressively from preparatory track graduates. A Science Directorate grant is among the most sought-after professional recognitions in civilian life.

**Transportation Directorate**: manages freight windows, transit schedules, and interstellar corridor allocation. Its harvest festival logistics work is its most publicly visible annual function.

**Civil Government**: district administration, education, public health, memorial coordination, permit processing. The level of government most Dominion citizens interact with daily.

The **Strategic Security Directorate** is present in civilian life primarily as a name on certain permit restrictions, research classification decisions, and border-zone travel advisories. Most civilians understand it manages security without knowing precisely how.

### Preparedness Doctrine Embedded in Economic Life

Most Dominion economic activity reflects preparedness doctrine without civilians thinking of it in those terms. Factories run at higher than current-demand capacity because slack capacity is considered institutional vulnerability. Freight lanes are maintained at higher than current-use capacity because surge capacity is necessary for mobilization. The Science Directorate funds applied research at a higher rate than pure research because applicable results support preparation.

None of these decisions are announced as preparedness doctrine. They are announced as good management. The result is the same.

## 12. Regional Variation

### Unifying Elements (Consistent Across All Member Worlds)

- The harvest festival (dates synchronized across member worlds; local expression varies; memorial reading and gold-thread banners do not)
- Primary school core curriculum (sacrifice narrative; regional application varies)
- Tea as default institutional and civilian drink
- Chess as professional pastime
- The canonical proverbs (the same sayings from different mouths; a grandmother on an agricultural world and a transit worker on a core administrative world may use the same phrase without any connection)
- Public funerals as community events
- Private promotions

### Core Administrative Worlds

Life here is more institutional, more aware of Directorate decisions, more mixed across professions. Citizens are more likely to know what the Industrial Directorate announced last month. The architecture is the oldest — layered over centuries; an administrative building may have three distinct architectural periods visible in its exterior without anyone considering this unusual.

### Industrial Worlds (e.g., Korrast)

Factory culture dominates. Shift identity is strong; district identity is working-class in character. The science-and-academy prestige hierarchy is present but felt as distant from daily life. Local culture emphasizes competence and reliability over formal credentials. The harvest festival evening gathering runs later and louder than on administrative worlds; district orchestras are more prominent; community presence in public squares is denser.

### Agricultural Worlds (e.g., Ostavar region)

The most locally-rooted Dominion civilian culture. Families may have farmed the same land for four or five generations; a grandfather who served is also a grandfather whose grave is in the family's field. The harvest festival is most practically significant here — it marks actual agricultural labor completed, not only a symbolic season. Gold-thread banners appear earliest on agricultural worlds. School pageants draw from intensely local history.

### Scientific Worlds and Institute Stations

Small, dense communities; high professional prestige; high internal debate. Harvest festival is observed but occupies a smaller share of the community calendar than on agricultural or industrial worlds — research schedules do not pause easily. Scientists are among the most intellectually skeptical about institutional rhetoric, including the sacrifice narrative in its more ceremonial forms, while often being among the most personally committed to their work as a form of service. The contradiction is not resolved; it is lived.

### Frontier Worlds and Border Stations

See Section 13.

## 13. Frontier Civilians

### The Texture of Frontier Life

Civilians at border stations and frontier worlds live in the most visible evidence of Dominion preparedness doctrine. The installations exist because the doctrine says the border must be maintained; the civilian staff — logistics workers, administrative personnel, technical specialists, family members of garrison personnel — live in communities built specifically to support that function.

This produces a civilian culture that is simultaneously ordinary (the café exists, the market exists, children attend school, the harvest festival happens) and quietly aware of its strategic position. A child growing up in Dreva Station knows, from an early age, that their community is here specifically because the border is here. This is not experienced as threat. It is experienced as fact.

### Border-World Children

Children who grow up on frontier stations have a specific relationship to Dominion history. The sacrifice narrative is not abstract to them; it is the reason their parents moved here, the reason the garrison unit is stationed next door, the reason the transit schedule includes military freight windows. They encounter *strategic depth* as a lived concept — not a phrase from a civic textbook but the name for why they live where they live.

These children are often better prepared for military academy history courses than their core-world peers. Their teachers know the subject from proximity rather than from the standard curriculum.

### The Sleepy Assignment Culture

Military personnel assigned to border stations frequently describe the postings as "sleepy" — meaning routine, low-incident, procedurally heavy. Civilian life on these stations reflects this: rhythms are regular, incidents are rare, conversations in the café after shift run to logistics schedules, chess, family news, and complaints about the transit calendar.

This apparent quietness exists alongside quiet strategic weight. The station's existence is the point; the routine is the function. Veterans who returned to core-world districts and then took civilian jobs at border stations often describe the return as "feeling right" in a way they find difficult to explain to people who grew up elsewhere.

### Civilian-Military Social Mix

On frontier stations, the civilian-military divide is at its narrowest. Garrison personnel and civilian staff share cafés, schools, and the harvest festival. The district administrator and the garrison commander know each other by name. The children of civilian logistics workers and garrison officers attend the same primary school. The public funeral culture is most visible here: the memorial board is the most-consulted public surface in the community, and everyone in the station knows everyone named on it.

## 14. Civilians and Information

### What Ordinary Citizens Know

The average Dominion civilian has a functional understanding of the strategic situation that is simultaneously accurate in broad outline and incomplete in specific detail. They know:
- The Dominion maintains extensive border infrastructure because the border has historically required maintenance
- There is some form of expansion activity beyond the frontier — not the specific identity of the civilization involved, but the general pressure
- Military exercises and construction activity fluctuate, and the fluctuations are not random

What they do not know:
- The name or specific nature of the civilization Fleet Authority represents
- The status of advanced military programs
- The contents of strategic planning conferences
- The Intelligence Directorate's current assessment of risk timelines

### Information as Preparation, Not Alarm

The Dominion government manages civilian information in a way that emphasizes preparation over alarm. When freight windows are adjusted for military transport, the announcement is logistical. When shipyard expansion is authorized, it is framed as economic development. When research funding is accelerated, it is framed as scientific investment.

This is not propaganda in the crude sense. It is institutional framing that reflects the same preparedness doctrine embedded throughout the culture: good management requires preparation; preparation is ongoing; there is no reason for the population to be alarmed by what is simply the civilization doing its job. Civilians mostly accept this framing because the institutions have, within their experience, continued to function.

### Education's Role

The education system shapes civilian understanding of the strategic environment in ways that are visible in retrospect but not experienced as indoctrination during childhood. By the time a Dominion adult reaches their twenties, they understand:
- That buffer space is strategically necessary
- That preparation is institutional work, not individual anxiety
- That sacrifices of previous generations created the conditions of current stability
- That when the state asks for more, there is usually a reason, even if it is not immediately shared

These convictions are not examined more than any other received cultural inheritance. They are the water the fish swims in.

## 15. Cultural Memory in Civilian Life

### Proverbs Across Contexts

Dominion proverbs are the most visible form of civilian cultural memory. The canonical sayings appear from the widest possible range of speakers — not primarily from officers, but from grandmothers at kitchen tables, market vendors who use preparation proverbs as business philosophy, teachers who frame history lessons around the same words, engineers who cite depth doctrine when explaining why they over-specify materials.

> *"Preparation honors those who come after us."*

This appears in civic textbooks, grandmother-to-grandchild conversations, market vendor wisdom about stocking for slow seasons, academy instructor rhetoric, and veterans' hall discussions. Its meaning shifts slightly with each speaker; the accumulation is consistent. What you do now has consequences for people who have not arrived yet.

> *"Conquest is what other people call it when sacrifice succeeds."*

This circulates primarily among adults rather than children — it requires some acquaintance with how the Dominion is perceived from outside. It appears from veterans, from administrators handling foreign-facing matters, from senior history teachers. It is not a boast; it is a reframing.

> *"The keel goes down while the weather is still kind."*

Associated specifically with yard communities and frontier preparation contexts. Grandmother Ilya in the Korrast yards is its documented community carrier (Book One canon); it appears from other mouths in later books. Its application extends well beyond shipbuilding: any early preparation before the need is fully visible. Market vendors use it about stocking before the harvest rush; engineers use it about infrastructure margins.

### Memorial Culture

Every Dominion district has a memorial surface. In cities, this is typically a carved stone structure in the public square — old enough that the earliest names are in an archaic script that younger residents cannot read without reference. In smaller communities, a wall plaque in the district building. On frontier stations, the memorial board is the most regularly updated surface in the community.

New names are added after public funerals. The ceremony of addition is brief and institutional: an administrator records the name, a civil government official certifies the inscription, and the name appears. There is no specific ceremony for the addition itself. The public funeral was the ceremony. The inscription is the permanent record.

Schoolchildren are brought to the district memorial annually, usually in connection with the Review Assembly. They are not expected to speak or perform. Teachers often say nothing during this portion of the visit. Silence is the appropriate register.

### How Civilian Memory Differs from Military Memory

Military memory organizes around campaigns, commands, and strategic outcomes. Civilian memory organizes around names, families, and what was preserved. A military history of the Vereth Corridor campaigns describes logistics failures and tactical reversals leading to eventual border stabilization. A grandmother in that district describes which family members served, which came home, and the proverb they still repeat because of it.

Both memories reinforce the same values — sacrifice, preparation, continuity — through different organizational frameworks. The military history explains why the sacrifice was necessary. The civilian memory makes the sacrifice human. Neither form is more authoritative; they are complementary transmissions.

## 16. Civilian Tensions & Disagreements

### What Ordinary People Argue About

The Dominion is unified. It is not unanimous. Genuine disagreements exist that do not threaten national loyalty but are real, persistent, and recognized.

**Economic complaints**:
- Factory workers in industrial districts periodically organize against unsafe working conditions and excessive shift lengths, through district-level channels rather than cross-Directorate action
- Agricultural world residents resent freight scheduling that prioritizes industrial cargo during non-festival periods; they understand their harvests as more than logistics events
- Small market vendors in districts adjacent to large Directorate facilities complain about institutional procurement crowding out civilian commerce

**Regional grievances**:
- Frontier worlds consistently feel under-resourced relative to their strategic importance; civilians there know they live in the most important geography and receive the least institutional attention
- Agricultural worlds resent being treated as supply infrastructure rather than communities
- Core administrative worlds are quietly resented by frontier and agricultural civilians for what the latter perceive as comfortable distance from real sacrifice

**Scientists versus the military**:
The most consistent internal intellectual tension in Dominion professional life. Science Directorate personnel — particularly research scientists and academics — frequently argue that military doctrine overvalues tactical preparation at the expense of scientific research that would produce more durable long-term security. This argument is conducted through resource allocation battles rather than public debate, and it is old enough to have developed its own institutional archaeology of prior disputes.

**The sacrifice rhetoric skeptics**:
In every generation, some workers and lower-professional-class civilians push back against the sacrifice framing. Their argument is not ideological; it is personal: *"We are always the ones sacrificing. The Directorates are not sacrificing."* This resentment is real, understood, periodically expressed, and does not translate into disloyalty because the Dominion's institutional framework has historically been good enough to absorb the criticism without it becoming systemic. Civilians who make this argument still attend the public funeral. They still tell the proverbs. The disagreement exists within the framework; it does not question the framework itself.

## 17. Children's Culture

### What Children Learn

Dominion children learn, before they learn to read, that their community has a history. This is not taught as a formal lesson; it is absorbed through adult conversation, family storytelling, and the presence of memorial surfaces in every public space they inhabit.

By the end of primary school, a Dominion child has:
- Memorized at least two canonical proverbs (usually the ones their teacher or grandparent uses most)
- Participated in at least one Review Assembly recitation
- Visited the district memorial at least twice
- Performed in at least one harvest festival school pageant
- Heard the family's service history from a grandparent or parent, at least in outline
- Been told, in approximately these words: *"The Dominion is here because people before you made sure it would be."*

### Games and Stories

Children's play in the Dominion tends toward strategy and coordination rather than individual performance. Block-building, simple route-mapping games (logistics puzzles presented as play), and team coordination games are culturally encouraged. Individual athletics exist but are not the primary form of play; Dominion play culture emphasizes collective problem-solving.

Chess is introduced formally in preparatory school, but children learn the basic movements informally much earlier — usually from a grandparent, using the café set or a household set made from worn pieces.

Children's storytelling is heavily historical. The same accounts that teachers tell in Review Assembly are told by grandparents in the evening with different details — more personal, more local, more emotional. The child eventually encounters the same event in the district memorial. The accumulation across contexts is how history becomes genuinely known rather than merely learned.

### The Role of Grandparents

Grandparents are the primary civilian transmitters of cultural memory. In multi-generational households, grandparents tell the family service history, repeat the proverbs, explain the district memorial names (the ones they knew personally), and maintain the connection to previous generations that parents — busier with work and children — often cannot.

A Dominion child who grows up without grandparents nearby is understood to have missed something specific: not just affection, but the historical voice. Extended family, older neighbors, or trusted teachers can partially compensate; it is never fully replaced.

### Passage from Childhood to Civic Life

**Passage Day** (end of primary school): The first public civic choice. The student selects a preparatory track before their family. Teachers speak briefly about what the community expects from young people — rarely in military terms, almost always in terms of continuation: *"You are inheriting something. You will pass it forward."*

**Civic Attestation** (end of preparatory school, age 18): The formal passage into adult civic identity. A brief ceremony in which graduates pledge to contribute to the Dominion's continuation in the form of their profession. Not a military oath; a general pledge of civic participation. Military academy graduates take a separate military oath alongside it.
